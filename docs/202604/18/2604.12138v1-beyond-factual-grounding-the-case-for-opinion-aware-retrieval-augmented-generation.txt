## Abstract 

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) systems have transformed how Large Language Models access external knowledge, but we find that current implementations exhibit a bias toward factual, ob-jective content, as evidenced by existing benchmarks and datasets that prioritize objective retrieval. This factual bias—treating opin-ions and diverse perspectives as noise rather than information to be synthesized—limits RAG systems in real-world scenarios involv-ing subjective content, from social media discussions to product reviews and policy debates. Beyond technical limitations, this bias poses risks to transparent and accountable AI: echo chamber effects that amplify dominant viewpoints, systematic underrepresentation of minority voices, and potential opinion manipulation through biased information synthesis. We formalize this limitation through the lens of uncertainty: factual queries involve epistemic uncer-tainty reducible through evidence, while opinion queries involve aleatoric uncertainty reflecting genuine heterogeneity in human perspectives. This distinction implies that factual RAG should mini-mize posterior entropy, whereas opinion-aware RAG must preserve it. Building on this theoretical foundation, we present an Opinion-Aware RAG architecture featuring LLM-based opinion extraction, entity-linked opinion graphs, and opinion-enriched document in-dexing. We evaluate our approach on e-commerce seller forum data, comparing an Opinion-Enriched knowledge base against a traditional baseline. Experiments demonstrate substantial improve-ments in retrieval diversity: +26.8% sentiment diversity, +42.7% entity match rate, and +31.6% author demographic coverage on entity-matched documents. Our results provide empirical evidence that treating subjectivity as a first-class citizen yields measurably more representative retrieval—a first step toward opinion-aware RAG systems. Future work includes custom retrievers optimized for our formalized opinion-aware loss function and end-to-end learn-ing approaches that jointly optimize retrieval and generation for distributional fidelity.

## 1 Introduction 

Retrieval-Augmented Generation has become an important method for making Large Language Models more reliable and factually grounded [ 1 , 2]. By conditioning generation on external evidence re-trieved at inference time, RAG systems have shown success in reduc-ing hallucinations and improving performance across knowledge-intensive tasks [ 3 , 4 ]. Recent evaluations have confirmed RAG’s effectiveness in enabling accurate, up-to-date, and verifiable text generation across diverse applications [ 5]. This approach has en-abled LLMs to access current information, provide transparent sourcing, and ground their outputs in verifiable evidence. However, beneath this success lies a fundamental assumption that has largely gone unexamined: that knowledge bases should contain factually correct, objective information. This assumption manifests across multiple levels of the RAG ecosystem—from the datasets used for evaluation, to the retrieval mechanisms optimized for factual recall, to the generation objectives that prioritize con-sistency with ground truth answers. While this factual orientation has driven advances in question answering and knowledge synthe-sis, it leaves the handling of inherently subjective content—which pervades real-world information landscapes—as an unexamined, non-systematic capability rather than an explicit design goal. Consider the disparity between how current systems handle these two queries: “What is the corporate tax rate in the United States?” versus “What do small business owners think about the cur-rent tax policy?” The first query has a definitive, factual answer that existing RAG systems excel at retrieving and presenting. The second query requires synthesizing diverse, potentially conflicting opinions from multiple sources. While some large language models demonstrate emergent opinion synthesis capabilities—potentially drawing from patterns learned during training (see Appendix C for an example from contemporary LLMs)—systematic RAG ar-chitectures designed explicitly for transparent, reproducible, and evaluable opinion synthesis remain largely underexplored. The difference between accidental capability and intentional design be-comes important when deploying systems in applications where understanding the methodology, sources, and potential biases of opinion synthesis is essential. This limitation becomes particularly pronounced in domains where understanding human opinion is crucial. Despite growing en-terprise adoption across customer service, knowledge management, and domain-specific applications [ 6 , 7 , 8], RAG systems remain op-timized primarily for factual accuracy, creating a systematic gap in their ability to handle the subjective, opinion-rich content prevalent in market research, policy analysis, social listening, customer feed-back synthesis, and personalized content generation. The timing is critical: as RAG deployments expand beyond traditional knowledge bases into social media, customer forums, and user-generated con-tent [ 9 , 10 ], the mismatch between factual-oriented architectures and opinion-rich data sources risks creating echo chambers that ex-clude minority perspectives and present artificial consensus where genuine disagreement exists. In these contexts, the goal is not to

Addressing this gap requires rethinking how systems handle different information types. Current RAG systems lack these mechanisms, and approaches to “robustness” primarily focus on filtering contradic-tory information [ 13 , 14 ], treating opinions as defects rather than valuable information to be synthesized. This paper introduces Opinion-Aware RAG , a framework that explicitly models and leverages subjectivity throughout the retrieval-generation pipeline. Rather than seeking consensus or filtering disagreement, opinion-aware systems embrace the complexity of human discourse, providing users with nuanced understanding of how different groups, individuals, or contexts shape perspectives on topics of interest. Our contributions are: (1) A novel theoretical framework for-malizing opinion-aware retrieval through the lens of epistemic vs. aleatoric uncertainty (2) An Opinion-Aware RAG architecture— the first systematic approach to making retrieval systems opinion-aware—featuring LLM-based opinion extraction and entity-linked opinion graphs; (3) Empirical evaluation on e-commerce forum data demonstrating +26.8% sentiment diversity and +42.7% entity match rate, showing that opinion enrichment yields measurably more representative retrieval than semantic similarity alone; and (4) An evaluation framework measuring opinion coverage, per-spective diversity, and representation fairness. While this paper presents a complete system—from theoretical framework to em-pirical validation—we view our most important contribution as drawing systematic attention to opinion-aware RAG as a research priority. 

## 2 Motivation 2.1 Factual Bias Across the RAG Ecosystem 

Surveys of RAG systems [ 2, 5] document a field-wide consensus around factual accuracy as the primary evaluation criterion. Of 35 major RAG benchmarks surveyed (Appendix A), only 1 (2.9%) addresses opinion synthesis. As Table 1 demonstrates, the entire RAG ecosystem has evolved around assumptions of objective truth: benchmarks like Natural Questions [ 15 ], MS MARCO [ 16 ], and HotpotQA [ 17 ] all assume questions have single correct answers; evaluation frameworks treat contradictory information as noise rather than valuable diversity; and retrieval architectures optimize for semantic convergence rather than perspective coverage. Only OpinioRAG [ 18 ] addresses opinion synthesis. This factual orienta-tion becomes problematic when users seek to understand public sentiment, explore arguments on controversial topics, or gauge community reactions, using contemporary RAG systems.

However, when responses sound balanced, it remains unclear whether this reflects genuine diversity in retrieved con-tent or the model’s learned tendency—often attributed to RLHF—to present information evenhandedly regardless of input [ 19 ]. Without transparency into retrieval, users cannot distinguish substantive balance from stylistic hedging. This raises fundamental questions: 

Retrieval Opacity. How do retrieval systems translate opin-ion queries into search strategies that surface diverse perspectives rather than just semantically similar content? Current embedding models, trained primarily on factual content, may embed opposing opinions similarly if they discuss the same topics—making it diffi-cult to distinguish “Tax cuts help small businesses” from “Tax cuts primarily benefit large corporations.” Retrieval systems provide no transparency into which perspectives were considered versus systematically excluded. 

Synthesis Opacity. Even when diverse opinions are retrieved, users cannot understand how conflicting perspectives were weighted or what constitutes “balanced representation.” Unlike factual re-trieval where citation suffices, opinion synthesis requires under-standing how conflicting viewpoints are reconciled. The limitations of current evaluation approaches become ap-parent when examining how traditional RAG metrics can assign high scores to obviously biased outputs. Recent work on bias am-plification [ 20 , 21 , 22 ] demonstrates that RAG systems can inherit and amplify biases present in their retrieval corpora—for instance, leadership discussions that overrepresent masculine-coded traits like “assertiveness” and “aggressive persistence” while underrepre-senting collaborative qualities. If a knowledge base contains such skewed content, a query like “What leadership qualities are most important for startup CEOs?” would retrieve and synthesize this bias into the response (see Appendix C.2). Such a response could score excellently on traditional metrics—high ROUGE-L from lexi-cal overlap with retrieved sources, strong context relevance from semantic alignment, high answer faithfulness from accurately re-flecting the retrieval set—yet systematically underrepresent certain perspectives. ## 3 Related Works 

Recent RAG research frames subjectivity as a problem to be solved rather than information to be leveraged. RAGuard [ 23 ] treats Reddit discussions as “naturally occurring misinformation” to be filtered; CONFLICTS [ 24 ] focuses on conflict resolution rather than preserv-ing perspective diversity; and robustness approaches [ 25 , 26 , 27 ]emphasize filtering and source verification. While valuable for fac-tual domains, these approaches are misaligned with scenarios where understanding diverse perspectives is the goal. Work on argument-based retrieval has begun exploring how systems can handle argumentative content. RAFTS [ 28 ] synthesizes contrastive arguments for fact verification, explicitly retrieving both supporting and refuting evidence, though it focuses on claim cred-ibility rather than synthesizing diverse perspectives. Other work explores argument mining [ 29 ] and perspective-aware retrieval, but typically addresses isolated components. A deeper challenge emerges from findings that LLMs struggle with conflicting infor-mation [ 30 , 31 ]—even state-of-the-art models perform only slightly better than random guessing at contradiction detection, exhibiting high precision but low recall [ 31 ]. This limitation makes opinion-aware RAG particularly challenging, since opinions are inherently contradictory. Opinion mining and stance detection research has matured sig-nificantly [ 32 , 33 , 34 ], but these capabilities remain largely isolated from RAG frameworks [ 35 , 36 , 37 ]. OpinioRAG [ 18 ] bridges this gap with RAG-based opinion summarization from product reviews. While OpinioRAG focuses on summarization —condensing retrieved opinions into coherent narratives—our work addresses the com-plementary challenge of retrieval diversity : ensuring the retrieved document set faithfully represents the full spectrum of opinions before summarization. Sophisticated approaches have been developed for opinion-rich content on social networking websites, including stance detec-tion [ 38 , 39 ], multimodal opinion analysis [ 40 ], and comment senti-ment analysis [ 41 , 42 , 43 ]. CrediRAG [ 44 ] demonstrates how social network structures can enhance misinformation detection. How-ever, these contributions remain disconnected from broader RAG research, and the wealth of naturally occurring opinion data re-mains underutilized for training opinion-aware systems. Simply combining these fragmented approaches is insufficient: factual RAG optimizes for convergence toward ground truth, while opinion-aware systems must preserve divergence, and no unified framework exists for jointly optimizing retrieval diversity, synthesis fidelity, and demographic fairness. This gap motivates our formal problem formulation.

## 5 Methodology 

We propose a general framework for opinion-aware RAG that intro-duces an opinion enrichment step before indexing. This section presents the framework abstractly, then instantiates it for our ex-perimental domain. Figure 1 illustrates the architecture. 

## 5.1 General Framework 

The key insight is that traditional RAG optimizes for semantic similarity, which tends to retrieve documents expressing similar viewpoints. By enriching documents with structured opinion meta-data before indexing, we enable retrieval that accounts for opinion diversity—operationalizing the coverage objective from Section 4. The framework consists of three domain-agnostic components: 

Entity Registry. A hierarchical taxonomy of opinion targets organized by granularity. Entities represent the “what” of opinions— topics, policies, products, or concepts that people hold views about. A tiered structure (general → specific → granular) enables flexible aggregation and analysis. 

Opinion Attributes. Structured metadata capturing the nature of expressed opinions. Core attributes include: 

• Sentiment & Intensity : Polarity and strength of the opinion 

• Stance : Position on a spectrum (e.g., strongly against to strongly for) 

• Impact Assessment : Perceived severity or importance 

• Evidence Type : Basis for the opinion (personal experience, anecdotal, data-driven) 

Author Attributes. Contextual factors about opinion holders that enable fairness analysis. These capture demographic or ex-periential characteristics relevant to the domain, supporting the fairness term max 𝑔 ∈𝐺 Δ𝑔 in our objective (Section 4). 

Per-Entity Document Splitting. Each source document is split into multiple enriched documents—one per mentioned entity. This ensures that when a document discusses multiple topics with dif-ferent sentiments, each opinion is indexed separately with its cor-responding metadata. The enriched documents embed opinion and author attributes alongside content, making this information avail-able during retrieval and generation. This architecture directly operationalizes the theoretical frame-work: per-entity splitting with opinion metadata enables retrieval that minimizes distributional divergence W2 (𝑃 pop , ˆ𝑃 R ), while struc-tured attributes support analysis of demographic representation. 

## 5.2 Case Study: E-commerce Seller Forums 

We instantiate this framework for e-commerce seller discussion forums, where merchants discuss policies, fees, and operational challenges. This domain is well-suited for studying opinion-aware RAG because: (1) sellers hold genuinely diverse views on policies, (2) author characteristics (business size, tenure) meaningfully influence perspectives, and (3) the content is naturally opinionated with real business implications. 

Entity Registry. We construct a 98-entity registry organized across three tiers: Tier 1 covers broad categories (e.g., “Shipping”, “Fees”), Tier 2 captures intermediate topics (e.g., “Fulfillment Fees”), and Tier 3 addresses specific aspects (e.g., “Storage Fee Changes”). The registry is constructed through domain expert curation and LLM-assisted extraction, seeding Tier 1 from service taxonomy and using clustering to identify finer-grained entities.

## 6.2 Evaluation Framework 

6.2.1 Dataset. We use seller discussion data similar to publicly accessible e-commerce seller forums such as Amazon Seller Fo-rums, eBay Community, and Etsy Community. Our dataset spans six months of discussions, comprising approximately 8,000 unique sellers and averaging 500 discussions per week. These forums con-tain rich opinionated content where sellers discuss policies, fees, operational challenges, and business strategies—making them ideal for studying opinion diversity in RAG systems. 

6.2.2 Knowledge Base Configurations. We construct two knowl-edge base configurations using identical source content: 

Raw Discussions KB (Baseline): Documents are indexed with their original content only, without any opinion metadata. This represents a traditional RAG setup where retrieval is based purely on semantic similarity. 

Opinion-Enriched KB (Ours): Documents are enriched with structured opinion and author attributes as described in Section 5, then indexed with embedded metadata. Each document is split per-entity, with opinion attributes included in the indexed content. Both configurations use Amazon OpenSearch Serverless with Ti-tan Text Embeddings v2 (1024 dimensions) and fixed-size chunking (4000 tokens, 15% overlap). We compare against Raw KB rather than diversity-aware retrieval (e.g., MMR) because our contribution is at indexing, not retrieval—and MMR optimizes for semantic diversity, not opinion diversity.

## 6.3 Metrics 

The theoretical objectives (Equation 10) involve distributional quan-tities that are difficult to compute directly. We design practical proxy metrics to operationalize each hypothesis: 

Coverage Metrics (H1). We approximate the coverage term 

W2 (𝑃 pop , ˆ𝑃 R ) using variance-based measures that capture how well retrieval spans the opinion space: 

• Sentiment-Intensity 𝜎 : Combines sentiment polarity and intensity into a single score (e.g., Positive+High = +30, Neg-ative+High = -30, Neutral = 0). Higher 𝜎 indicates broader coverage of the sentiment-intensity space. 

• Business Impact 𝜎 : Maps impact levels to numeric values (High = 30, Medium = 20, Low = 10). Higher 𝜎 indicates retrieval of both high-impact and low-impact perspectives. 

• Entity Match Rate : Fraction of retrieved documents that mention the queried entity, measuring retrieval precision for opinion-relevant content. 

Fidelity Metric (H2). The fidelity term 𝐷 KL (𝑃 pop ∥𝑃 reader ) mea-sures whether readers perceive the full opinion spectrum—a quan-tity we cannot compute directly. We approximate this through human evaluation: five annotators compared response pairs (KB identity hidden) on sentiment diversity, providing binary prefer-ences with ties allowed. 

Fairness Metrics (H3). We approximate the fairness term using categorical coverage ( max 𝑔 ∈𝐺 Δ𝑔 ): 

• GMS Band Coverage : Fraction of business size categories represented 

• Tenure Coverage : Fraction of experience levels represented 

• Evidence Type Coverage : Fraction of evidence types (per-sonal, anecdotal, data-driven) represented

## 7.1 H1: Coverage Improvement 

Finding: Supported. Opinion enrichment significantly improves retrieval coverage of the opinion distribution. Table 2 shows that at 

𝑘 = 20 , Opinion-Enriched KB achieves +26.8%, +14.5%, and +42.7% improvements in sentiment-intensity 𝜎 , business impact 𝜎 , and entity match rate respectively, all at p <0.05. These improvements indicate that embedding opinion metadata enables retrieval that better spans the sentiment-intensity space, operationalizing the coverage term W2 (𝑃 pop , ˆ𝑃 R ) from our theo-retical framework. The entity match rate improvement suggests that per-entity splitting provides better semantic anchoring for opinion-relevant content. 

## 7.2 H2: Fidelity to Readers 

Finding: Supported. Human annotators confirmed that opinion-enriched retrieval produces responses that better communicate opinion diversity. Of 48 question-response pairs, annotators pre-ferred Opinion-Enriched KB responses in 38 cases (79.2% win rate, 95% CI: [65.0%, 89.5%], p <0.001). ## 9 Challenges and Call to Action 

Technical Challenges. Current RAG benchmarks lack datasets with naturally occurring subjective content and rich annotations for stance, sentiment, and perspective diversity. Opinion-aware architectures must balance faithfulness to diverse sources with coherent generation, avoiding bias toward dominant opinions while maintaining efficiency. 

Ethical Considerations. Without opinion-aware capabilities, systems risk algorithmic opinion manipulation through training biases, echo chamber amplification by retrieving only confirm-ing information, and minority voice erasure by underrepresenting marginalized viewpoints. The goal is not eliminating bias but mak-ing it transparent and auditable. 

Community Call to Action. We call on the research commu-nity to pursue: (1) benchmark development with subjective annota-tions and opinion synthesis metrics; (2) foundation model research on opinion-aware tasks; (3) retrieval algorithms for diverse per-spective identification; and (4) evaluation protocols for subjective content synthesis. 

## 10 Future Work 

Our experiments validate key aspects of the theoretical frame-work in Section 4: opinion enrichment improves retrieval coverage (W2 reduction through increased sentiment diversity) and entity-relevant retrieval (better alignment with 𝑃 pop ). However, fully re-alizing opinion-aware RAG requires addressing several open chal-lenges. 

Representation Learning for Subjective Content. Our opin-ion extraction relies on LLM-based annotation, but the theoretical framework’s opinion embedding function 𝜙 : D → R𝑚 suggests the need for learned representations that jointly encode seman-tic content with subjective attributes. Future work should explore multi-task learning objectives and hierarchical embedding spaces where documents can be similar at the topic level while differing at the stance level. 

Optimizing the Coverage Term. While our diversity metrics approximate the coverage objective W2 (𝑃 pop , ˆ𝑃 R ), we do not di-rectly optimize this during retrieval. Query expansion techniques generating stance-diverse queries, and re-ranking mechanisms us-ing submodular optimization, could more directly minimize distri-butional divergence. 

Fairness Constraints. The framework’s fairness term max 𝑔 ∈𝐺 Δ𝑔 

addresses demographic equity, but our ablations reveal trade-offs be-tween opinion diversity and author demographic coverage. Future work must develop retrieval strategies that jointly optimize for both, ensuring no subpopulation is systematically under-represented. 

Temporal Dynamics. The framework assumes a static 𝑃 pop , but opinions evolve continuously. Extending the theoretical foundation to handle temporal opinion dynamics—distinguishing temporary reactive opinions from lasting attitudinal changes—remains an im-portant direction. 

## 11 Conclusion 

We presented Opinion-Aware RAG, an architecture that treats sub-jectivity as a first-class citizen rather than noise to be filtered. Our novel theoretical framework distinguishes factual queries (mini-mize posterior entropy) from opinion queries (preserve entropy reflecting genuine perspective heterogeneity), formalizing opinion-aware retrieval through coverage, fidelity, and fairness objectives. Experiments on e-commerce seller forum data demonstrate that opinion enrichment yields measurably more representative retrieval: +26.8% sentiment diversity, +42.7% entity match rate, and +4.7% evidence type coverage. Human evaluation confirms these gains translate to perceptible quality improvements, with annotators pre-ferring Opinion-Enriched responses 79.2% of the time (p < 0.001). Ablations reveal that simply retrieving more documents does not achieve comparable diversity—opinion metadata is essential for surfacing varied perspectives. Opinion-Aware RAG 

Our work represents a first step. The trade-off between opinion and author demographic diversity motivates future research on multi-objective retrieval. Fully realizing the theoretical framework requires learned opinion embeddings, direct optimization of distri-butional coverage, and evaluation metrics for generation fidelity. As AI systems increasingly mediate access to information, their ability to fairly represent diverse perspectives becomes essential for transparent and accountable AI.

They support targeted deductions and pass-through relief, espe-cially proposals allowing deduction of the first $25,000 of qualifying business income. ## C.3 Opinion Extraction Prompt 

We use Claude Sonnet 4.5 with a structured output schema for opinion extraction. The prompt template is shown below (with service-specific terms anonymized):      

> You are an expert at analyzing seller opinions from e-commerce service content. Analyze the following post and extract structured opinion information for EACH entity (business topic/issue) mentioned. POST CONTENT: {content} SERVICE: {service_type} CATEGORY: {service_category} {entity_registry} Extract the following information and respond ONLY with valid JSON: {"entities": [{ "entity_name": "entity name", "confidence": "Low|Medium|High", "sentiment": "positive|negative|neutral|mixed", "sentiment_intensity": "Low|Medium|High", "emotional_markers": ["marker1", "marker2"], "evidence": "supporting evidence", "business_impact": "Low|Medium|High", "context_snippet": "relevant quote", "evidence_type": "personal|anecdotal|data" }]}

Key extraction guidelines include: 

• Entity matching : If the entity matches one from the hier-archical registry, use that exact name; prefer granular (Tier 3) over general (Tier 1) entities 

• Per-entity extraction : Each entity receives its own com-plete opinion structure; a single post may yield multiple entities with different sentiments 

• Evidence typing : Classify as personal (first-hand experi-ence), anecdotal (heard from others), or data (cites specific statistics) 

• Emotional markers : Common markers include Frustra-tion, Anger, Resignation, Adaptation, Hope, Satisfaction, Confusion, Anxiety 

• Business impact : Consider financial mentions, operational disruption, and account risks when assigning impact levels The model outputs a JSON array with one object per entity-opinion pair, enabling downstream per-entity document splitting.
