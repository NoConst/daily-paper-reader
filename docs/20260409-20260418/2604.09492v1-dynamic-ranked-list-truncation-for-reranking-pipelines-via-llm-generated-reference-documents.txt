## Abstract 

Large Language Models (LLM) have been widely used in rerank-ing. Computational overhead and large context lengths remain a challenging issue for LLM rerankers. Efficient reranking usually involves selecting a subset of the ranked list from the first stage, known as ranked list truncation (RLT). The truncated list is pro-cessed further by a reranker. For LLM rerankers, the ranked list is often partitioned and processed sequentially in batches to re-duce the context length. Both these steps involve hyperparameters and topic-agnostic heuristics. Recently, LLMs have been shown to be effective for relevance judgment. Equivalently, we propose that LLMs can be used to generate reference documents that can act as a pivot between relevant and non-relevant documents in a ranked list. We propose methods to use these generated reference documents for RLT as well as for efficient listwise reranking. While reranking, we process the ranked list in either parallel batches of non-overlapping windows or overlapping windows with adap-tive strides, improving the existing fixed stride setup. The gener-ated reference documents are also shown to improve existing effi-cient listwise reranking frameworks. Experiments on TREC Deep Learning benchmarks show that our approach outperforms existing RLT-based approaches. In-domain and out-of-domain benchmarks demonstrate that our proposed methods accelerate LLM-based list-wise reranking by up to 66% compared to existing approaches. This work not only establishes a practical paradigm for efficient LLM-based reranking but also provides insight into the capabil-ity of LLMs to generate semantically controlled documents using relevance signals.

## 1 Introduction 

Retrieval and reranking pipelines are increasingly being adopted in search systems. Managing the efficiency-effectiveness trade-off for multi-stage retrieval pipelines poses a significant challenge due to the use of effective but computationally expensive reranking models [ 12 , 15 , 40 ]. Reranking strategies vary significantly across models and can be broadly categorized into pointwise, pairwise, and listwise setups [22, 26, 31–33]. Typically, in a pointwise or pairwise setup, the first-stage retrieve candidate list is pruned before reranking [ 19 ]. This method of prun-ing before reranking in a typical “ retrieve then rerank ” setup is known as ranked list truncation (RLT) and has been widely adopted in reranking pipelines [ 13 , 16 ]. However, recent literature shows that a fixed cut-off or score-based heuristic method is often subopti-mal because they ignore query-specific relevance distributions and reranking gains [ 1 ]. Most existing approaches view truncation as a selection over the retrieved list, without exploiting richer semantic structure [ 10 ]. Additionally, there are instances where reranking a larger ranked list without truncation is beneficial to facilitate performance gains, particularly for listwise LLM rerankers [ 25 , 33 ]. However, the improvement in retrieval performance comes at the expense of added reranking time, which harms the overall respon-siveness of retrieval pipelines, particularly in real-time applica-tions [23]. Recent studies indicate that LLMs demonstrate strong capabil-ities in understanding semantics and query-document relation-ships [ 7 , 36 ]. In particular, LLMs have been successfully utilised for zero-shot, few-shot, and instruction-tuned relevance judgment, surpassing even human annotators in terms of consistency and com-pleteness [ 7 ]. However, while research has extensively explored the capability of LLMs for relevance judgment, their potential to gener-ate semantically controlled reference-documents within retrieval pipelines remains largely unexplored [30]. Our work investigates the complementary role of LLMs in multi-stage retrieval, utilizing them to generate a moderately relevant reference-document for a given query and then leveraging this document as a semantic pivot to distinguish between highly rel-evant and non-relevant documents in a ranked list. The idea of leveraging a semantic pivot enables designing a new RLT approach for efficient reranking in pointwise and pairwise setups that is both adaptive and model-agnostic, thereby improving efficiency without requiring explicit model training or parameter tuning. In listwise reranking, for which LLMs are now commonly used, the ranked list is often partitioned into overlapping windows and processed sequentially in batches to reduce the context length, known as the sliding window [ 21 , 31 ]. Using the generated pivot, we propose methods that can process the ranked list in either parallel batches of non-overlapping windows or overlapping windows with adaptive

Our Contributions. The primary contributions of this work are summarized as follows: (1) LLM-generated semantic reference document: We leverage the generation capability of LLMs to generate a semantically controlled pivot-document that acts as a pivot distinguishing relevant from non-relevant documents within a ranked list. (2) Adaptive truncation via pivot placement: We propose PSI-Rank to utilize the generated pivot for RLT in pointwise and pairwise setups, wherein the retriever itself determines the pivot’s position in the ranked list. This approach requires only a single model inference call for scoring the pivot, thereby improving reranking efficiency without exhaustive scoring. (3) Parallel listwise reranking through document propaga-tion: In listwise reranking, we incorporate the semantic pivot to enable document propagation across non-overlapping windows (SNOW), facilitating parallel batch processing and substantially reducing inference latency. (4) Adaptive stride mechanism for sliding windows: We ex-tend the conventional sliding window strategy by introducing an adaptive stride mechanism (VS-Sliding). The pivot dynami-cally estimates relevance density across reranking windows to balance contextual completeness and computational efficiency. (5) Enhanced anchor for TD-Part framework: We adapt the TD-Part algorithm for efficient listwise reranking and demon-strate that substituting the conventional top-document anchor with our LLM-generated pivot yields improved relevance con-sistency and ranking stability (GPTD-Part). Our experiments across in-domain and out-of-domain bench-marks demonstrate that our truncation mechanism outperforms ex-isting RLT-based approaches, while requiring no task-specific train-ing or sensitive hyperparameter tuning. Furthermore, our method accelerates LLM-based list-wise reranking by up to 66% relative to strong baselines, while maintaining, and in many cases improving, retrieval effectiveness. We highlight not only the practical signifi-cance of using LLM-generated documents to improve RLT but also offer insight into their robustness, model dependence, and applica-tion in multi-stage retrieval pipelines. The repository containing environment details and implementations of our proposed methods is shared for research and development 1.

## 2 Related Work 

Ranked List Truncation . Traditionally, RLT has been formu-lated as a cascade ranking problem. These methods determine the optimal cut-off depth for a ranked list before reranking to balance effectiveness against efficiency. Lien et al. [ 13 ] explored efficient top-𝑘 processing by dynamically pruning candidates unlikely to affect the final evaluation metric. For neural retrieval, RLT has evolved from heuristic score-based thresholding to learned models. Bahri et al. [ 1] introduced Choppy ,a transformer-based model that predicts query-specific cut-offs by analyzing the score distribution of the top retrieved documents. Sim-ilarly, Wu et al. [ 40 ] proposed AttnCut , which utilizes an attention 

> 1https://github.com/nilanjansb/DynamicRLT

mechanism to model the dependencies between document scores and the optimal truncation point. More recently, Meng et al. [ 16 ] in-vestigated RLT specifically for reranking pipelines, demonstrating that fixed-depth reranking is often suboptimal and that dynamic truncation can significantly improve the efficiency of LLM-based rerankers. 

LLM-based reranking . The recent developments in LLM ca-pabilities and scale, especially large context windows, have led to the emergence of zero-shot listwise reranking with generative ranking models [ 9, 14 , 33 , 34 ]. Listwise objectives better capture inter-document dependencies than pointwise or pairwise methods or traditional cross-encoders [ 33 ]. Distilled LLMs, trained via per-mutation distillation on teacher rankings from zero-shot prompts, outperform much larger supervised cross-encoders like monoT5 [ 24 ,25, 33] Despite these gains, inference efficiency remains the main chal-lenge while deploying listwise LLM rankers at scale. Recent ap-proaches, such as FIRST and its variants, limit generation to a single identifier token, thereby improving retrieval efficiency [ 2 , 9]. Li et al. [ 11 ] use documents from the ranked list as references for efficient zeroshot LLM reranking. Parry et al. [ 21 ] proposed Top-Down Partitioning (TDPart), which replaces window traversal with a pivot-guided selection that can be reranked in parallel. 

LLMs for Judgment and Generation . The capabilities of LLMs extend beyond ranking to relevance judgment and data generation. Thomas et al. [ 36 ] and Faggioli et al. [ 7] have benchmarked LLMs extensively as automated relevance judges. Upadhyay et al. pro-pose UMBRELA for relevance judgments with zeroshot and fewshot prompting. Meng et al. [ 17 ] employ reranking frameworks for im-proved relevance assessment. These frameworks demonstrate the LLM’s intrinsic understanding of query-document relevance rela-tionships [37]. Consequently, LLMs have been successfully employed to gen-erate synthetic training data to enable high performance dense retrieval with minimal examples [ 5, 30 ]. Approaches like HyDE [10 ]and Query2Doc [39 ] generate hypothetical documents to improve retrieval via zeroshot expansion. Ruiyang et al. [ 28 ] use finetuned LLMs to generate global scores for the documents in the ranked list, improving listwise reranking efficiency. 

## 3 Reference-Document Generation and its Utility 

In this section, we propose that an LLM can generate a moderately relevant answer ( reference-document ) to a query. This reference-document, based on its ranking position, can serve as a pivot in a ranked list to separate relevant from non-relevant documents. 

Motivation . Prior work has shown that LLMs have been suc-cessfully utilized to generate relevance judgments for query docu-ment pairs [ 6, 27 , 37 ]. LLMs serve as effective judges for relevance in IR collections, often demonstrating high correlation with human annotators [ 6, 27 ]. This implies that LLMs can understand graded relevance between query-document pairs when provided with a relevance scale similar to that of human assessors [ 37 ]. However, the focus of existing strategies remains solely on LLMs as judges, rather than generating documents with a specified relevance label. ## 3.2 Generative Listwise Reranking 

LLM-based reranking is typically performed using pointwise, pair-wise, or listwise strategies, depending on the model and type of task [ 26 , 31 , 32 ]. Among these reranking strategies, the listwise approach is generally preferred due to the fewer inferences and contextual comparisons required [31, 33]. 

Background . Listwise ranking is implemented using either a single window or a sliding window approach. However, existing lit-erature suggests that a single window approach can rerank approx-imately 20 documents from the first stage, limiting its applicability when more documents need to be reranked [ 21 , 31 ]. In contrast, the sliding window can rerank a larger number of documents by overlapping windows that slide across the entire document span. The use of overlaps ensures that potentially relevant documents are propagated across adjacent windows, allowing documents initially positioned at lower ranks to be promoted toward the top positions. Since this method lets the model focus on smaller windows itera-tively, it enables reranking without exceeding model context limits. However, the drawback of this strategy is the need for ≈ 𝑛 /𝑆 serial inferences, where 𝑛 is the number of documents to be reranked and 

𝑆 is the stride. 

Reranking using Non-Overlapping Window . Our proposed solution is to consider multiple single windows that contain non-overlapping sets of documents, i.e., implementing a sliding window with a stride equal to the window size (𝑆 = 𝑤 ). We name our reranking method as Shared Non-Overlapping Window (SNOW ). Under this strategy, rankings are generated independently for each localized window. However, the absence of overlapping documents between windows restricts the possibility of repositioning relevant documents from one window to another, thereby limiting the over-all effectiveness. Therefore, to make this approach functional, it is essential to avoid this restriction and allow for the propagation of documents across adjacent windows. SNOW employs the same pivot document 𝐷 ∗ generated by PSI-Rank as a shared semantic reference point across all non-overlapping windows. Unlike traditional sliding window approaches that rely on overlapping documents for propagation, SNOW injects 𝐷 ∗ into each window independently. This design exploits the pivot’s query-specific relevance boundary, thereby enabling document propaga-tion without sequential dependencies. Documents are promoted or demoted based on their ranking relative to 𝐷 ∗ within their re-spective windows as shown in Figure 3. Critically, this requires no additional pivot generation or retrieval scoring beyond the single inference already performed in PSI-Rank, making it computation-ally efficient while maintaining semantic consistency across all parallel reranking operations. ## 4 Evaluation 

In this section, we provide empirical evidence and experimental details to demonstrate the utility of the generated pivot in RLT and LLM-based listwise rerankers. The effectiveness of our pro-posed approach depends on several critical assumptions, which we systematically investigate through three research questions. 

## 4.1 Research Questions 

The fundamental hypothesis of our approach is that LLM-generated pivots can serve as an adaptive threshold between relevant and non-relevant documents. Unlike fixed thresholds that remain con-stant across queries, our pivots should theoretically adjust based on query complexity, domain characteristics, and the distributional properties of retrieved candidates. This question examines whether our generated pivots genuinely exhibit threshold-like behavior, cre-ating meaningful boundaries in the ranking space, or merely act as arbitrary reference points. Understanding this property is essential for our adaptive RLT, making our first research question: 

RQ1: Does the generated pivot function as a dynamic threshold for RLT in pointwise and pairwise reranking setups? 

Even if pivots function as dynamic thresholds, their utility de-pends on whether they translate into measurable ranking improve-ments. Listwise reranking with LLMs faces challenges due to sig-nificantly higher inference overhead compared to other reranking methods. We investigate whether pivot-guided comparisons help LLMs make more accurate relative judgments by providing stable reference points throughout the ranking process. This question directly addresses the practical value of our generated reference-document and therefore makes our second research question: 

RQ2: Does the generated pivot effectively improve LLM listwise reranking performance and efficiency? 

The quality of generated pivots likely depends on the gener-ating LLM’s understanding of relevance, domain knowledge, and text generation capabilities. If ranking performance varies substan-tially based on which model generates the pivot, this suggests that pivot generation itself is a critical bottleneck that requires careful model selection. Conversely, if diverse LLMs produce functionally equivalent pivots, it indicates that RLT is robust to variations in pivot quality. This question addresses whether investing in stronger models specifically for pivot generation is necessary or whether lightweight models are sufficient, making our third research ques-tion: 

RQ3: Does the ranking effectiveness significantly change with pivots generated by different LLMs?

## 4.3 Evaluation Measures 

To assess the performance of the proposed approach against the baselines, three key metrics that capture both the effectiveness and efficiency of the ranking pipelines are employed. 

• Mean Average Precision (MAP): We report the mean aver-age precision (MAP) at cut-off 100 (MAP@100) with the binary relevance threshold set to 2 for DL-19 and DL-2020 topics only. 

• Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain (nDCG): We use nDCG as the standard measure because it accounts for a docu-ment’s relevance and its position in the ranked list. We report this metric for both in-domain and out-of-domain evaluation [35]. 

• Inferences Per-Query (IPQ): Since the initial ranked list is truncated before reranking in pointwise and pairwise setups, we report IPQ by dividing the total inferences by the number of queries in the test set 1. A method with a lower IPQ is considered better when overall performance is not significantly reduced. 

• Speed-Up (SU): To quantify efficiency gains in listwise reranking setups, we report the proportional gains in run-time relative to the sliding window baseline. SU values greater than 1 indicate an improvement in efficiency. We include the time required to generate the pivot in the execution time for methods that use it.

4.4.4 Reranking Models. 

• mono-T5 : A widely adopted point-wise neural reranker that independently scores each document with respect to the query using a sequence-to-sequence formulation [20].

• Rank-Zephyr : A list-wise LLM-based reranker designed to jointly assess and reorder a set of candidate documents in a sin-gle inference. It captures global ranking context and has demon-strated strong effectiveness in modern retrieval benchmarks [ 25 ]. 

• Rank-Vicuna : A list-wise reranker built on the Vicuna instruction-tuned LLM, utilizing conversational reasoning to perform holistic ranking over document lists. It provides competitive performance while remaining adaptable across ranking setups [24]. 

• Rank-GPT : A list-wise reranking framework that utilizes large instruction-following language models to generate relevance-aware rankings over candidate lists. In our experiments, we use 

GPT-4o-mini for its balance between efficiency and effective-ness [31, 33].

## 5 Conclusion 

Our empirical analysis confirms that LLM-generated pivot docu-ments function as effective semantic thresholds for adaptive ranked list truncation (RLT) in both pointwise and pairwise reranking pipelines, achieving competitive MAP and nDCG@10 scores with 35-66% inference reductions across in-domain and out-of-domain collections. Our proposed pivot-guided listwise reranking meth-ods (SNOW, VS-Sliding, and GPTD-Part) consistently outperform baselines across test collections while showing stability across mul-tiple LLMs (RankZephyr, RankVicuna, RankGPT). Our methods in listwise setups achieved the significant efficiency gain while conserving or improving performance. Pivot generation proves robust across different models, exhibiting very low variance. These findings establish LLM-generated pivots as stable anchors that en-hance multi-stage reranking efficiency and stability without model training, solely by leveraging LLMs’ generative capabilities.
