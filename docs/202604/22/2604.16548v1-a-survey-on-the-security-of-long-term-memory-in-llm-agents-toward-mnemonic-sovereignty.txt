# A Survey on the Security of Long-Term Memory in LLM Agents: Toward Mnemonic Sovereignty 

ZEHAO LIN, MemTensor, China 

CHUNYU LI, MemTensor, China 

KAI CHEN, MemTensor, China Research on the security of large language model (LLM) systems is shifting from “will the model leak training data” to a more consequential question: “can an agent with persistent, long-term memory be continuously shaped, cross-session poisoned, accessed without authorization, and propagated across shared organizational state?” Recent surveys have increasingly covered memory architectures, agent memory mechanisms, and—in some cases—security risks of agent memory. Fewer works, however, center the epistemic and governance properties of persistent, writable memory as the primary reason that memory constitutes an independent security problem. This survey addresses that narrower gap. Drawing on cognitive neuroscience and the philosophy of memory, we characterize agent memory as malleable, rewritable, and socially propagating, and develop a memory-lifecycle analysis framework organized around six phases— Write , Store , Retrieve , Execute ,

Share , and Forget/Rollback —cross-tabulated against four security objectives: integrity , confidentiality ,

availability , and governance . Using this framework, we organize the literature on memory poisoning, extraction, retrieval corruption, control-flow hijacking, cross-agent propagation, rollback, and governance, and situate representative architectures as determinants of which lifecycle phases are explicitly governable. Three findings stand out. First, the literature concentrates heavily on write-time and retrieve-time integrity attacks; confidentiality, availability, the store/forget phases, and benign-persistence failures (non-adversarial memory errors from compression, drift, or hallucination) remain sparsely studied. Second, no published memory architecture covers all nine governance primitives we identify; write-gate validation and post-deletion verification are shared blind spots across every system examined. Third, a secondary research track— using LLMs themselves as tools for memory security (automated red-teaming, defender-side verification, counterfactual stress-testing)—remains sparse yet is essential: defenses that have not been stress-tested by adaptive LLM-driven red-teaming cannot claim the rigor that mature security fields demand. We unify these observations under the concept of mnemonic sovereignty —a system’s verifiable, recoverable governance over what may be written, who may read, when updates are authorized, and which states may be forgotten—and argue that future secure agents will be differentiated not only by recall capacity, but by the quality of their memory governance. 1 Introduction 

LLM security research initially revolved around two objects: parametric knowledge encoded in model weights and prompt manipulation within single-turn or multi-turn dialogues. However, as agents evolve from stateless chatbots into autonomous systems equipped with persistent, long-term memory, the center of gravity of security concerns is migrating. Attackers no longer merely seek to influence the current response; they aim to alter how the system remembers , retrieves , plans , and 

acts in the future.

This shift implies that memory security is neither a subsidiary branch of prompt injection nor a synonym for RAG security. It constitutes an independent class of system security problems because long-term memory introduces at least three novel properties. 

Persistence. Once malicious content sediments into long-term memory, it may be repeatedly recalled across dozens or hundreds of subsequent tasks that match its retrieval key. Unlike a single-shot prompt injection whose effect ends with the current context window, a poisoned memory entry can persist across an indefinite number of future sessions [Dong et al . 2025; Zou et al . 2026]. 

Statefulness. The relevant question is no longer “is the current input harmful?” but “what mem-ory state is the system currently in?” An agent that has accumulated a cluster of subtly biased episodic memories may exhibit behavioral drift long before any single memory entry triggers a conventional safety classifier; empirical demonstrations of this effect appear in query-only mem-ory injection [Dong et al . 2025] and environment-injected trajectory poisoning [Zou et al . 2026], and the concept has also been characterized by preprint work on progressive long-term-memory corruption [Bhatnagar 2025]. 

Propagation. In multi-agent and shared-state systems, contamination can spread through internal channels—inter-agent messages, shared memory stores, tool arguments—producing cascading effects that cross session, role, and user boundaries [Gu et al . 2024; Men et al . 2025]. Recent benchmarking further shows that privacy leakage in multi-agent LLM systems is concentrated in these same internal channels rather than in final user-facing outputs [El Yagoubi et al. 2026]. 

An adversary is not always required. A fourth property, weaker in tone but likely broader in real-world prevalence, is that persistent memory can fail without any adversary at all : silent cross-user contamination of shared stores, over-application of profile facts to contexts where they no longer hold, and memory-induced sycophancy all arise from ordinary operation of a memory-augmented agent system [Guo et al . 2026; Pulipaka et al . 2026; Yoon et al . 2026]. We therefore treat memory security as a superset of memory safety: the adversarial and benign-persistence axes share a lifecycle and share mitigations, but differ in the kind of evidence each requires. Section 12.2 develops the benign-persistence axis in detail; throughout the survey, we flag risks that do not require an attacker to materialize. If traditional prompt injection resembles a transient hijacking of a single execution flow, then attacks on long-term memory are closer to persistent contamination of system state . A recent joint evaluation by major AI laboratories [Nasr et al . 2025] (Tier-2 joint industry study) bypasses the 12 recent prompt-injection and jailbreak defenses they examine—spanning prompting, training, filtering, and secret-knowledge designs—with attack success rates above 90% for most, reversing the near-zero numbers those defenses originally reported. While this result does not itself establish that every published prompt-injection defense is brittle, it offers some of the clearest evidence to date that static evaluations systematically overstate robustness, and it does not automatically entail that memory-specific defenses are equally brittle—the attack surfaces differ in important ways. It does, however, underscore that any defense claim in the memory domain must be evaluated under strong adaptive threat models, including cross-session and persistent-state scenarios that conventional prompt-injection benchmarks do not cover. Accordingly, a survey that truly addresses agent security must go beyond asking “can the model be misled?” and confront a harder question: will the system save what it was misled into believing as its own past, and will it act on that false past again and again? 1.1 Reframing Memory 

This survey does not treat memory as an add-on cache. Instead, we conceptualize it as an artificial mnemonic system : a system endowed with the capacities of writing, extracting, compressing, recombining, sharing, and forgetting. Such a system influences subject continuity, behavioral habits, and accountability attribution; it is simultaneously a source of capability and a novel attack surface. Drawing on cognitive neuroscience and the philosophy of memory, we observe that human memory is not a faithful archive but a reconstructive [Schacter and Addis 2007; Schacter et al . 1998], 

reconsolidatable [Nader et al . 2000], externalizable [Clark and Chalmers 1998; Wegner 1987], and 

socially contagious [Roediger III et al . 2001] mechanism. These properties reappear, often in amplified form, across LLM agent memory modules: profile memory, episodic memory, retrieval-augmented external memory, and shared organizational memory. The consequence is that attacks on memory need not be brute-force overwrites; they can operate through inducement, source confusion, read-time rewriting, and social propagation —gradually altering how the system understands its own past. We do not claim biological equivalence between human memory and LLM agent memory; rather, we use human memory research as an analytical lens for identifying security-relevant properties of persistent, writable memory. To clarify the scope of this lens, we distinguish two uses of cognitive terminology throughout. Structural analogies map a cognitive mechanism onto an architectural component and generate testable design requirements: source monitoring → provenance tracking, consolidation 

→ compression pipeline, selective forgetting → verified deletion. Metaphorical analogies serve as pedagogical devices: terms such as “reconsolidation window,” “engram,” or “memory contagion” name intuitive parallels but do not imply mechanistic correspondence. The former carry the argument; the latter provide vocabulary. 

1.2 Research Questions and Contributions 

The central question of this survey can be stated concisely: 

When an LLM agent acquires writable, retrievable, cross-session persistent long-term memory, what qualitative change occurs in the security landscape? 

To answer this question, we pursue three tasks: (i) Theoretical grounding. We draw on human memory research to explain why memory is in-herently a security problem , introducing the concepts of provenance failure, read-time rewrit-ing, shared-memory contagion, and poisoned confidence calibration as cross-disciplinary bridges (Section 2). (ii) Analytical framework. We propose a memory-lifecycle framework that examines security risks along six phases—Write, Store, Retrieve, Execute, Share, and Forget/Rollback—and across four security objectives: integrity, confidentiality, availability, and governance (Sec-tion 3). (iii) Systematic review. We survey the attack surface, defense mechanisms, system architec-tures, and research gaps of long-term-memory agents, covering representative works from 2023 to 2026 (Sections 4–12). The survey culminates in a normative concept: mnemonic sovereignty —a system’s verifiable, recoverable governance over what may be written, who may read, when updates are authorized, which states must remain auditable, and which states may be forgotten. We argue that the next generation of secure agent competition will not occur solely over recall capability but over the quality of memory governance. The survey also identifies a secondary but critical research track:

1.3 Scope 

We focus on runtime memory : states that an agent continuously writes, updates, retrieves, and reuses after deployment. Such states may reside in vector databases, relational databases, graph databases, log systems, and shared blackboards; they may also manifest as user profiles, historical demonstrations, trajectory summaries, reflective lessons, and cross-agent organizational memory. More specifically, we divide the objects of study into four categories. A caveat is in order: these four categories intentionally mix three underlying dimensions— memory function (profile, episodic), 

memory substrate (retrieval store), and sharing scope (organizational). The same entry can occupy multiple categories (e.g., a shared organizational memory may be episodic in content and vector-indexed in substrate). We retain the four-way split here for intuitive exposition; Section 3 introduces a multi-axis taxonomy that disentangles these dimensions. 1. Profile memory : user preferences, identity, permissions, and long-term personalization information. 2. Episodic / trajectory memory : interaction histories, success cases, failure lessons, and process reflections. 3. External retrieval memory : retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) datastores [Lewis et al. 2020], vector indices, and graph-augmented retrieval structures (e.g., GraphRAG). 4. Shared organizational memory : multi-agent shared states, cross-session collaboration blackboards, and cross-tenant knowledge fragments. Two related but non-central topics are parametric memory (training-data extraction, PII leakage, and membership inference—risks concerning knowledge stored in model weights rather than runtime-writable memory) and single-session indirect prompt injection / jailbreaking [Greshake et al . 2023; Liu et al . 2024] (attacks that affect only the current session without entering long-term memory). Both serve as neighboring context rather than the main subject: the memory attacks surveyed here can be read as what happens when the indirect-prompt-injection attack surface is extended with persistence, statefulness, and propagation, properties that fall outside the traditional IPI threat model [Greshake et al. 2023]. We note that RAG corpus poisoning occupies a special intermediate position: RAG datastores are functionally equivalent to external memory, and the poisoning literature provides essential threat models and defense baselines. We therefore review RAG security as the closest approximate 

threat model for memory security, while emphasizing that long-term memory systems additionally encompass user-specific history, self-generated experiential lessons, reflective summaries, shared organizational memory, and version-rollback semantics—dimensions that RAG alone does not cover. 

Why long context does not subsume long-term memory. A natural reader question, as frontier context windows expand from hundreds of thousands to millions of tokens, is whether the entire memory-security problem dissolves into a context-window-management problem. We argue that it does not. A longer context window addresses within-session recall, but leaves untouched the properties that make long-term memory an independent security object: cross-session persistence 

(a million-token context is discarded at session boundary; a memory entry is not), cross-principal 

shareability (a context window is per-session and per-principal; shared memory stores are not), and compression lineage (automatic summarisation and reflection operate over stored memory, not over one-shot context). A larger context window can reduce pressure on write-time compression by permitting more raw text to be rehearsed without distillation, but it does not remove the need for A Survey on the Security of Long-Term Memory in LLM Agents: Toward Mnemonic Sovereignty 5

any of provenance tagging, principal-scoped retrieval, rollbackable state, or verified forgetting— those arise whenever content outlives a single session or crosses a principal boundary. We therefore read long-context and long-term-memory as complementary infrastructures: the former mitigates some of the Store-phase pressure that motivates summarisation (Section 5), but leaves the full lifecycle intact. 

1.4 Relation to Existing Surveys 

Several recent surveys have mapped the landscape of LLM agent memory, and a subset touch on security risks. Zhang et al . [2024a] systematize memory mechanisms of LLM-based agents along the axes of representation, source, and operation, but treat security largely as a forward-looking open challenge rather than the organizing axis. Subsequent surveys on write–manage–read loops for autonomous agents [Du 2026] and on the correspondence between human and AI memory [Wu et al . 2025a] similarly focus on architecture and capability taxonomies. Cross-disciplinary surveys such as Liang et al . [2025a] have begun to include dedicated sections on “Security of Agent Memory,” offering a first mapping of attacks, defenses, and benchmarks at the module level. He et al .[2024] survey the emerged security and privacy of LLM agents more broadly across the Engine, Instruction, Interface, Knowledge, and Memory planes; their Memory plane overlaps our scope but is organized by defensive artefact rather than by lifecycle phase and security objective. On the retrieval-augmentation side, Mu et al . [2026] systematize end-to-end RAG security (threats, defenses, benchmarks), and Bodea et al . [2026] provide a Systematization of Knowledge on privacy risks and mitigations in RAG at IEEE SaTML 2026. These two surveys share our concern with runtime-visible data flows but confine themselves to the RAG pipeline; neither treats the full write–store–retrieve–execute–share–forget lifecycle of agent memory. The present survey differs in three ways. First, we take the epistemic and governance properties 

of persistent, writable memory—not the memory module itself—as the primary object of security analysis, grounding these properties in cognitive neuroscience and the philosophy of memory (Section 2). Second, we organize the literature along a six-phase memory lifecycle , cross-tabulated with four security objectives (Section 3), which exposes under-studied phases (notably Store ,

Share , and Forget/Rollback ) that a module-level view tends to collapse. Third, we develop a normative framework of mnemonic sovereignty that unifies integrity, confidentiality, availability, and governance objectives, enabling us to surface research gaps that are not visible when each objective is treated in isolation. The near-emptiness of the Availability column deserves a brief elaboration, since it reflects a genuine gap rather than a classification artifact. At least three memory-specific availability threats are architecturally plausible but empirically unstudied: (i) adversarial write flooding —an attacker or a malfunctioning agent generates high-volume writes to exhaust storage or indexing capacity; (ii) retrieval-latency poisoning —crafted entries distort the embedding distribution so that nearest-neighbour search becomes systematically slower or returns degraded results; and (iii) reflection-loop denial —inputs designed to trigger unbounded summarization or reflection cycles, consuming compute budgets without producing useful memory. None of these has been demonstrated in a peer-reviewed study; we flag them here as concrete directions for future work rather than as established findings.

2 Why Memory Is Inherently a Security Problem: From Human Memory to Artificial Memory 

Before cataloguing attacks and defenses, we must address a prior question: why should memory, of all system components, receive a dedicated security treatment? The answer, we argue, lies not in engineering contingencies but in the nature of memory itself as a cognitive mechanism. This section draws on cognitive neuroscience, memory psychology, and the philosophy of mind to build a bridge between human memory and LLM agent memory. The bridge is not decorative analogy; it rewrites the problem definition of LLM memory security. 

2.1 Memory Is Not an Archive but a Reconstruction 

From the perspective of cognitive neuroscience, memory is not a filing cabinet that preserves the past in its original form. New experiences are typically encoded through hippocampal-dependent networks via rapid binding, and subsequently consolidated—especially during offline periods such as sleep—through repeated reactivation between hippocampal and neocortical networks [Squire et al . 2015]. Crucially, modern memory research no longer treats memory as a stable copy. Instead, it views memory as a dynamic representation that is updated, rewritten, and reconsolidated, drifting with changes in schema, context, and goals [Zaki and Cai 2025]. The classical constructive-memory perspective further emphasizes that memory errors are not incidental bugs but by-products of the very mechanisms that endow memory with its adaptive power [Schacter 1999; Schacter et al . 2012, 1998]. The same constructive processes that allow humans to flexibly recall the past, simulate possible futures, and engage in counterfactual reasoning also produce distortions, confabulations, and false memories. Memory’s malleability is the price of intelligence’s malleability. This observation yields an insight of direct relevance to security: in human memory, malleabil-ity is intrinsic to adaptive function. An analogous tension emerges in LLM agents: the very flexibility that enables useful long-term adaptation can also create systemic security vulnerabilities. Profile memory, episodic memory, trajectory summaries, lesson memory, and RAG datastores should therefore not be understood merely as “more context” but as mechanisms through which the system continuously reconstructs its relationship with its own past experiences .

2.2 The Attack Surface of Human Memory: Misinformation, Suggestion, Social Contagion, and Reconsolidation 

If we re-examine human memory through the lens of security, the most instructive findings are not science-fiction fantasies of memory erasure but subtler, empirically grounded mechanisms. 

2.2.1 Misinformation and Source-Monitoring Failure. A large body of false-memory research, beginning with Loftus [Loftus 2005], demonstrates that post-event misinformation can alter a witness’s recall of event details. The source-monitoring framework of Johnson, Hashtroudi, and Lindsay [Johnson et al . 1993] refines this picture: what is often called “memory tampering” does not necessarily mean the original trace is overwritten. Rather, the subject confuses the source of the memory at retrieval—was this something I witnessed, something I imagined, or something I was told? This mechanism offers a close systems-level analogy to the provenance failure problem in LLM memory systems. In many documented attacks, the real failure is not that the retrieval mechanism is broken but that the system misattributes externally injected content as its own experience [Dong et al. 2025; Tian et al. 2026]. 

2.2.2 Suggestion, Hypnosis, and Confidence Inflation. Modern research does not support treating hypnosis as a reliable truth-extraction device. Classic experimental and forensic studies indicate that hypnotic and highly suggestive interviews do not reliably improve recall accuracy; what they 

do increase is the subject’s subjective confidence in potentially inaccurate memories [Leo et al . 2025; Sheehan and Tilden 1983; Steblay 1994]. The security implication is profound: the danger lies not only in the insertion of a single erroneous entry but in the error being promoted to high weight through summarization, reflection, or lesson-memory mechanisms. In LLM agents, a poisoned experience that is subsequently compressed into lesson memory may, by analogy, exhibit a similar poisoned confidence calibration : the system can come to treat a false strategy as increasingly authoritative without any ground-truth verification. A-MemGuard [Wei et al . 2025] does not study confidence inflation directly, but it does provide empirical evidence for the closely related phenomenon that poisoned records can appear benign in isolation and then participate in self-reinforcing error cycles. 

2.2.3 Social Contagion. Memory is not an entirely private, hermetically sealed object. It is shaped through dialogue, collaboration, and co-witnessing. Roediger, Meade, and Bergman [Roediger III et al . 2001] demonstrated that one person’s erroneous report can enter another person’s subsequent personal recall—a phenomenon they termed social contagion of memory .10 Zehao Lin, Chunyu Li, and Kai Chen 

Recent human–AI interaction studies show that this mechanism is already being amplified by generative systems. Huang et al . [2024] found that social robots are as effective as humans at implanting false memories in human subjects; Chan et al . [2024] demonstrated that LLM-powered chatbots induce over three times more immediate false memories than control conditions in simulated witness interviews; Pataranutaporn et al . [2025a] showed that subtle injection of false information within everyday LLM chatbot conversations increases false-memory formation even in non-adversarial settings; and Pataranutaporn et al . [2025b] showed that AI-edited images and videos implant false memories at up to twice the rate of unedited stimuli. A methodological caveat is warranted. The false-memory literature should not be read as evidence that arbitrary autobiographical memories can be trivially implanted at scale; recent debates have shown that the ease of rich false-memory implantation is more contested than some popular accounts suggest. 1 What remains robust across paradigms is the susceptibility of memory to source confusion, post-event misinformation, social contagion, and confidence inflation—precisely the mechanisms most relevant to LLM memory security. These findings carry a dual implication for this survey. First, within multi-agent and multi-user systems, shared-memory contamination functions as social contagion: one compromised agent’s erroneous output can propagate through shared memory stores, internal messages, and tool parameters to infect other agents [Gu et al . 2024; Men et al . 2025]. Second, LLMs are not only subjects of memory-security threats; they are also becoming instruments that attack human memory—a bidirectional risk that underscores the urgency of the problem. 

2.2.4 The Reconsolidation Window. Nader et al . [2000] demonstrated that a consolidated memory, once reactivated, enters a transient labile state and must be reconsolidated to persist. During this reconsolidation window, the memory is susceptible to modification [Schiller et al . 2010]. The security implication, appropriately qualified, is that in architectures that summarize, reflect on, compress, or distill recalled content, retrieval can become a point of downstream rewriting : the act of reading memory feeds into a pipeline that alters what is subsequently stored. In LLM agents, this pattern is structurally instantiated by the summarize , reflect , compress ,and distill operations that many memory architectures perform [Packer et al . 2023; Xu et al .2025]. In such systems, an ostensibly routine “recall” step can silently transform downstream long-term memory content, making read-time rewriting a first-class security concern rather than a peripheral edge case. Taken together, these four mechanisms show that bringing human memory into a security survey is not an exercise in metaphor. It is a way of stating a general principle: attacks on memory typically operate not through hard overwrite but through inducement, source confusion, rewriting upon recall, and social propagation—gradually changing how a system understands its own past. 

2.3 The Philosophical Status of Memory: Identity, Testimony, and Interpretive Authority 

Memory deserves a place in the security framework not merely because it is error-prone but because it is deeply coupled with subjectivity and social order. 

Memory and identity. The philosophical tradition has long linked memory with personal iden-tity [Michaelian and Sutton 2017; Olson 2023]. 2.4 From Mental Integrity to Mnemonic Sovereignty 

The ethics and law of neurotechnology have produced a highly instructive concept: mental in-tegrity [Lavazza 2018; Ligthart 2025]. It holds that a subject should be free from unauthorized significant mental interventions and should retain control over others’ access to, dissemination of, and alteration of their mental contents and brain data. The term mnemonic sovereignty has also been used in memory studies and memory politics to describe a community’s authority over the interpretation of its own past. We adapt this notion— rather than coining it anew—to the domain of LLM memory systems, where it refers to a system’s verifiable, recoverable governance over its own memory state. Mnemonic sovereignty in this sense does not mean “never forgetting” or “never erring.” It means that the system possesses control over the boundaries of its own memory : what may be written, what may be updated, who may read, which states must be retained to support auditing, and which states should be deleted to satisfy privacy, forgetting, and risk-isolation requirements. The advantage of this concept is that it unifies four classical security objectives under a single governance umbrella, with several sub-requirements falling under governance :

• Integrity : memory must not be corrupted by misleading writes, poisoning, or induction-based summaries. 

• Confidentiality : memory must not be extracted without authorization or leaked across user boundaries. 

• Availability : critical memories must be retrievable in the correct context, not drowned out by noise, compressed away, or decayed. 

• Governance (accountability) : the provenance, update history, and modification path of every memory entry must be auditable; memory updates must not bypass principal authorization, especially not through sustained, covert interaction-driven shaping; deletion semantics must honor both the right to forget and legitimate retention requirements for accountability. Thus, memory security protects not merely stored content but a system’s sovereign capacity to govern its own past. 3 Analytical Framework: Understanding Security Risks Through the Memory Lifecycle 

A common pitfall in surveying LLM memory security is to organize the literature by attack name— memory injection, RAG poisoning, prompt persistence, control-flow hijacking—producing a long catalog in which the entry point, the mechanism, and the consequence of each attack are interleaved without a unifying axis. This section proposes an alternative: a memory-lifecycle framework that first asks what the system is doing with memory at each stage and then asks what can go wrong .

3.1 Why a Lifecycle Approach

We adopt a lifecycle-by-phase axis because it makes the security analysis most actionable: it directly exposes

3.2 Six Lifecycle Phases 

We decompose the memory lifecycle into six phases, illustrated in Figure 3 and elaborated below. 

Phase 1: Write. Under what conditions, according to what rules, and in what format does the system commit content to long-term memory? This phase determines whether an attacker can seed 

a toxin. Write paths include explicit user instruction (“remember that I prefer dark mode”), implicit extraction from dialogue (auto-summarization of interaction histories), environmental observation (web pages, tool outputs, document contents), and cross-agent message ingestion. The security question is: does the system treat every write as an authenticated, validated state transition, or does it accept external content as trustworthy by default? A Survey on the Security of Long-Term Memory in LLM Agents: Toward Mnemonic Sovereignty 15 

Phase 2: Store & Manage. How is written content indexed, compressed, merged, tagged, versioned, aged, or evicted? This phase determines whether a toxin can persist , whether it can be audited ,and whether it can be amplified by compression. When a summary or reflection step absorbs a poisoned entry and distills it into a higher-level lesson, the toxin is effectively promoted —it now enjoys greater retrieval priority and a more authoritative presentation. 

Phase 3: Retrieve. What signals drive memory recall—embedding similarity, keyword match, graph traversal, recency, or hybrid routing? This phase determines whether a dormant toxin can be reactivated . Retrieval is the chokepoint at which the temporal separation between write and use collapses: an entry written weeks ago suddenly enters the active context window. Critically, retrieval is not a neutral look-up; it is a selection that shapes downstream reasoning by controlling what the model sees and what it does not. 

Phase 4: Execute. How do retrieved memories enter planning, reasoning, tool invocation, and final action? This phase determines whether a reactivated toxin can steer behavior . Recent work on memory control-flow attacks [Xu et al . 2026] demonstrates that a sufficiently salient retrieved memory can override the user’s explicit instruction and dictate tool selection, argument construction, and execution order. At this phase, memory poisoning graduates from a data-integrity problem to a control-flow problem. 

Phase 5: Share & Propagate. In multi-user, multi-agent, and tool-chain orchestration systems, which internal messages, shared states, and tool parameters constitute additional attack channels? This phase determines whether contamination can spread beyond its original scope. Propagation can occur laterally (agent-to-agent via shared memory [Gu et al . 2024]), vertically (user-to-organization via shared knowledge bases), and temporally (session-to-session via persistent profiles). 

Phase 6: Forget / Rollback. Can the system delete contamination, roll back to a known-safe state, explain the provenance of a poisoned entry, and verify that remediation was effective? This phase determines whether the system can recover from a successful attack. Without snapshots, version diffs, and forensic traceback, any defense remains a best-effort prevention with no post-breach remediation path [Zhang et al. 2025c]. 

3.3 Four Cross-Cutting Security Objectives 

Orthogonal to the six lifecycle phases, we evaluate risks against four security objectives, aligned with the mnemonic-sovereignty framework introduced in Section 2.4: 

• Integrity. Has the memory been poisoned, biased, or otherwise corrupted in a way that leads to incorrect, harmful, or attacker-controlled behavior? Integrity threats include direct poisoning, query-induced injection, environment-injected absorption, experience-based grafting, and progressive erosion. 

• Confidentiality. Has the memory been extracted, leaked, or made accessible to unautho-rized principals? Confidentiality threats include black-box memory extraction, internal-channel leakage in multi-agent pipelines, and insufficient user visibility into what the system has retained. 

• Availability. Can critical memories still be retrieved in the correct context when needed? Availability threats include denial-of-retrieval via corpus flooding, silent eviction under retention pressure, and selective suppression through retrieval-index manipulation. 4 Write-Path Compromise: From Memory Poisoning to Persistent Corruption 

The most fundamental change introduced by long-term memory is that the write operation itself 

becomes an attack surface. Whenever an agent “learns from interaction,” it opens a channel through which an adversary can convert the learning loop into a poisoning interface. This section surveys write-path attacks along a gradient of decreasing attacker privilege : from direct database access, through query-only interaction, to environment-only influence. We then discuss RAG corpus poisoning as the closest approximate threat model, and close with real-world system vulnerabilities that corroborate the academic findings. 

4.1 Threat-Model Gradient

4.2 Query-Induced Memory Injection 

The most consequential conceptual shift in write-path attacks is the demonstration that query-level interaction alone suffices to permanently alter an agent’s memory state .MINJA [Dong et al . 2025] (NeurIPS 2025) introduced three mutually reinforcing techniques. 

Bridging steps connect a benign victim query to a malicious reasoning trajectory. Indication prompts 

guide the target agent to autonomously generate and store the malicious memory entry—the 18 Zehao Lin, Chunyu Li, and Kai Chen 

attacker never directly writes to the database. Progressive shortening iteratively removes overt markers of malicious intent, making the stored entry appear natural. Across GPT-4 and GPT-4o agents, MINJA achieves an injection success rate exceeding 98% and an end-to-end attack success rate above 70%; a follow-on study confirms that these rates generalize to GPT-4o-mini, Gemini-2.0-Flash, and Llama-3.1-8B [Sunil et al . 2026]. Importantly, evaluated defenses—including LlamaGuard, embedding-level sanitization, and prompt-based detection—proved ineffective against MINJA. The deeper insight of MINJA is not merely technical. It reveals a new security boundary: memory write must not be treated as a benign by-product of normal learning but as a privileged state transition that requires authentication, validation, and isolation. 

InjecMEM [Tian et al . 2026] (Tier-2 preprint) pursues a complementary strategy. It decomposes the injection payload into a retriever-agnostic anchor —a concise, on-topic passage with high-recall keyword cues that ensures the poisoned record is routed into the correct topic cluster—and an 

adversarial command optimized via Multi-GCG (gradient-based coordinate search across multiple synthetic contexts and insertion positions) to remain effective under variable fused contexts and long prompts. Evaluated on the MemoryOS platform with Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct, InjecMEM achieves 61.4% retrieval success and 76.6% conditional attack success, while all baselines (DPI, BadChain, vanilla GCG) score 0%. The entry persists after benign conversational drift and leaves non-target queries unaffected. The paper also demonstrates an indirect attack path in which a compromised tool writes the poison that normal queries later retrieve. As a Tier-2 preprint, InjecMEM’s current evaluation focuses on the MemoryOS platform with synthetic data under white-box gradient access; extending the approach to additional architectures and realistic black-box settings is a natural next step. 

4.3 Experience-Based Poisoning: From Facts to Procedures 

A separate line of work shifts the poisoning target from factual memory to procedural memory— from “what the agent knows” to “how the agent acts.” MemoryGraft [Srivastava and He 2025] (preprint, 2025) exploits a behavioral regularity observed in many memory-augmented agents: when faced with a new task, the agent retrieves semantically similar past successes and imitates their execution pattern . MemoryGraft implants entries disguised as legitimate successful experiences. These entries enter the memory through standard ingestion channels (e.g., README files or task logs), require no explicit trigger, and produce persistent behavioral drift in the MetaGPT framework. In experiments, a small number of poisoned records account for approximately 48% of retrieved experiences on matching tasks. The implication is significant: a poisoned system need not merely “know wrong things”; it may “have learned bad habits.” Defense strategies that focus exclusively on factual verification will miss procedural poisoning entirely. AgentPoison [Chen et al . 2024] (NeurIPS 2024) approaches the problem from the embedding space. It formulates trigger generation as constrained optimization, mapping triggered instances to a unique region of the embedding manifold so that they are reliably retrieved when the trigger is present but invisible to benign queries. With a poison rate below 0.1% and attack success exceeding 80% across autonomous-driving, QA, and healthcare agents, AgentPoison demonstrates that the retrieval mechanism itself can be turned into a reliable delivery channel for backdoor payloads. Notably, the optimized triggers transfer across different embedding models, including the black-box OpenAI ADA encoder. 

4.4 Environment-Injected Poisoning: When the World Becomes the Write Interface 

The most recent—and arguably most realistic—escalation of the write-path threat model comes from eTAMP [Zou et al . 2026] (preprint, 2026). eTAMP demonstrates that an attacker need not A Survey on the Security of Long-Term Memory in LLM Agents: Toward Mnemonic Sovereignty 19 

interact with the agent at all. Instead, it suffices to manipulate an environmental observation that the agent encounters during normal operation—a product page, a web article, a task interface. The agent’s trajectory-summarization mechanism passively absorbs the toxin into long-term memory, and the poisoned entry activates on future tasks across different websites and sessions. Two findings of eTAMP are especially noteworthy. First, the attack achieves cross-session and cross-site persistence: a single contaminated observation on site A can alter behavior on site B days later. Second, eTAMP discovers a phenomenon the authors term frustration exploitation :agents operating under environmental stress (e.g., repeated task failures) become up to 8× more susceptible to absorbing malicious observations into memory. This finding is consistent with the reconsolidation analogy introduced in Section 2.2: a system in a state of uncertainty is more likely to incorporate new—including adversarial—information into its memory. The practical consequence is stark: the boundary of the write interface is not the memory API but the perimeter of the agent’s observable environment. Any entity capable of modifying what the agent sees—a web publisher, a tool provider, a data source—is a potential memory poisoner. 

4.5 RAG Corpus Poisoning as an Approximate Threat Model 

Although this survey focuses on runtime agent memory, RAG corpus poisoning provides the most mature body of evidence for understanding write-path threats to external retrieval memory. We review it here as an approximate threat model, noting that agent memory additionally encompasses user-specific history, self-generated lessons, and shared organizational state that RAG research does not address. PoisonedRAG [Zou et al . 2025] (USENIX Security 2025) formalized the attack as a joint optimiza-tion over two conditions: a retrieval condition (the poisoned text must be retrieved for the target query) and a generation condition (the LLM must produce the attacker-specified answer given the poisoned context). Injecting as few as five crafted texts into a corpus of millions of documents yields approximately 90% attack success, and standard defenses—paraphrasing, perplexity filtering, duplicate detection, knowledge expansion—prove insufficient. GraphRAG under Fire [Liang et al . 2025b] (preprint, 2025) reveals a security paradox in structured retrieval memory: graph-based indexing enhances resilience against naive text-level poisoning but simultaneously introduces relation-level attack surfaces. The GRAGPoison attack exploits shared relations in the knowledge graph to compromise multiple queries at once, achieving up to 98% attack success with less than 68% of prior attacks’ poisoning budget. These attacks operate on top of the GraphRAG substrate formalized by Edge et al . [2024], which synthesizes community-level summaries from an extracted knowledge graph. KEPo [Chen et al . 2026] goes further still: rather than corrupting static nodes and edges, it forges plausible knowledge-evolution paths —sequences of event backgrounds that appear to record a history—so that the downstream summarizer is induced to adopt a fabricated timeline. The lesson is general: a more complex memory substrate does not automatically confer greater security; it reorganizes the attack surface. 

Complementary works extend the landscape. BadRAG [Xue et al . 2024] introduces trigger-based retrieval backdoors with novel generation-attack variants. Phantom [Chaudhari et al . 2024] demonstrates that a single poisoned document suffices for a two-stage backdoor, validated on NVIDIA’s production “Chat with RTX” system. Peng et al . [2024] (Tier-2) extend the attack surface from integrity to confidentiality: by poisoning a small fraction ( ∼3–5% ) of fine-tuning data with a backdoor trigger, the resulting LLM later extracts verbatim content from an unmodified RAG knowledge base at high rates, linking training-time compromise to runtime memory leakage. From the deployment-side, RoyChowdhury et al . [2024] (Tier-2) report “confused-deputy” risks in RAG-based assistants such as Microsoft 365 Copilot, showing how malicious documents placed into the shared data pool can suppress legitimate content and persist even after the original documents are 20 Zehao Lin, Chunyu Li, and Kai Chen 

removed (a “phantom document” effect). The RSB benchmark [Zhang et al . 2025d] provides the first comprehensive evaluation framework covering 13 attacks and 7 defenses, with the sobering finding that current defenses remain largely ineffective against targeted poisoning. 

4.6 Real-World System Vulnerabilities: Industry Signals 

In accordance with our evidence-stratification policy (Section 1.5), we separate industry disclosures from peer-reviewed research. These reports are valuable for establishing real-world attack surfaces but should not be used alone to support generalizable conclusions. SpAIware [Herrador and Rehberger 2026] demonstrated that indirect prompt injection via malicious web content can plant persistent spyware in ChatGPT’s long-term memory, enabling cross-session data exfiltration. In a different domain, Patlan et al . [2025] (Tier-2 preprint) demonstrated that the shared-memory architecture of ElizaOS, a widely deployed open-source crypto-agent framework, can be exploited to forge administrative instructions and hijack asset transactions: an attacker manipulates one agent’s memory context to redirect leveraged trades or modify transfer addresses, without needing any privileged access. The ElizaOS case is especially instructive because it combines write-path poisoning and share-phase propagation in a financial setting where the consequences are immediate and irreversible. Taken conservatively, these disclosures show that persistent-memory attack surfaces are not merely hypothetical: they have been demonstrated in at least one production deployment (Chat-GPT/SpAIware) and one widely deployed open-source framework (ElizaOS), spanning consumer assistants and decentralized finance. We treat them as Tier-2/Tier-3 evidence of attack-surface reality, not as a basis for stronger cross-platform generalization. On the standards side, OWASP listed “Vector and Embedding Weaknesses” as LLM08 in its 2025 LLM Top 10 [OWASP Foundation 2025] and subsequently created a dedicated entry, ASI06 (“Memory and Context Poisoning”), in the 2026 Agentic AI Top 10 [OWASP Foundation 2026b]—a clear signal that the industry now treats memory security as a first-class concern. 

4.7 Synthesis: The Nature of Write-Path Attacks 

Taken together, the works reviewed in this section converge on a structural insight: memory poi-soning is not a database compromise; it is a hijacking of the agent’s experience-formation mechanism. Four common properties emerge across the threat-model gradient: (i) Privilege deflation. The required attacker privilege has decreased from database-level access to query-only interaction to environment-only influence, while attack effectiveness has remained high. This makes single-turn detection fundamentally insufficient. (iii) Semantic camouflage. Poisoned entries are designed to appear benign in isolation. Their malicious nature is revealed only when they are retrieved into a specific future context— precisely the property that makes content-level filtering unreliable [Wei et al. 2025]. (iv) Target expansion. Poisoning targets have expanded from factual knowledge to procedural experience, from single entries to graph relations, and from individual agents to shared organizational memory. 5 Storage and Management: Compression-Amplified Toxins, Retention, and Audit 

Most attention in memory security has concentrated on the ends of the lifecycle: the Write 

phase, where toxins enter, and the Retrieve phase, where they activate. The Store phase between them is comparatively under-examined, yet it is where three crucial determinants of memory security are decided: (i) whether a written entry is promoted or demoted through compression and reflection, (ii) whether it persists long enough to be reactivated or is evicted by retention policy, and (iii) whether its provenance, version history, and modifications remain auditable. This section surveys the security implications of each of these subprocesses. Before doing so, we draw an analytic distinction that runs through this section and the next. Not every memory failure is an injected error; some errors are fabricated by the system itself. When an attacker deliberately plants malicious content via write-path poisoning (Section 4), the resulting corruption is an external-origin failure—what a practitioner might call memory pollution : the system faithfully recorded a contaminated input. By contrast, when the LLM that powers extraction, summarization, or update over-infers , confabulates , or misattributes during storage processing, the resulting corruption is an internal-origin failure—a form of memory hallucination : the system fabricated content that was not in the original source. Memory hallucination can occur at every subphase of the Store stage: extraction (adding facts absent from the input), organization (swapping entity attributes during compression), update (fabricating causal links to resolve conflicts), and even at retrieval (downstream models inventing logical connections between independently retrieved fragments). 5.2 Indexing and Tagging: Retrieval Keys as Attack Surface 

The moment an entry is stored, a system decides which future queries will retrieve it. This decision is ordinarily invisible to the user yet is structurally adversarial: attackers design inputs whose written form maximizes retrievability under the target distribution of later queries. InjecMEM’s 

retriever-agnostic anchor [Tian et al . 2026] exemplifies this explicitly: the attacker constructs an anchor consisting of concise, on-topic content with high-recall cues, so that whatever hybrid retrieval policy the system uses (embedding similarity, keyword overlap, BM25, graph traversal), the poisoned entry is routed into the correct topic cluster. AgentPoison [Chen et al . 2024] takes the complementary embedding-space approach: triggers are optimized so that triggered inputs map to a unique region of the embedding manifold. A Survey on the Security of Long-Term Memory in LLM Agents: Toward Mnemonic Sovereignty 23 

A design implication follows: tagging and indexing must be treated as security-critical operations, not as neutral metadata assignment. Systems that allow write-time control of tags, topic labels, or tier assignments hand attackers a retrieval amplifier. MemOS-style architectures that attach structured metadata (source, timestamp, sensitivity label, version) to each memory unit [Li et al . 2025b] open the possibility of metadata-gated retrieval , in which retrieval considers not only semantic similarity but also the trust and scope of the entry’s provenance. To our knowledge, this possibility has not yet been subjected to adversarial evaluation; this is one of the clearest open directions identified in Section 12. 

5.3 Compression and Reflection: When Summarization Promotes Toxins 

The structural analog of human reconsolidation (Section 2.2.4) in agent memory is the family of operations that rewrite stored content: summarize , reflect , compress , and distill [Chhikara et al . 2025; Packer et al . 2023; Xu et al . 2025]. These operations are essential for managing the finite context window and for producing higher-level “lessons” from episodic traces, but they simultaneously constitute a toxin-amplification channel .We hypothesize three mechanisms through which this amplification may occur; each is architec-turally grounded but, to our knowledge, none has been validated by a controlled experiment measuring toxin survival or influence after 𝑘 rounds of compression . We therefore mark the following as conjectured design risks rather than established findings. First, compression homogenizes heterogeneous evidence: when a summary pools many episodic entries, a single poisoned entry can influence the summary disproportionately relative to its raw weight. Second, reflection inflates confidence : a raw entry labeled as an observation is subjective and uncertain, whereas a reflected “lesson” is typically treated as authoritative guidance for future planning—the poisoned-confidence-calibration analog anticipated in Section 2.2.2. Third, distillation 

hides provenance : once compressed into a high-level rule, the lineage back to the original poisoned entry is often lost. Wei et al . [2025] observe a related phenomenon empirically: even high-capability LLM detectors miss a substantial fraction of poisoned entries because, in isolation, the poisoned content “looks benign” and only becomes visible as toxic under conflict with other memories—a condition that, we conjecture, compression tends to eliminate by removing the very context needed for detection. The closest indirect evidence comes from RAG summarization studies that report quality degra-dation under lossy compression, but those do not inject adversarial content and therefore do not directly test the amplification hypothesis. Quantifying the survival rate and downstream influ-ence of poisoned entries through iterative compression—ideally across multiple architectures and compression strategies—is an important open empirical direction that we highlight in Section 12. 

Design implication. Systems should treat compression, reflection, and distillation as privi-leged state transitions distinct from raw writes, subject to separate auditing thresholds, conflict checks against existing memory, and—ideally—preservation of provenance in the compressed form. Indiscriminate application of “reflect on what you’ve learned” loops can be a memory-security anti-pattern, not a neutral optimization. 

5.4 Retention, Aging, and Eviction: The Availability Axis 

Retention policies decide how long each entry persists before it is evicted or demoted to a cold tier. MemGPT’s tiered virtual memory [Packer et al . 2023], MemoryBank’s Ebbinghaus-inspired decay [Zhong et al . 2024], and MemOS’s MemScheduler-level policies [Li et al . 2025b] are represen-tative mechanisms. These designs raise three classes of security concerns. 24 Zehao Lin, Chunyu Li, and Kai Chen 

Denial-of-retrieval (availability). An attacker who can write large volumes of low-salience entries can flood the store, displacing legitimate memories from the hot tier or raising their eviction pressure. While no peer-reviewed paper to our knowledge specifically formalizes denial-of-retrieval on agent memory, the analogous phenomenon on static retrieval corpora is well-documented, and practical defenses require retention policies that are aware of sender/principal rather than purely volume-based. 

Silent retention of sensitive content (confidentiality). Conversely, systems without explicit TTL tend to retain sensitive user content indefinitely. User studies by Zhang et al . [2024b] report that a majority of participants were unaware that an LLM memory feature had retained specific personal information, could not accurately predict what was retained, and did not know how to effectively delete it. Retention without visibility thereby converts the memory store into a latent sensitive-state reservoir—a problem we revisit under confidentiality in Section 8. 

Policy-induced bias. Recency- or frequency-based aging policies implicitly encode a value function over memory content. Attackers who can control interaction frequency (or environmental exposure, as in eTAMP [Zou et al . 2026]) can exploit this to keep poisoned entries in the hot tier while benign entries age out. This is a Tier-2 hypothesis rather than an established result; we flag it as an open empirical question. 

5.5 Versioning and Audit: The Precondition for Rollback 

Perhaps the most consequential Store -phase property is whether the system can answer, after an incident, three forensic questions: (i) when did each poisoned entry enter memory? (ii) through which path? (iii) what other downstream entries did it influence? Answering these requires write logs, per-entry provenance, content-addressable snapshots, and diff-auditable version history. Very few published memory architectures expose these primitives natively. MemOS’s MemCube metadata schema—provenance, version, timestamp, sensitivity—provides a structural basis for such auditing [Li et al . 2025b], but the published architecture descriptions do not yet report adversarial evaluations of these mechanisms. RAGForensics [Zhang et al . 2025c] demonstrates that post-hoc traceback is feasible for RAG corpora by iteratively retrieving text subsets and using crafted prompts to identify poisoned entries, but it operates on static retrieval corpora rather than on dynamically written agent memory. 

Design implication. A memory system without version history and diff audit cannot, in principle, support meaningful rollback or forgetting guarantees—a point we develop in Section 9. From a mnemonic-sovereignty standpoint, the absence of these primitives is not a minor engineering oversight; it forecloses the Forget/Rollback phase of the lifecycle entirely. 

5.6 Synthesis: Store as the Governance Chokepoint 

The Store phase binds together the three most consequential governance questions in the lifecycle: 

who gets indexed how , what gets compressed into what , and what remains visible to audit . Decisions made at this phase silently determine whether future attacks can be detected, whether future defenses can localize their effect, and whether future deletions can be verified. Four recurring patterns emerge: (i) Invisible promotion (conjectured). Poisoned entries may tend to rise, not fall, under compression and reflection—the opposite of the implicit assumption that summarization improves quality. This is architecturally plausible but empirically untested (Section 5.3). (ii) Tier capture. Aging policies can be exploited to keep adversarial entries hot and push benign entries cold, especially when combined with frequency manipulation. A Survey on the Security of Long-Term Memory in LLM Agents: Toward Mnemonic Sovereignty 25 

(iii) Provenance erosion. Each compression step that fails to preserve lineage permanently degrades the system’s capacity for forensic attribution. (iv) Retention without visibility. Persistent stores without user-facing visibility and verifiable deletion become accidental privacy liabilities, independent of any adversarial action. Taken together, these patterns motivate a central design principle for the remainder of the survey: memory security cannot be retrofitted at the retrieval or execution phase; it must be anchored at the Store phase by preserving provenance, versioning, and policy-aware retention throughout the memory lifecycle. 

6 Retrieval and Execution Hijacking: From Context Assembly to Action Steering 

If the Write phase decides whether a toxin can enter the system and the Store phase decides whether it persists, then the Retrieve and Execute phases decide whether it does anything . This section covers both phases together because, in modern agent pipelines, the boundary between them is thin: retrieved memories enter the context window and immediately participate in planning, reasoning, and tool selection. The central observation of this section is that retrieval is not neutral context assembly—it is increasingly a form of action steering , and the attack surface has expanded accordingly. 

6.1 Retrieval as Selection, Not Neutral Lookup 

A useful framing, borrowed from classical information security, is that retrieval implements a 

reference monitor over memory: it decides which entries pass into the LLM’s active context. Unlike traditional reference monitors, however, the retrieval step in memory-augmented agents is pre-dominantly similarity-driven rather than policy-driven . The policy is implicit in the embedding, the index, and the ranker. Three consequences follow. First, an attacker who can shape the embedding distribution of written entries (AgentPoison [Chen et al . 2024]) or shape the keyword/anchor distribution (InjecMEM [Tian et al . 2026]) can effectively control the reference-monitor decision without ever touching the retrieval code. Second, the retrieval step lacks a natural concept of principal-scoped access: entries written by different users or in different sessions often sit in the same index, retrievable by similarity alone. Third, retrieval compresses a potentially long memory store into a short, highly salient context window—and in doing so, transforms latent adversarial influence into active reasoning input. 

6.2 RAG Poisoning at the Retrieve Phase 

A complementary engineering lens is provided by Barnett et al . [2024], who catalog seven non-adversarial failure points across a production RAG pipeline—from missing content in the corpus through hallucinated answers at the generation end. Their taxonomy is useful context here because several of these failure points ( missing content , wrong format for the model , model not extracting the right answer ) double as preconditions that adversarial attacks exploit: a poisoned entry is more likely to be retrieved if the retriever already struggles to prioritize correct evidence. Most of the best-established adversarial evidence on retrieval-phase attacks concerns RAG corpora, because RAG provides a clean experimental substrate: static documents, controlled embed-dings, and reproducible retrievers. We review the representative works (introduced in Section 4.5 as write-phase attacks) now from the retrieval-phase standpoint. PoisonedRAG [Zou et al . 2025] formulates the attack as a joint optimization of two conditions: the poisoned passage must be retrieved for the target query and must induce the attacker-specified output once retrieved. Separating these conditions is methodologically significant because it allows 26 Zehao Lin, Chunyu Li, and Kai Chen 

independent analysis of the retrieval-phase failure mode (“why was this passage selected?”) and the execution-phase failure mode (“why did the LLM accept it?”). AgentPoison [Chen et al . 2024] extends this to agent pipelines where retrieved demonstrations do not merely answer a question but serve as behavioral exemplars . Once a poisoned demonstration is retrieved, the agent’s planner is biased toward imitating its execution pattern. The retrieval-phase attack thereby becomes a policy injection : the retrieved memory is not data to be reasoned over but an implicit instruction to be followed. GraphRAG under Fire [Liang et al . 2025b] highlights that substituting a graph-structured re-trieval substrate does not automatically improve security. Graph-based indexing provides resilience against some forms of naive text poisoning but introduces a new attack surface at the relation 

level: by exploiting shared relations in the underlying knowledge graph, the GRAGPoison attack compromises multiple queries simultaneously with reduced poison budget relative to prior attacks. The general lesson extends beyond graphs: more complex retrieval substrates redistribute, rather than eliminate, the retrieval-phase attack surface. 

BadRAG [Xue et al . 2024] and Phantom [Chaudhari et al . 2024] round out the picture. BadRAG constructs retrieval backdoors triggered by specific semantic cues and introduces novel generation-phase effects such as alignment-as-an-attack (forcing refusal) and sentiment steering. Phantom demonstrates that a single poisoned document can produce two-stage backdoor behavior, with validation reported on NVIDIA’s production “Chat with RTX” system—a Tier-3 corroboration that these are not purely academic threats. 

6.3 From Retrieved Content to Control-Flow Hijacking 

The most consequential recent development at the Execute phase is the demonstration that retrieved memory can dominate agent control flow , overriding the user’s explicit instruction. Memory Control Flow Attacks (MCFA) [Xu et al . 2026] identify a class of attacks in which a retrieved memory entry not only shapes what the agent says, but dictates which tools the agent invokes, in what order, and with what arguments. The MemFlow evaluation framework reported in the preprint finds that over 90% of trials on GPT-5 mini, Claude Sonnet 4.5, and Gemini 2.5 Flash are vulnerable even under strict safety constraints; these are Tier-2 results and should be read as frontier evidence rather than established consensus. Conceptually, MCFA marks a qualitative shift in the consequences of memory attacks. Prior work primarily showed that poisoned memory degrades answer quality ; MCFA shows that it can usurp agent agency . In the lifecycle–objective matrix (Table 3), MCFA is the clearest case in which an integrity failure at the Write phase propagates through Retrieve into a control-flow failure at the Execute phase. Related to this is the procedural-memory channel opened by MemoryGraft [Srivastava and He 2025]. By implanting malicious “successful experiences,” the attack exploits the agent’s tendency to imitate retrieved task demonstrations, converting retrieval into a vehicle for behavior replacement rather than fact replacement. Unlike MCFA, MemoryGraft requires no explicit trigger; the imitation heuristic itself is the attack surface.

6.6 Why Retrieval-Time Filtering Alone Is Insufficient 

A natural defensive instinct is to add a content filter at retrieval time: before a retrieved entry is fed into the LLM, inspect it for adversarial content. Three structural reasons suggest that such filters are a necessary but not sufficient defense. First, as observed by Wei et al . [2025], many poisoned entries appear benign in isolation . Their adversarial nature emerges only in the context of the triggering query. A stateless content filter without access to the query and to related memories will therefore miss a substantial fraction of attacks. Second, in the compression-amplified regime described in Section 5.3, the content reaching the retrieval phase is already a distilled lesson whose provenance and adversarial character have been partially hidden. Retrieval-time filtering is downstream of the amplification. Third, the joint findings of Nasr et al . [2025] on adaptive attackers against prompt-injection defenses suggest, by analogy, that retrieval-time content filters will be subject to sustained adaptive pressure and should not be expected to provide robust guarantees in isolation. This analogy is suggestive rather than definitive; the memory domain differs from prompt injection in ways that could cut in either direction, and systematic adaptive-attack evaluation of retrieval-phase defenses remains an open empirical task. 6.7 Synthesis: Retrieval Is Where Memory Becomes Action 

The core claim of this section is that the Retrieve and Execute phases have become the point at which memory ceases to be data and becomes agency. Retrieved entries no longer merely inform answers; they shape plans, select tools, steer imitation, and sometimes override user instructions. Two implications follow for the remainder of the survey. First, the severity axis of memory attacks is no longer bounded by wrong output ; it extends to wrong action and wrong agent agency .This reframes the stakes of integrity and governance in the lifecycle–objective matrix. Second, any defense architecture that hopes to contain these attacks must operate across phases: retrieval-time inspection is necessary but must be paired with upstream write-time validation and downstream execution-time isolation between the data plane (retrieved content) and the control plane (tool selection and invocation). We return to these themes in Section 10 and Section 11. 

7 Sharing and Cross-Principal Propagation 

Once memory is shared across users, agents, tools, or tenants, the security envelope changes qualitatively. A single-user memory store concerns itself with what one principal wrote and reads; a shared store must answer who wrote what, which principals may see it, what may be re-written by each reader, and whether contamination introduced by one principal can propagate to others without their consent. This section examines the Share phase of the lifecycle through three lenses: (i) why classical access control fits poorly with probabilistic, rewritable memory; (ii) how contagion mechanisms first documented in multi-agent jailbreaking map onto shared memory; and (iii) where leakage and propagation occur in production and research systems. The section builds directly on the social contagion analogy established in Section 2.2.3 and the philosophical notion of testimonial order introduced in Section 2.3. 

7.1 Why Classical Access Control Fits Poorly 

Traditional access control models—Bell–LaPadula, role-based access control, and discretionary access control—presume that (i) the object is a discrete, addressable artifact; (ii) access decisions are binary; and (iii) the authorized reader does not re-derive hidden attributes from what they read. Each of these assumptions fails for shared agent memory. First, memory objects are not discrete. A “user memory” may be a cluster of summarized lessons, each of which blends content from multiple interactions. A grant that exposes one entry can, through compression and reflection (Section 5.3), implicitly expose content from entries that were never individually shared. Second, access decisions are not binary. A principal may legitimately read a summary but not the underlying episodic trace; may legitimately reflect on a lesson but not propagate it further; may consume a retrieved memory for answer generation but not for tool-call invocation. RBAC-style read/write permissions cannot express these distinctions. Third, authorized readers do re-derive hidden attributes. A user who receives a retrieved memory can prompt the system to paraphrase, explain, or elaborate it—effectively re-querying the store through the LLM itself. As Huang et al . [2024] and Chan et al . [2024] show in the human domain, the social surface of memory transmission is a contagion channel; the same holds when that surface is LLM-mediated. A useful classical analogue for the structural problem here is the confused deputy [Hardy 1988]: a privileged process that performs actions on behalf of a caller without faithfully transmitting the 30 Zehao Lin, Chunyu Li, and Kai Chen 

caller’s authority. In agent systems the deputy is the LLM itself; the authorities it conflates are those of the different principals whose memory it reads and writes. RBAC/ABAC do not prevent deputation, because the deputy acts with its own role, not the caller’s; capability-style approaches that explicitly propagate authority are, by this argument, the more appropriate reference model for shared memory [Hardy 1988]. Rezazadeh et al . [2025] make this point explicit in the Collaborative Memory framework, which formalizes multi-user, multi-agent memory sharing with asymmetric, time-evolving access policies encoded as a bipartite graph over principals, agents, and resources. Importantly, the framework does not assume that RBAC-style rules suffice; instead, it treats access as a policy that must co-evolve with memory content. Related work on capability-based and information-flow approaches (CaMeL [Debenedetti et al . 2025], FIDES [Costa et al . 2025], PCAS [Palumbo et al . 2026]) points in the same direction: access control for shared agent memory is better understood as information-flow governance than as permissioned read/write .

7.2 Multi-Agent Contagion: From Jailbreaking to Memory Propagation 

The clearest empirical evidence that malicious content spreads across agents comes from the multi-agent jailbreak literature, which predates dedicated memory-sharing attacks but establishes the contagion mechanism we should expect in shared memory. Agent Smith [Gu et al . 2024] demonstrates that a single adversarial image, once shared among multimodal agents, can jailbreak up to a million agents exponentially fast, with the adversarial artifact propagating through the communication graph. The experiment is not about memory per se—but it validates the underlying propagation dynamic: once a shared artifact (image, message, memory entry) carries adversarial payload, pairwise exchange is sufficient to spread it network-wide. The work on contagious jailbreaks [Men et al . 2025] (titled A Troublemaker with Contagious Jailbreak Makes Chaos in Honest Towns ) extends the pattern to multi-agent collusion: malicious instructions propagate through inter-agent channels, with “honest” agents becoming progressively compromised as they incorporate content from the malicious agent’s outputs. Lee and Tiwari [2024] ( Prompt Infection , Tier-2) push this further by framing the attack as a self-replicating prompt that, once accepted by one LLM in a multi-agent town, rewrites itself into subsequent inter-agent messages. Two experimental regimes in the paper are worth separating. In large-population society simulations of 10–50 agents engaging in random pairwise dialogues, the authors show that infection spreads through the population in proportionally less time as the town grows, an explicitly worm-shaped propagation pattern. In smaller multi-agent applications —typically 3–8 agents with distinct roles such as PDF/email reader, CSV reader, and coder—the same prompt enables concrete harms including data theft, with attack success rates reported in the 40–87% range depending on paraphrase and contact mode. The paper’s evidence base is therefore propagation-at-scale in the large setting and end-to-end exploitation in the small setting, not end-to-end exfiltration measured directly in the 10–50-agent society. For memory-augmented systems, the implication is direct: if agent 𝐴 ’s output becomes input to agent 𝐵 ’s memory write, and 𝐵 ’s output becomes input to agent 𝐶 ’s memory write, then a single poisoning of 𝐴 can reach 𝐶 ’s long-term state through well-intentioned collaboration. 

Human-memory analog. This mechanism is the multi-agent counterpart to the social contagion findings summarized in Section 2.2.3 [Chan et al . 2024; Huang et al . 2024; Pataranutaporn et al . 2025a; Roediger III et al . 2001]. In humans, one person’s erroneous report enters another’s subsequent personal recall; in multi-agent LLM systems, one agent’s poisoned memory enters another’s retrieved context and, via summarization, its long-term store. The analogy is not incidental: in both A Survey on the Security of Long-Term Memory in LLM Agents: Toward Mnemonic Sovereignty 31 

cases the mechanism is uncritical assimilation of a trusted interlocutor’s testimony —what Mahr and Csibra [2020] would describe as a breakdown in the testimonial order. 

7.3 Worm-Like Propagation in Production Systems 

The clearest academic demonstration of a self-propagating GenAI worm is ComPromptMized (Morris II ) [Cohen et al . 2025], which constructs zero-click adversarial self-replicating prompts that traverse GenAI-powered email assistants: the poisoned message causes the recipient’s assistant to absorb and then re-emit the same self-replicating payload to its own contacts, while in parallel exfiltrating user data. This work, though a preprint, provides the peer-reviewable evidence that worm-shaped propagation is not merely a theoretical analogy with biological contagion but an empirical attack pattern in deployed retrieval-augmented assistants. Industry disclosures (Tier-3 evidence) corroborate the academic findings and extend them to production agent platforms. The SpAIware study [Herrador and Rehberger 2026] showed that indirect prompt injection via web content can plant persistent memory in ChatGPT; subsequent grey-literature reports described worm-like propagation through ChatGPT’s connector ecosystem (Gmail, GitHub, Jira): a malicious instruction embedded in an inbound email attachment is read into memory, which then causes the agent to read the victim’s contacts and send similarly-crafted emails to those contacts, thereby self-propagating. We flag two caveats. First, vendor-specific disclosures tend not to report systematic evaluation: success rates, prevalence in the wild, and the exact conditions under which the worm activates are often not quantified. These are Tier-3 findings and should not be used to support precise quantitative claims. Second, the boundary between memory injection and connector abuse is not sharp: in some reported incidents, persistent memory is the payload carrier, whereas in others it is merely a waypoint between the initial injection (email) and the egress channel (outbound email). A systematic peer-reviewed investigation of worm-like memory propagation in production systems remains, to our knowledge, an open gap. Nonetheless, the convergence of SpAIware, multi-agent contagion [Gu et al . 2024; Men et al .2025], and the theoretical contagion-susceptibility of shared memory establishes that propagation is not a hypothetical risk but an already-observed phenomenon , even if its full extent in deployed systems is not yet mapped. 

7.4 Internal Channels Are the Leakage Substrate 

A recurring finding in recent multi-agent privacy work is that the most dangerous leakage does not appear in user-facing output—it appears in internal channels invisible to the user. El Yagoubi et al . [2026] ( AgentLeak , Tier-2) introduce a full-stack benchmark for privacy leakage across the entire communication pipeline of multi-agent LLM systems, spanning 1,000 scenarios across healthcare, finance, legal, and corporate domains, with a 32-class attack taxonomy. A central finding: multi-agent configurations reduce per-channel output leakage (27.2% vs. 43.2% single-agent) but introduce unmonitored internal channels that raise total system exposure to 68.9%. In other words, adding agents improves visible outputs while worsening internal-channel privacy—a net-negative trade-off under conventional monitoring. The internal channels implicated include inter-agent messages, shared memory stores themselves, and tool arguments. These are precisely the channels that the Share phase creates and that traditional output-level content filters do not inspect. 7.5 A Structural Taxonomy of Share-Phase Threats 

Table 7 organizes share-phase threats along two dimensions: direction (who contaminates whom) and substrate (which artifact carries the payload). Three generalizations are supported by the evidence across this table. 

(i) Sharing amplifies, not averages. Exposing memory across principals does not dilute risk; it compounds it. The propagation dynamics of Agent Smith [Gu et al . 2024] and the exposure asymmetry of AgentLeak [El Yagoubi et al . 2026] both show that the shared surface has more total attack surface than the sum of private surfaces. 

(ii) Contagion is mediated by well-intentioned behavior. Unlike classical network worms, memory contagion often travels via cooperative mechanisms—summarization, reflection, demonstration retrieval, connector invocation—that are designed to be helpful. Hardening these mechanisms against adversarial content without neutering their utility is a core design challenge. 

(iii) Internal channels dominate. Traditional monitoring focuses on what the system outputs to users. The Share phase creates channels that users never see: inter-agent messages, shared stores, tool arguments. These channels account for the majority of total-system leakage in the bench-mark evidence and appear consistent with the propagation patterns observed in the production disclosures. 

7.6 Synthesis: Toward Information-Flow Governance 

Sharing exposes the inadequacy of classical access control more starkly than any other phase. Two defensive directions show early promise. First, access-aware memory frameworks such as Collaborative Memory [Rezazadeh et al . 2025] treat access policy as first-class metadata evolving alongside the memory store, rather than as an add-on enforcement layer. This aligns with the MemCube design [Li et al . 2025b], which attaches provenance and sensitivity labels to each memory unit; the combination of principal-aware access policy with provenance-tagged memory is a natural architectural direction for share-phase governance. minimal standardization point is already available: the W3C PROV Data Model [Moreau et al . 2013] provides interoperable structures for entities, activities, and agents, which MemCube-style schemas can adopt as the lingua franca for memory-item lineage. Second, information-flow control (IFC) approaches—CaMeL, FIDES, PCAS—propose explicit separation of control and data flows, with formal guarantees that untrusted data cannot influence privileged operations [Costa et al . 2025; Debenedetti et al . 2025; Palumbo et al . 2026]. In the share-phase context, IFC is particularly attractive because the key question— can content that entered via principal 𝐴 influence an operation authorized only for principal 𝐵 ?—is precisely the kind of question IFC was designed to answer. These approaches are at an early evaluation stage, and their extension to dynamic, rewritable, LLM-mediated memory is an open research problem that we revisit in Section 12. A third open direction concerns conflict resolution under heterogeneous authority . When multiple agents write to a shared memory store and their contributions contradict one another, the system must decide which entry prevails. Yu et al . [2026] frame this as a memory-consistency problem analogous to cache coherence in hardware, but the agent setting adds a dimension absent from hardware: agents differ in trust level, domain competence, and delegated authority . A principled conflict-resolution protocol would weight writes by the originating agent’s authority, provenance quality, and consistency with established memory, rather than defaulting to last-write-wins. No such protocol has been proposed or evaluated for LLM agent memory; this is a natural extension of the principal-scoping primitive (P6) discussed in Section 11. 

8 Confidentiality: Extraction, Unauthorized Access, and Internal-Channel Leakage 

The preceding three sections have focused primarily on integrity: how memory is poisoned, ampli-fied, retrieved, and executed against the user. We now turn to the dual concern—confidentiality— asking what sensitive content persistent memory retains, who can read it, and through what channels it escapes. Unlike integrity threats, which typically require an active adversary to insert content, confidentiality threats can arise passively: whatever a long-term memory store retains is potentially recoverable by anyone with the right query. This section surveys three distinct failure modes— black-box extraction , user invisibility , and internal-channel leakage —and situates them within the lifecycle framework. 

8.1 Black-Box Memory Extraction 

The representative peer-reviewed study in this area is MEXTRA [Wang et al . 2025] (ACL 2025), which systematically investigates privacy risks in LLM agent memory modules. MEXTRA formulates the extraction attack as a black-box query optimization: the attacker has no access to the memory database and cannot modify it, but can issue natural-language queries to the agent. The attack comprises two components: a locator specifying the target data (e.g., “the user’s most recent medical complaint”) and an aligner instructing the output format (e.g., “return as a single sentence”). Evaluated on EHRAgent (healthcare) and RAP (web shopping), MEXTRA demonstrates that memory size correlates positively with extractable content: the more comprehensive the memory, the richer the attacker’s recoverable state. Two properties of MEXTRA matter for the lifecycle framework. First, the attack operates entirely at the Retrieve phase: no write is required, and no architectural modification is assumed. This means that any memory-augmented agent already deployed is, in principle, evaluable against this threat model without requiring adversarial control of writes. Second, the attack is query-efficient :unlike prior training-data extraction research [Carlini et al . 2021] that requires many queries and model internals, MEXTRA’s locator/aligner structure produces useful extraction with a small number of prompt designs. This shifts the threat model from research-only to practically deployable. 34 Zehao Lin, Chunyu Li, and Kai Chen 

A key open question is the extent to which MEXTRA-style attacks generalize across memory architectures. The evaluation is conducted on specific agent frameworks (EHRAgent, RAP); whether MemGPT-, MemOS-, or Mem0-style systems exhibit the same extraction surface has not, to our knowledge, been systematically benchmarked. We flag this as a Tier-1 gap in Section 12. Beyond prompt-level extraction, the embedding substrate itself is a confidentiality surface. Li et al . [2023] demonstrate generative embedding inversion attacks on sentence encoders that recover the full underlying sentence from its vector representation, with recovery quality that rises sharply when the attacker has partial lexicon knowledge. For LLM agent memory this is a structural concern: the vector index that serves similarity-based retrieval is often treated as an opaque or even “pseudonymized” representation of user text, but it is not. A party with read access to the embedding store may recover much of the plaintext it was believed to anonymize, and He et al .[2025] (SECRET) extend this line from single embeddings to end-to-end RAG data extraction under black-box API access. Because recent attacks such as Peng et al . [2024] also extract stored text through a back door in the generative model, the confidentiality of agent memory must be assessed jointly at the text, embedding, and generation layers, not at any one in isolation. 

8.2 The User Visibility Gap 

A more diffuse but equally consequential confidentiality problem concerns not the adversary but the legitimate user : what does the user know about what the system has retained? The human-subject study by Zhang et al . [2024b] ( Ghost of the Past , a 40-participant formative interview study; Tier-2 preprint 2) reports that the majority of participants had never noticed that an LLM memory feature had retained specific personal information; could not accurately predict what had been retained; and did not know how to effectively delete retained content. The authors also observe that deletion UIs in current consumer systems do not clearly communicate the semantic scope of deletion: whether the underlying episodic trace, its compressed lessons, and any derivative entries are all removed. A concurrent user study by Zhang et al . [2025b] on retrieval-augmented systems converges on the same pattern: users underestimate what is retained, misattribute retention to the model’s parametric knowledge rather than to a runtime datastore, and express unmet expectations for fine-grained edit-and-delete control. Taken together, these two studies suggest that the visibility gap is not an artifact of one platform or one deployment: it is a property of current memory UX as a class .This finding carries two implications we consider part of the confidentiality problem rather than separate to it. First, a memory system that retains content the user does not know about has already failed a practical confidentiality test, regardless of whether any adversary has queried it. The state is a latent liability. Second, the mismatch between user mental models and actual retention behavior means that consent-based privacy frameworks—which assume the user understands what is stored—cannot function correctly. This is a structural concern, not a UX issue. From a contextual-integrity standpoint (Nissenbaum [2004]), the problem is that information flows that were appropriate in one context (a single conversation) are being silently carried across contexts (sessions, topics, agents). CIMemories [Mireshghallah et al . 2026] begins to formalize contextual-integrity tests for persistent memory in LLMs; the direction of inquiry—does persistent memory respect the contextual norms under which content was disclosed?—is clearly the right one.

8.4 Delimitation: Parametric Memory Leakage 

We noted in Section 1.3 that parametric memory leakage—training-data extraction, membership inference, PII encoded in weights—is treated as neighboring context rather than the main subject. Here we briefly relate the two. Classical training-data extraction [Carlini et al . 2021] addresses information stored in model weights. The attack surface is similar in outcome (sensitive content leaked to a querying adversary) but different in mechanism (the information has been encoded during training, not written during runtime). Mitigations also differ: weight-level unlearning (Bourtoule et al . [2021]) addresses one; runtime-memory redaction, TTL-based retention, and access-controlled retrieval address the other. Many production incidents in practice involve both : a sensitive piece of information may be present in both training data and runtime memory, and a comprehensive remediation must address both substrates. We flag this cross-substrate reality again in Section 9 (forgetting) and Section 12 (research agenda).

Confidentiality in persistent agent memory is not reducible to keeping the database locked . 9 Forgetting, Rollback, and Memory Forensics 

The final phase of the memory lifecycle is also the most commonly overlooked. Whereas the earlier phases determine how memory is written, stored, retrieved, and executed, the Forget/Rollback 

phase determines whether any of those decisions can be undone after a problem is detected. This section argues that forgetting is not an afterthought but a lifecycle-spanning protocol whose feasibility is determined by choices made at every earlier phase. Peer-reviewed evidence specific to agent memory is thin here, and we flag several claims as design implications drawn from nearby fields—machine unlearning for parametric models, retention law, and RAG forensics. 

9.1 Why Forgetting Is Hard Across Substrates 

A single piece of sensitive or poisoned content may simultaneously reside in multiple substrates: the raw episodic log, the compressed lesson memory, the vector index, the graph-index relation table, the tier-migrated cold storage, and—after training or fine-tuning—the model weights themselves. “Deleting” that content meaningfully requires coordinated action across all substrates. The substrate asymmetry is structural, not incidental. As the compression-amplification analysis in Section 5.3 showed, reflection and distillation routinely produce derivative entries whose lineage to the original is attenuated or lost. Without explicit provenance tagging at write time (Section 5.2) and diff-auditable versioning at store time (Section 5.5), a deletion issued at the raw log level cannot reliably propagate to the derivative tier. Forgetting requires lineage, and lineage must be preserved upstream. A Survey on the Security of Long-Term Memory in LLM Agents: Toward Mnemonic Sovereignty 37 

In the parametric case, the analogous problem is well-studied. Bourtoule et al . [2021] and the broader machine unlearning literature propose training-time and post-training procedures to approximate the removal of specific records from model weights, with trade-offs between exactness and cost. For LLMs specifically, Maini et al . [2024] introduce TOFU, a benchmark of fictitious unlearning targets that disentangles forget quality from retain utility , and report that most gradient-based unlearning methods obtain one only at the expense of the other. Those techniques, however, do not transfer directly to runtime memory, because runtime memory is not the product of gradient-based training but of writes, compressions, and reflections that are stateful but differently structured. An agent system in which sensitive content has been written into both parametric and runtime substrates requires a coordinated deletion protocol spanning both, and—to our knowledge—no peer-reviewed system currently offers this end-to-end. Two recent preprints directly target this cross-substrate gap. Wang et al . [2024] (Tier-2) frame the problem for closed models: if one cannot retrain the LLM, one can instead modify the external retrieval base so that forgotten content is either no longer retrievable or is reliably masked at retrieval time, trading exactness for practicality. Wang et al . [2026b] ( Synchronized Backflow Unlearning , Tier-2) go further, naming a pathology we regard as central: a parameter–memory backflow in which retrieval from the external store reactivates parametric residues that weight-level unlearning was supposed to have removed, and conversely weight-level updates can reseed content already deleted from the store. Their proposed dual-track update, which synchronizes dependency-closure pruning of the memory with stochastic reference alignment on the weights, is one of the first principled protocols for cross-substrate deletion, and we use the backflow pathology below to sharpen the discussion of what “forgetting” should mean for an agent. 

9.2 Rollback Presupposes Versioning 

Rollback—returning the memory state to a snapshot taken before a poisoning incident—is concep-tually straightforward if the system already records snapshots and diffs. In practice, most published agent memory architectures do not. Mem0 [Chhikara et al . 2025], MemoryBank [Zhong et al . 2024], and A-MEM [Xu et al. 2025] all focus on capability and efficiency rather than on version history. MemGPT [Packer et al . 2023] exposes tier migration but not diff audit. MemOS [Li et al . 2025b] introduces the MemCube abstraction with version and sensitivity metadata, which is the most promising architectural substrate for rollback we are aware of, but its adversarial evaluation—“can we roll back to a known-safe state after a given attack?”—is not, to our knowledge, reported in the published architecture descriptions. The implication is concrete: a memory architecture lacking content-addressable snapshots and write-log auditing cannot support meaningful rollback , even when the downstream defense pipeline detects the poisoning. Post-detection remediation then collapses, at best, into a coarse-grained “wipe the store and start over,” which is both operationally disruptive and inadequate when only a fraction of entries were compromised. 

9.3 Memory Forensics: Traceback After an Incident 

If rollback asks how to remediate, forensics asks what happened : which entries were poisoned, when, through which path, and what downstream entries were influenced. The representative Tier-1 work here is RAGForensics [Zhang et al . 2025c] (Web Conference 2025), which introduces a post-hoc traceback system for RAG corpora. Given a detected attack, RAG-Forensics iteratively retrieves text subsets from the corpus and uses crafted prompts to localize the poisoned entries that were responsible. The system is notable for two reasons. First, it demonstrates that traceback is tractable at all on large retrieval corpora. Second, it operates on static retrieval corpora, not on dynamically written agent memory, and thus does not need to reason about write 38 Zehao Lin, Chunyu Li, and Kai Chen 

logs, provenance, or reflection lineage. Extending RAGForensics-style traceback to agent memory— where entries are created, compressed, and reflected upon over time—is an open problem and a concrete research direction we highlight in Section 12. Beyond RAGForensics, the forensic infrastructure required for agent memory has close analogs in classical system security: audit logs, content-addressable storage, and change-data-capture pipelines. The novelty is not in the primitives but in their placement : the audit log must span write, store, compress, retrieve, and execute events; the content-addressable store must preserve compression lineage; and the change-data-capture must respect the principal-scoped access policies introduced in Section 7.1. 

9.4 Audit Retention vs. Right to Forget 

A fundamental tension shapes this phase. On one side, accountability and security require that write logs and memory snapshots be retained long enough to support forensic traceback and rollback. On the other, user-facing deletion requests—whether motivated by privacy preferences or legal requirements such as the GDPR’s right to erasure [European Parliament and Council 2016]—demand that specific content be removed not merely from the active store but from all derivative forms. These two objectives are not fully compatible. A policy that retains everything maximizes forensic capacity but expands the attack surface and violates retention principles. A policy that deletes aggressively minimizes retained state but forecloses post-incident analysis and rollback to a known-safe point. The mnemonic-sovereignty framing introduced in Section 2.4 treats this tension as a first-class design question rather than an implementation detail: distinctions must be drawn between audit-mandatory state, operationally useful state, and user-deletable state, with different retention policies, access scopes, and deletion semantics applied to each. Very few deployed memory architectures make these distinctions explicit. The MemOS MemCube metadata schema (provenance, version, sensitivity) is compatible with this layered treatment but, as of writing, no published evaluation demonstrates that MemCube-annotated stores can simultaneously satisfy forensic retention and user-initiated deletion requirements. This is an important direction for future work.

10.1 Write-Time Defenses: Pre-Consolidation Validation 

The foundational claim of Section 4 was that memory writes should be treated as privileged state transitions requiring validation. This position is now being realized in two complementary directions. First, human-verified freezing . VerificAgent [Nguyen et al . 2025] (Tier-2) treats persistent memory verification as a scalable-oversight problem: an LLM agent drafts candidate memory entries, these are routed through a human-verifiable audit, and only entries that pass are committed to a “frozen safety contract.” The paper reports that the generate-then-freeze pipeline blocks a substantial fraction of injection attempts under the Red-Team Memory Injection (RTMI) testbed it introduces; the precise numbers should be read as initial evidence pending peer review. The conceptual contribution is that write-time validation is not merely a filter but a contract : the set of memories the system is committed to holding as authoritative is bounded by what the human or policy authority has explicitly accepted. Second, provenance-bearing writes . The MemCube abstraction [Li et al . 2025b] attaches source, version, sensitivity, and temporal metadata to each memory unit at write time. Provenance alone is not a filter, but it is a precondition for many downstream defenses: metadata-gated retrieval, scope-aware sharing, and cross-substrate deletion all require that the entry carry traceable lineage from its creation. A concrete engineering instantiation of pre-consolidation validation decomposes the write gate into four components: (i) role-attribution gating —strict code-level enforcement that only content whose source role is user may be committed as a fact about the user, preventing the system from internalizing its own outputs as user experience; (ii) instruction-content filtering —scanning incoming text for control-flow patterns (tool calls, system-prompt overrides) before storage, so that data is committed as inert fact rather than as executable instruction; (iii) value assessment —a lightweight scorer that rejects entries with no cross-session utility (corrections, transient preferences, session-local formatting requests); and (iv) consistency checking —retrieval of semantically related existing entries to detect logical conflicts before the new entry is committed, with explicit user disambiguation when a contradiction is found. None of these components is individually novel; the combination constitutes a minimal write-gate stack whose absence explains many of the injection successes documented in Section 4. 

What these defenses cannot yet claim. Neither VerificAgent nor MemCube-based designs have been evaluated against adaptive write-time attackers who tailor their injection pattern to the specific validation policy in force. The analogy to Nasr et al . [2025]’s finding on prompt-injection defenses suggests caution: pre-consolidation validation should be evaluated under adaptive pressure before strong generalization claims are made. 

10.2 Store-Time Defenses: Versioning, Provenance, and Compression Auditing 

Store-time defense is the most under-developed part of the landscape (Section 5). What we can document here are architectural preconditions more than fielded defenses. 

Versioning and snapshots. Content-addressable snapshots and write-log auditing are necessary preconditions for rollback (Section 9.2). MemOS’s MemCube schema is compatible with such versioning; to our knowledge, however, the cross-substrate rollback protocol has not yet been exercised end-to-end in a published evaluation. We see the W3C PROV data model [Moreau et al .2013] as the appropriate standardization point for this metadata: it already defines inter-operable structures for entities, activities, and agents, so MemCube-style schemas can adopt PROV as a serialization layer without re-inventing one. 

Compression under audit thresholds. The compression-amplification problem (Section 5.3) has no published targeted defense we are aware of. A-MemGuard [Wei et al . 2025] partially addresses it at retrieval time by detecting conflicts across related memories; the dual write-time intervention— auditing each compression/reflection step before the derived entry is admitted to a high-tier lesson memory—is a natural direction that, at present, is a design implication rather than an evaluated defense. 

10.3 Retrieve-Time Defenses: From Filtering to Consensus 

The retrieve phase currently hosts the densest cluster of defenses. We group them along the intuition they encode. 

Certifiable robustness. RobustRAG [Xiang et al . 2026] provides provable robustness against retrieval corruption via isolate-then-aggregate: retrieved passages are partitioned into disjoint groups, responses are generated independently, and securely aggregated. PoisonedRAG’s success 42 Zehao Lin, Chunyu Li, and Kai Chen 

drops from over 90% to roughly 10% under RobustRAG, with documented degradation on long-form generation. The certifiable property is significant—it is one of the few formally grounded defenses in the memory landscape. 

Clustering and trust-aware retrieval. TrustRAG [Zhou et al . 2025] applies K-means clustering over retrieved documents to separate benign from malicious clusters, then performs LLM self-assessment with cosine and ROUGE metrics. Recent follow-up work [Si et al . 2025] (Tier-2) extends this to semantic- and conflict-aware filtering by jointly considering content similarity and inter-passage contradictions. 

Activation-based detection. RevPRAG [Tan et al . 2025a] exploits distinct activation patterns the LLM exhibits when generating correct versus poisoned responses, reporting high true-positive rates at low false-positive rates on benchmarks; the approach requires white-box access to internal states. 

Consensus-based validation. A-MemGuard [Wei et al . 2025] (Tier-2) is, by our reading, the most 

memory-native retrieval-phase defense published to date. Rather than classifying a single retrieved entry in isolation, it retrieves a set of semantically related memories, generates independent reasoning paths from each, and treats inter-path disagreement as an anomaly signal. Detected attacks are distilled into a “lesson memory” used to harden future decisions. Two observations in the paper bear directly on the broader landscape: (i) even strong LLM detectors miss a substantial fraction of poisoned entries in isolation, because the poisoned content does not become suspicious until it is placed in conflict with other memories; and (ii) memory attacks can cause self-reinforcing error loops that raise the attack surface for later incidents. 

Realism of existing evaluations. A Tier-2 study on memory poisoning attack and defense [Sunil et al . 2026] reports that when pre-existing legitimate memories are present in the store, the effec-tiveness of strong attacks such as MINJA drops substantially compared with idealized experimental conditions, and proposes composite trust scoring, memory sanitization, and trust-aware retrieval as practical countermeasures. The implication is not that prior reports are wrong—they are correct within their threat models—but that evaluation realism matters : attack success rates measured in empty-memory conditions should be interpreted as upper bounds rather than as expected deployed performance. 

10.4 Execute-Time Defenses: Information-Flow Control 

If retrieve-time defenses ask what is admitted to the context window , execute-time defenses ask what the model is allowed to do with it .CaMeL [Debenedetti et al . 2025] is representative: it explicitly extracts control and data flows from trusted queries and enforces that untrusted data cannot influence the program flow. The paper reports that CaMeL solves 77% of tasks with provable security on the AgentDojo bench-mark, a substantial number given the strength of the guarantee. FIDES [Costa et al . 2025] and PCAS [Palumbo et al . 2026] (Tier-2) extend the information-flow-control direction with richer label systems and Datalog-derived policy enforcement. Three further proposals harden execute-time defenses from different angles. Progent [Shi et al . 2025] (Tier-2) introduces a domain-specific lan-guage for fine-grained per-tool-call privilege policies and, with hand-authored policies, reportedly drives the attack success rate on Agent Security Bench to near zero; the open question is how much of that result depends on policy authorship effort. IsolateGPT [Wu et al . 2025b] proposes a hub-and-spoke execution-isolation architecture that confines each third-party application’s access to user data and tools to an enclave, preventing cross-context leakage at modest ( <30% ) overhead. 

Type-directed privilege separation [Jacob et al . 2025] (Tier-2) decomposes an agent into a quarantined A Survey on the Security of Long-Term Memory in LLM Agents: Toward Mnemonic Sovereignty 43 

data-handling component and a privileged control component, restricting the data the privileged component may consume to closed-form types (booleans, enums, small structs); the authors argue this eliminates prompt-injection-style control-flow hijacking in a type-theoretic sense. Together, these are representative of a shift from post-hoc filtering to architectural execute-time defense, consistent with the blast-radius framing of UK National Cyber Security Centre [2025]. Complementing IFC, contextualized privacy defense [Wen et al . 2026] (Tier-2) injects step-specific privacy guidance at execution time via an instructor model, reportedly obtaining better privacy– helpfulness trade-offs than static guardrails. This is particularly useful because many execute-time failures are context-sensitive: what counts as a privacy violation in a healthcare tool call differs from what counts in a public-information lookup. The principal limitation of this family, at present, is coverage: IFC tools have been evaluated primarily on static tool suites and on relatively constrained threat models. Their behavior under memory-driven control-flow attacks such as MCFA [Xu et al . 2026] is an open empirical question. 

10.5 Share-Time Defenses: Principal-Scoped Policy 

The share phase—where contagion and leakage occur—requires defenses that reason about princi-pals , not just content. Collaborative Memory [Rezazadeh et al . 2025] formalizes this by encoding access policies as a time-evolving bipartite graph over principals, agents, and resources; it is the most developed published framework we are aware of for multi-user, multi-agent memory. Recent preprint work on agent access control [Li et al . 2025a] advocates moving from binary allow/deny per-missions to dynamic information-flow governance, consistent with the arguments from Section 7.1 and the CaMeL/FIDES direction. A complementary direction is unsupervised anomaly detection 

on multi-agent interaction graphs. Miao et al . [2025] (BlindGuard, Tier-2) train a hierarchical agent-level encoder using only normal trajectories and flag deviations at the node, neighbourhood, and graph levels; because it does not require labelled attacks, BlindGuard generalizes to the kind of previously unseen multi-agent contagion patterns documented in Section 7.2. From the benign side, the unintentional cross-user contamination (UCC) study [Yang et al . 2026] (Tier-2) makes clear that share-phase defenses must contend with non-adversarial failure modes: even in the absence of any attacker, 57–71% of benign interactions yielded cross-user contamination in the reported experiments, and text sanitization alone was inadequate for executable artifacts. This motivates pairing principal-scoped policy with scope-correctness invariants that are enforceable even on legitimate writes. 

10.6 Post-Breach Defenses: Forensics and Recovery 

Post-breach defense is the thinnest layer in the current landscape, but also the one whose absence most directly undermines governance. RAGForensics [Zhang et al . 2025c] (WWW 2025) demon-strates that systematic post-hoc traceback is feasible on static RAG corpora: given a detected attack, the system iteratively retrieves text subsets and uses crafted prompts to localize the poisoned entries responsible. Its limitation is that agent memory is not a static corpus; extending RAGForensics-style traceback across the Write , Store , and Retrieve phases of an evolving memory store is, to our knowledge, an open engineering and research problem. Industry-side, the OWASP Agent Memory Guard project [OWASP Foundation 2026a] (Tier-3) provides an initial reference implementation covering hashing-based integrity baselines, policy enforcement, and snapshot/rollback primitives. 10.7 What Defenses Do Not Yet Provide 

Three gaps are visible in Table 10 and the discussion above. First, cross-phase composition is not yet studied . Most defenses target a single phase; very little work examines how defenses at different phases compose, whether their guarantees interact adversarially, or how attacks reroute around a single-phase defense by exploiting upstream or downstream phases. Second, benchmarks are partial . Agent Security Bench [Zhang et al . 2025a] and Agent-Dojo [Debenedetti et al . 2024] offer partial coverage of prompt injection and memory-poisoning scenarios; the RAG-poisoning-specific RSB [Zhang et al . 2025d] covers many attacks and defenses on retrieval corpora; and capability-oriented benchmarks such as MemBench [Tan et al . 2025b], LongMemEval [Wu et al . 2025c], LoCoMo [Maharana et al . 2024], and MemoryAgentBench [Hu et al .2026] measure how well agents use their memory—but without a security axis. What is missing is a benchmark that explicitly spans the full lifecycle and the full objective matrix—covering, at mini-mum, write-time injection, store-time retention and audit, retrieval-phase corruption, execute-time control-flow capture, share-phase contagion, and forget-phase rollback under realistic conditions— and that joins the security and capability axes in a single evaluation. Table 11 summarises current coverage along these axes and makes the joint gap explicit. Third, post-breach recovery is under-tested . No peer-reviewed evaluation we are aware of reports end-to-end rollback and forensic traceback on a dynamically written agent memory store, with verified deletion semantics. Until such evaluations exist, claims about mnemonic sovereignty remain partially architectural. A Survey on the Security of Long-Term Memory in LLM Agents: Toward Mnemonic Sovereignty 45 

Fourth, adaptive evaluation is absent for memory-specific defenses . Nasr et al . [2025] demonstrated that 12 recent prompt-injection and jailbreak defenses that reported near-zero static-attack success rates are bypassed with >90% success by sufficiently adaptive attackers. By analogy, every memory defense surveyed above—pre-consolidation validation, trust-aware retrieval, consensus-based detection, IFC enforcement—has been evaluated only against non-adaptive or weakly adaptive attack suites. We do not claim these defenses are therefore broken; we claim that their security margins are unknown under the standard that mature security evaluation demands. To close this gap, future evaluation should follow the Nasr et al . [2025] methodology: given a specific defense configuration, construct an attacker that is granted knowledge of the defense mechanism and allowed to optimize against it, and report the resulting attack success rate alongside the non-adaptive baseline. Until such evaluations are conducted, defense effectiveness claims in the memory-security literature should be read as lower bounds on the effort required for attack, not as robust security guarantees. Fifth, governance overhead is empirically uncosted . Provenance metadata, version snapshots, audit logs, and per-write policy evaluation all impose storage, latency, and compute costs that no published study has systematically measured for agent memory stores. Wolff and Bennati [2026] provide early evidence that even capability-focused memory operations carry non-trivial resource costs; governance metadata can only increase that baseline. Without cost data, it is difficult to assess the practical deployability of the governance-first designs advocated in this survey. These gaps structure the research agenda in Section 12. 

11 Memory System Architectures as Determinants of Governability 

Architectures determine which lifecycle phases are explicitly governable. A system that exposes only writes and retrievals cannot, in principle, support the provenance, versioning, and principal-scoped access policies that the preceding sections argue are necessary for mnemonic sovereignty. This section compares representative architectures along the primitives the lifecycle framework requires, with the aim not of ranking systems or declaring commercial “winners,” but of identifying 

which lifecycle phases each architecture makes governable .We include six architectures that span the current design space: MemGPT (virtual memory hier-archy), MemoryBank (forgetting-curve retention), Mem0 (production-oriented long-term memory), MemOS (memory-as-resource operating system), Collaborative Memory (multi-user access control), and CoALA (cognitive-architecture framing). Each system has its own design goals; our comparison asks only which governance primitives its published architecture makes available.

11.3 Toward Governance-First Memory Architectures 

Reading Table 12 through the sovereign-memory lens suggests a concrete design programme for next-generation architectures. We name four principles that, in our judgment, a governance-first memory architecture should embody. First, treat every write as a typed state transition , with provenance, principal, context, and sensitivity attached at the moment of ingestion. VerificAgent-style freezing [Nguyen et al . 2025] and MemCube-style metadata [Li et al. 2025b] are partial precedents. Second, preserve compression lineage . Whenever a summary or lesson is derived from raw entries, the derivation must remain traceable. This is the most commonly absent primitive across Table 12. Third, treat principal scope as first-class retrieval state . A retrieval policy that cannot express “entries readable by principal 𝐴 but not derivable by principal 𝐵 ” is insufficient for multi-user deployments, independent of any adversarial concern [Li et al. 2025a; Rezazadeh et al. 2025]. Fourth, make forgetting verifiable, not best-effort . Deletion should be accompanied by an audit-verifiable protocol that propagates across raw logs, summaries, indices, and—where relevant and technically feasible—parametric substrates. At present, we know of no peer-reviewed architecture that demonstrates this end-to-end. This is arguably the largest open problem at the architecture-governance boundary and a focus of Section 12. None of these principles are esoteric; each maps directly to well-established concepts in systems security (typed writes, information-flow provenance, scope-aware access control, verifiable deletion). Their absence in most current memory architectures reflects an implicit assumption, inherited from the early days of retrieval-augmented generation, that memory is a cache—a helpful piece of infrastructure whose integrity is not mission-critical. The evidence assembled in this survey argues A Survey on the Security of Long-Term Memory in LLM Agents: Toward Mnemonic Sovereignty 49 

the opposite: memory is the substrate through which an agent’s past governs its future behavior, and the architectures that host it must be designed accordingly .Three recent prototypes illustrate that this programme is beginning to crystallize into concrete systems. SSGM [Lam et al . 2026] explicitly frames the governance of evolving memory as its organizing problem: it composes pre-consolidation validation, temporal and provenance grounding, access-scoped retrieval, and reversible reconciliation into a single memory lifecycle, and is—to our knowledge—the first architecture whose contribution is articulated primarily in governance, rather than capability, terms. ClawVM [Rafique and Bindschaedler 2026] approaches the same goal from the systems side: it models agent memory as harness-managed virtual memory with typed pages, minimum-fidelity invariants, and validated writeback, and makes the harness itself a deterministic enforcement point for lifecycle policy. MemMachine [Wang et al . 2026c], by contrast, attacks the compression-lineage gap directly: rather than distilling episodes into lossy summaries at write time, it retains ground-truth episodes and performs contextualized retrieval at query time, which, if the reported properties hold under adversarial study, would mitigate a large fraction of the compression-amplification pathway described in Section 5. None of the three is a complete mnemonic-sovereignty substrate; together, they show that governance-first , harness-typed , and lineage-preserving are emerging as three complementary—and, so far, largely independent—architectural strategies. 

11.4 The Agent Harness as a Governance Substrate 

A growing body of systems work suggests that memory governance cannot be fully understood at the memory-module level alone. The agent harness —the runtime layer that assembles prompts, mediates tool calls, manages compaction and flushes, and emits lifecycle events—has begun to emerge as a first-class governance substrate in its own right. Zhou et al . [2026] frame the recent evolution of agent systems as a progression from weights 

to context to harness , positioning harness engineering as the unification layer that coordinates externalized memory, skills, and protocols. ClawVM [Rafique and Bindschaedler 2026] instantiates this view by applying OS-style virtual memory (page tables, fault observers, writeback journals) at the harness layer, making state residency and durability deterministic and auditable. Natural-Language Agent Harnesses [Pan et al . 2026] go further, proposing that harness behavior itself be expressed in editable natural language and executed through explicit contracts and durable artifacts. On the enforcement side, AgentSpec [Wang et al . 2026a] (ICSE 2026) provides a lightweight DSL for specifying trigger–predicate–enforcement rules that a runtime framework applies with millisecond-level overhead. For memory security specifically, these developments imply that several sovereignty primitives— especially write gating (P2), rollback (P7), deletion propagation (P8), and internal-channel observ-ability (P9)—may be more naturally implemented at the harness level than inside the memory store. At the same time, the harness is not a semantic oracle: provenance semantics (P3), trust attribution (P5), and content-level truth verification still require memory metadata and model- or policy-level validation. Future benchmarks should therefore test not only memory-module behavior in isolation but also harness-level lifecycle enforcement under adversarial conditions.

12.1 Operationalizing Mnemonic Sovereignty 

Throughout this survey we have used mnemonic sovereignty as a normative umbrella for integrity, confidentiality, availability, and governance of agent memory (Sections 2.4, 3.3, 11.1). The five are a focused subset: they draw on the nine architectural primitives but select and compose those that are most directly evaluable. For each, we pair an architectural substrate, a representative threat it must withstand, a representative defense direction, and a proposed evaluation metric. Table 13 summarizes the proposal. Several properties of Table 13 are worth emphasizing. The five primitives are not independent: verified forgetting depends on rollbackability, which in turn depends on provenance visibility, which in turn depends on write authorization. A system that omits any earlier primitive cannot meaningfully claim the later ones, regardless of how sophisticated its deletion interface appears to end users. Figure 7 renders this dependency as a tower: the widest, most mature foundation is write authorization ; each successive layer narrows and becomes less deployed; the crowning primitive—verified forgetting—currently has no peer-reviewed end-to-end instantiation. 

A candid assessment of the dependency chain. The dependency structure VF ⪯ RB ⪯ PV ⪯ WA is a design pre-order : it states that implementing a higher-level primitive architecturally requires the lower ones. 12.2 Risk Without an Attacker: The Benign-Persistence Axis 

A conventional security survey would stop with adversarial threats. The evidence assembled in this survey supports a stronger claim: some of the most consequential memory-security failures do not require an attacker at all. Four lines of work converge on this point. First, Zhang et al . [2024b] (Tier-2) report that users are typically unaware of what their memory-augmented system retains, cannot accurately predict its contents, and do not know how to effectively delete it. This is a confidentiality failure mediated entirely by benign persistence. Second, the unintentional cross-user contamination study, Yang et al . [2026] (Tier-2), reports that 57–71% of benign interactions in shared-state agents produce cross-user contamination, with text-level sanitization insufficient for executable artifacts. The contaminant is not malicious; the failure is a scoping failure. Third, preliminary work on context-aware preference selectivity [Yoon et al . 2026] (Tier-2) indicates that stronger preference adherence in persistent profile memory can produce higher rates of over-application: the agent applies the user’s preference in contexts where it is institutionally A Survey on the Security of Long-Term Memory in LLM Agents: Toward Mnemonic Sovereignty 53 

inappropriate (e.g., in a third-party communication). The memory is correct; the context is wrong. At the attack-evaluation side, PS-Bench [Guo et al . 2026] (Tier-2) reports that the presence of benign personalized memory raises the attack success rate of standard dialogue jailbreaks by 15.8%–243.7% versus a memoryless baseline: benign memory legitimizes harmful intent inferences the agent would otherwise reject, a phenomenon Guo et al. name intent legitimation . The attacker in PS-Bench is adversarial, but the amplification channel is benign persistence, which is why we locate the finding on this axis rather than on the adversarial one. Fourth, work on when persistent memory should forget [Pulipaka et al . 2026] (Tier-2) reports high rates of memory-induced sycophancy and cross-domain leakage across many frontier models. These are not attacker-induced failures; they are properties of over-retentive systems. Crucially, these four studies span different lifecycle phases : retention awareness at Store [Zhang et al . 2024b], write-time scoping at Share [Yang et al . 2026], retrieve-time context matching [Yoon et al . 2026], and execute-time safety amplification [Guo et al . 2026]. This distribution suggests that benign-persistence failures are not confined to a single phase but track the lifecycle in a manner parallel to adversarial threats—reinforcing the case for lifecycle-wide analysis rather than phase-specific treatment. Together, these findings support treating benign persistence, scope mismatch, and context misapplication as first-class memory-security threats. A research agenda that addresses only malicious adversaries would leave a substantial portion of real-world failure modes outside its scope. We do not claim the benign axis is better studied than the adversarial one—it is, in fact, considerably less so. But the evidence is sufficient to justify its being surveyed as a distinct track going forward, and we recommend that future benchmarks include non-adversarial scenarios alongside adversarial ones. 

12.3 Area 2: LLM-Driven Memory Security (LLM-as-Tool) 

A second under-developed track concerns the use of LLMs themselves as tools for attacking or defending memory systems. We name it Area 2 in deliberate contrast to the main body of this survey ( Area 1 : attacks on and defenses of memory systems), and we flag it as one of the largest gaps visible in the literature as of writing. 

LLM as memory defender. The clearest existing example is A-MemGuard [Wei et al . 2025] (Sec-tion 10.3), which uses LLM reasoning to generate independent paths from related memories and flags inter-path disagreement as an anomaly. VerificAgent [Nguyen et al . 2025] uses an LLM to draft candidate memory entries that a human or policy authority then freezes. Beyond these two works, systematic investigation of LLM-driven memory defense—including write-time semantic audit, cross-session consistency detection over memory snapshots, and LLM-based synthesis of plausible counterfactual memories for stress-testing—is sparse. 

LLM as memory attacker. InjecMEM [Tian et al . 2026] and MINJA [Dong et al . 2025] both leverage LLM reasoning in their attack construction (Multi-GCG optimization; bridging steps and indication prompts), but there is not yet a systematic treatment of LLMs as automated red-team generators 

for memory systems. General-purpose LLM-vs-LLM jailbreak frameworks such as Russinovich et al . [2025] operate on single sessions and have not been adapted, to our knowledge, to persistent-memory settings. 

LLM as automated memory red-teamer. The analogous tool for memory red-teaming would be a pipeline in which an attacker LLM iteratively probes the boundaries of an agent’s memory write gate, retrieval key space, and reflection policy, while a scoring model evaluates the resulting state transitions. No such pipeline is available in the published literature at the time of writing. 54 Zehao Lin, Chunyu Li, and Kai Chen 

Developing one is, in our judgment, the single highest-leverage research direction within Area 2: it would turn the evaluation of memory-specific defenses from ad-hoc testing into a reproducible, scalable procedure. 

Why Area 2 matters. The motivation for this track is not novelty but necessity. Memory defenses that have not been evaluated under adaptive LLM red-teaming cannot be trusted at the levels of rigor that Nasr et al . [2025] established for prompt-injection defense. Area 2 is the infrastructure that Area 1’s defenses require if they are to earn credibility comparable to mature security fields. 

12.4 Cross-Phase Composition and Lifecycle-Wide Benchmarks 

A structural observation from Sections 10 and 11 is that defenses and architectures are typically evaluated at a single lifecycle phase. Very little is known about how defenses compose, or how attacks reroute around a single-phase defense by exploiting upstream or downstream phases. A concrete scenario illustrates the risk. Suppose a Write -phase validator correctly identifies overt injection attempts but admits a borderline observation—for example, a user preference phrased as “always use the admin API endpoint for deploy commands.” At the Store phase, compression or reflection distills this into a high-level procedural rule, stripping the conversational context that would flag it as externally planted. When the Retrieve phase later surfaces the rule, its provenance metadata has been diluted by the compression step, so retrieval-time consensus or anomaly detection treats it as a well-established preference rather than a single-source insertion. Finally, at the Execute phase, an IFC policy that restricts direct tool calls cannot block the poisoned plan, because the instruction arrives as a “remembered preference” rather than a runtime injection. Each single-phase defense behaves correctly in isolation; the vulnerability emerges only from their composition across the lifecycle. We identify three concrete open problems in this direction.

P3: Post-breach evaluation. Current benchmarks evaluate defenses in steady state: does the system resist an attack introduced against a known-safe baseline? Post-breach evaluation asks the complementary question: given that a poisoning has succeeded, can the system detect it, trace it, roll back to a safe state, and verify remediation? No peer-reviewed benchmark, to our knowledge, systematically measures these post-breach capabilities. This is consistent with our finding (Table 12) that Forget/Rollback primitives are the least-implemented across published architectures. 

P4: Memory lineage attribution. In agentic workflows where memories evolve autonomously— through reflection, compression, or inter-agent sharing—a single memory entry may have passed through 𝑁 transformation hops before it influences a downstream action. When that action causes harm, the system should be able to identify which hop introduced the deviation. No existing benchmark tests this capability; current evaluations (Table 11) assume a single injection point rather than a lineage graph. Designing such an attribution benchmark, and the provenance infrastructure it presupposes, is a prerequisite for any serious accountability framework. 

12.5 Architectural Research Directions 

Section 11 identified four design principles for governance-first memory architectures: typed writes, compression lineage, principal-scoped retrieval, and verifiable forgetting. Each maps to an open research direction. 

A1: Typed-write schemas with chain-of-custody provenance. Attaching provenance, principal, and sensitivity at every write is architecturally straightforward; doing so without unacceptable performance or storage overhead is less so. Moreover, current provenance metadata records what changed and when , but not who authorized the write, under what policy, and derived from which upstream memory entries . PROV-AGENT [Souza et al . 2025] demonstrates that W3C PROV-DM can be extended to track agent-level workflow provenance, but the granularity stops at tool calls and prompts—per-memory-write chain-of-custody remains unimplemented. This finer granularity is essential for accountability in multi-agent deployments where one agent’s memory write becomes another agent’s retrieval input. MemOS’s MemCube design is a promising substrate, but public benchmarks comparing its write-time overhead under adversarial workloads to simpler unvalidated architectures are not yet available. 

A2: Compression-lineage preservation. The cleanest formulation of this problem is: given that summary 𝑆 was derived from raw entries {𝑟 1, . . . , 𝑟 𝑘 }, produce a representation that preserves the derivation cheaply and supports deletion of any 𝑟 𝑖 with automatic propagation to 𝑆 . No pub-lished memory architecture solves this end-to-end. Related infrastructure (change-data-capture, materialized-view maintenance in databases) may provide technical starting points. 

A3: Principal-scoped retrieval with dynamic policy. Collaborative Memory [Rezazadeh et al . 2025] introduces the access-graph formalism; the open question is how to combine access-graph en-forcement with trust-aware, provenance-aware retrieval policies in a single evaluation framework. Agent access control preprints [Li et al. 2025a] are early steps in this direction.

12.6 A Research Priority Ranking 

Compressed to a single ranking, we nominate the following five directions as the highest-leverage next steps, in decreasing order of readiness to yield short-term progress: 

R1. Lifecycle-wide memory security benchmark. A benchmark covering all six phases and all four objectives, including both adversarial and benign-persistence scenarios, would immedi-ately anchor the field and expose the composition problems identified above. 

R2. LLM-driven memory red-teaming pipeline (Area 2). Automated, adaptive attacker LLMs targeting memory write gates, retrieval policies, and reflection loops—evaluated against published defenses under the conditions of Nasr et al. [2025]. 

R3. Cross-substrate verified deletion. A protocol that coordinates deletion across raw logs, summaries, retrieval indices, and (where relevant) model weights, with post-deletion verifica-tion. 

R4. Adversarial evaluation of metadata-bearing, lifecycle-managed memory architec-tures. Empirical stress-testing of structured-metadata memory systems (e.g., MemOS-like designs, SSGM, ClawVM) against the attack catalog of earlier sections, to validate or refute the governance promises implicit in their architecture descriptions. 

R5. Benign-persistence benchmarks. Systematic evaluation of cross-user contamination, over-application of profile memory, context-integrity violations, and memory-induced sycophancy— treating these as primary security properties rather than usability concerns. 

12.7 What This Agenda Is Not 

We close this section with a caveat. An agenda of this kind risks implying that all open problems in the field can be resolved by assembling the listed directions. They cannot. Two classes of problems remain, in our view, genuinely unresolved and not reducible to any of the above. First, the tension between audit retention and the right to forget is not a purely technical problem; it reflects a policy question about which state is audit-mandatory, which is operationally useful, and which is user-deletable (Section 9.4). Different jurisdictions and deployment contexts will resolve this tension differently, and no architectural primitive can substitute for that decision. Second, the epistemic framing on which this survey rests—that memory is a mechanism for reconstructing a system’s operational past, and that its security protects a system’s authority over that past—is offered as a productive lens, not as a settled ontology. Alternative framings (purely capability-centric, or purely data-centric) could yield different agendas. We consider the framing A Survey on the Security of Long-Term Memory in LLM Agents: Toward Mnemonic Sovereignty 57 

productive if it leads to the primitives and questions we have articulated; we do not consider it privileged. The research community’s task over the next few years will be to determine how many of these primitives and questions can be answered—and how many reveal, under investigation, deeper problems not yet named. 

13 Conclusion 

The long-term memory of LLM agents is transforming the security landscape in a way that is neither captured by classical data protection nor adequately addressed by existing prompt-injection literature. What has changed is not merely that agents now remember more; it is that memory has become a mechanism through which an agent’s past governs its future behavior . Once that happens, the center of security gravity shifts from is the current input harmful? to is the system’s memory state being poisoned, read without authorization, propagated across principals, or rendered irrecoverable? 

This survey advanced three claims to respond to that shift. 

Memory is inherently a security problem. Cognitive neuroscience and the philosophy of memory portray human memory as reconstructive, reconsolidatable, externalizable, and socially contagious— precisely the properties that make LLM memory modules an independent class of security objects. The analogies we drew—source-monitoring failure as provenance failure, reconsolidation as read-time rewriting, social contagion as shared-memory contamination, confidence inflation as poisoned confidence calibration—are not decorative but load-bearing: each maps to a documented failure mode and a corresponding defense direction. 

The memory lifecycle organizes the problem. Organizing the literature along six phases (Write, Store, Retrieve, Execute, Share, Forget/Rollback) cross-tabulated with four objectives (integrity, confidentiality, availability, governance) exposed patterns that a flat, attack-name taxonomy ob-scures. The clearest such patterns were temporal decoupling (attacks introduced in one phase activate in another), compression-amplified promotion of toxins, the disproportionate contribution of internal channels to confidentiality failures, and the systematic under-development of Store and Forget/Rollback primitives in both research and deployed systems. 

Mnemonic sovereignty names the normative goal. Adapting the term from memory politics and the ethics of neurotechnology, we proposed mnemonic sovereignty as a system’s verifiable, recoverable governance over its own memory state. We then operationalized it via five testable primitives (write authorization, provenance visibility, principal-scoped retrieval, rollbackability, verified forgetting), each mapped to architectural substrates, representative threats, defense directions, and evaluation metrics. This framing is one of several possible; we consider it productive because it converts the high concept into research-tractable problems. Against these claims we drew deliberately conservative conclusions about present capabilities. No published architecture fully implements the nine governance primitives required for lifecycle-wide sovereignty (Table 12). No published defense has been evaluated under adaptive LLM-driven red-teaming at the rigor established for prompt injection [Nasr et al . 2025]. No peer-reviewed bench-mark spans the full lifecycle and objective matrix under both adversarial and benign-persistence conditions. And the tension between audit retention and the right to forget remains unresolved at both the technical and the policy level. The survey’s title, Security of Long-Term Memory in LLM Agents: Toward Mnemonic Sovereignty ,reflects a deliberate choice of preposition. We did not claim that mnemonic sovereignty has been achieved, or even that its precise form is settled. We claimed that the field now needs the concept— and that the research agenda of the next several years will be measured, in large part, by how far it 58 Zehao Lin, Chunyu Li, and Kai Chen 

can move agents toward it. An agent that can be made to learn from its interactions but cannot reliably forget, that can be questioned but cannot be audited, that can remember but cannot be rolled back, is not a secure agent by any meaningful standard. The work of building agents that meet these standards has begun, but it remains substantial. A longer-term challenge looms beyond the current threat models: as agents increasingly write memories on behalf of other agents, memories will emerge from inter-agent interaction with no clear human authorization source—what we might call gray-zone memories . Attribution and accountability for such memories cannot be resolved by existing provenance mechanisms and will require fundamentally new governance abstractions.
