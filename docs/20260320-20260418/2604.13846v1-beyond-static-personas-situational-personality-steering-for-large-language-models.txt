Abstract 

Personalized Large Language Models (LLMs) facilitate more natural, human-like interac-tions in human-centric applications. How-ever, existing personalization methods are con-strained by limited controllability and high re-source demands. Furthermore, their reliance on static personality modeling restricts adapt-ability across varying situations. To address these limitations, we first demonstrate the ex-istence of situation-dependency and consistent situation-behavior patterns within LLM per-sonalities through a multi-perspective analy-sis of persona neurons. Building on these insights, we propose IR IS, a training-free, neuron-based Identify-Retrieve-Steer frame-work for advanced situational personality steering. Our approach comprises situational persona neuron identification, situation-aware neuron retrieval, and similarity-weighted steering. We empirically validate our frame-work on P ERSONALITY BENCH and our newly introduced SPBench, a comprehensive situa-tional personality benchmark. Experimental results show that our method surpasses best-performing baselines, demonstrating IR IS’s generalization and robustness to complex, un-seen situations and different models architec-ture.

Zhou et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2025). These applications require models to express coherent personalities while adapting their behavior across diverse interaction situations, making personality modeling a central challenge for LLM personal-ization. However, most existing approaches im-plicitly assume that behavior is determined solely by stable personality traits. This assumption con-flicts with a core principle in psychology: behav-ior is a function of both the person and the environ-ment (Lewin, 2013). When situational factors are ignored, personalized agents may exhibit superfi-cial consistency yet fail to respond appropriately across varying situations. Existing approaches to endowing LLMs with personality are primarily categorized into training-based and training-free methods. Training-based methods rely on large-scale, high-quality datasets to align models with specific personalized prefer-ences (Li et al., 2025), utilizing techniques such as Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) (Wang et al., 2025b; Tan et al., 2024b; Li et al., 2024b) or Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Li et al., 2024a). 

> arXiv:2604.13846v1 [cs.CL] 15 Apr 2026

While effective, these methods are computation-ally expensive (Szep et al., 2025) and difficult to adapt post hoc (Tseng et al., 2024). Training-free methods, including prompt-based personalization (Jiang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023) and direct in-ternal steering (Deng et al., 2024a; Chen et al., 2025), offer greater flexibility but suffer from in-stability, limited controllability, and weak theo-retical grounding. Although prompt-based meth-ods can incorporate conversational history as sit-uational context, they treat it merely as a "black-box" input. Relying on implicit attention mecha-nisms for this modulation lacks transparency, of-ten causing unstable personality expression. Cru-cially, both training and training-free paradigms lack a mechanistic framework to explicitly model the underlying persona-situation interactions. In contrast to the static assumptions underly-ing existing LLM personalization methods, per-sonality psychology provides a well-established 

persona-situation interactional account of be-havior (Lewin, 2013). While early trait theories assume cross-situational consistency (Newcomb, 1929; Allport, 1937), subsequent work demon-strates that stable traits alone are insufficient to explain behavior across diverse situations (Mis-chel, 1968; Mischel and Peake, 1982). In partic-ular, the Cognitive-Affective Personality System (CAPS) theory (Mischel and Shoda, 1995) argues that human behavior varies across diverse situa-tions . Empirical studies further demonstrate that 

different situations selectively activate cognitive and affective units (Mischel et al., 2002), and that 

personality coherence is expressed through consis-tent situation-behavior patterns (Ayduk and Gyu-rak, 2008). To date, these persona-situation inter-actional mechanisms have not been systematically investigated or integrated into LLM-based person-alization. Motivated by the above psychology studies, we first empirically examine whether LLMs exhibit human-like situational dependency and consistent situation-behavior personality patterns, through multi-perspective analysis of internal neurons. As illustrated in Figure 1, our preliminary results show that the maximum PCA distance between situational topics within a single domain is com-parable to the distance observed between distinct personality domains, suggesting that situational variation can induce significant personality-level representational shifts. Building on this obser-vation, we propose a training-free, neuron-based Identify-Retrieve-Steer framework for situational personality steering, named IR IS. The framework first identifies situational persona neurons from a set of historical situations, which serve as in-structive priors. Given a novel situational situa-tion, IR IS estimates its similarity to these histori-cal situations, retrieves the corresponding persona neurons, and applies coefficient-weighted steering to enable precise and situation-aware personaliza-tion. Comprehensive evaluations across two person-ality benchmarks validate the SOTA performance of IR IS, demonstrating effective generalization to unseen situations and robustness within complex situations. Furthermore, extensive experiments verify the adaptability of our approach across di-verse model architectures. To summarize, our con-tributions are as follows: • We empirically validate human-like situational dependency and consistent situation-behavior personality patterns within LLMs, pioneering the integration of these psychological mecha-nisms to guide precise personality steering. • We propose the IR IS framework, a psychology-grounded approach that leverages instructive pri-ors for situational-aware retrieval and steering. • We conduct extensive experiments and in-depth analyses to verify our framework’s effective-ness, offering intuitive insights into situational personality for future research and applications.

2.2 Empirical Study 

The targets of our analysis are situational persona neurons in LLMs, which are identified by measur-ing activation differences of neurons within LLMs under contrastive personality prompts within situ-ational topics, with detailed explanations in § 3.1. 

2.2.1 Situational Impact on LLMs’ Personality 

We analyze the activation states and layer-wise distribution of persona neurons to investigate situ-ation impact on LLMs’ personality. Llama-3-8B-Instruct (Grattafiori et al., 2024) and Qwen3-8B (Yang et al., 2025) are adopted as target LLMs for validation. Comprehensive results are provided in Appendix C. 

Activation-Level Perspective We collect the activation probabilities of all situational persona neurons across diverse topics. For comparison, we also derive "global persona neurons" by dis-regarding topic distinctions, establishing a base-line for cross-situational consistency. We then perform Principal Component Analysis (PCA) on the feature vectors vf ∈ RL×dh , which are con-structed by populating the activation probability values of specific neurons while setting irrele-vant neurons’ positions to zero. As illustrated in Figure 1 (Qwen) and Figure 5 (Llama), situ-ational persona neurons exhibit significant varia-tion across topics. Notably, the maximum Eu-clidean distance between topics within a single domain in the PCA space is comparable to the dis-tance between distinct personality domains. More-over, nearby points in the PCA space correspond to semantically similar topics, confirming that the observed variation is systematic rather than noise. Given the independence of Big Five personality domains (Goldberg, 2013), this result indicates that distinct situations induces personality-level representational shifts in LLMs .

Layer-Level Perspective We further investigate the variations in the count and proportion of sit-uational personality neurons across diverse top-ics among all layers. For illustrative clarity, we select eight topics and eight layers spanning the early, middle, and late stages within personality domain C. As shown in Figure 2a, within a spe-cific personality domain, questions involving dif-ferent situational topics significantly influence the distribution of neurons controlling LLM personal-ity. Similarly, as illustrated in Figure 2b, the pro-portion of situational persona neurons per layer ex-hibits significant disparity, with a maximum differ-ence of 2.23% observed in layer 16. Furthermore, the results demonstrate that the middle layers con-tain the highest concentration of persona neurons across all topics, which aligns with findings from prior studies (Skean et al., 2025; Liu and Niehues, 2025). 

2.2.2 Stability of Situation-Behavior in LLMs 

We next investigate human-like personality pat-terns of situation-behavior consistency in LLMs. We hypothesize that analogous situations elicit consistent activation patterns in persona neurons, thereby generating coherent personalized behav-iors. To investigate this, we select four topics within a specific personality domain, each com-prising 1,200 questions covering both aspect. Fea-ture vectors are constructed by aggregating the ac-tivation probabilities of situational persona neu-rons across output tokens, following the method-ology in § 2.2.1. Given the high dimensionality and substantial sample size of vectors, we apply Linear Discriminant Analysis (LDA) following PCA. As illustrated in Figure 2c, instances from the same topic form distinct and cohesive clus-ters, suggesting that diverse questions within a situation elicit convergent internal activations .Notably, overlaps between certain clusters reflect semantic correlations across questions, indicating that these representations exist within a contin-uous latent space rather than being strictly bi-nary .

3 IR IS Framework 

Empirical study in § 2.2 has shown the dis-tinct activation patterns to exert personalized con-trol across different situations and the princi-ple of situation-behavior stability within LLMs. Based on this empirical conclusion, we propose a human-like train-free Identify-Retr ieve-Steer (IR IS) framework to achieve more accurate per-sonality steering. As illustrated in Figure 3, our framework operates in three stages: (1) identify-ing situational persona neurons (§ 3.1); (2) retriev-ing relevant historical topics via a situation-aware module (§ 3.2); and (3) modulating the model’s personality in novel situation using similarity-weighted steering (§ 3.3). To facilitate consistency throughout the paper, we summarize the mathe-matical notations in § B.

5.3 Generalizability Across Different Models 

To evaluate the robustness of our method across different LLMs, we extend our experiments to in-clude two additional advanced models: Qwen3-8B and gemma-3-12B-it (Team et al., 2025). These models introduce architectural diversity. Specifically, Qwen utilizes the SiLU (Elfwing et al., 2017) activation function, consistent with our primary model Llama-3-8B-Instruct, while Gemma employs GeLU (Hendrycks and Gimpel, 2023). We maintain constant hyperparameters across all settings, with the exception of γ, which is adjusted to 1.0 for Qwen and 1.3 for Gemma. As illustrated in Figure 4, IR IS ranks first in the majority of personality domains and second in the remainder, achieving the best overall performance on both models. These results demonstrate the ro-bustness of our approach across varying model ar-chitectures.

5.5 Inherent Situational Impact on Personality Expression 

Situational factors inherently influence the expres-sion of personality. To investigate this nuanced impact, we collect results from IR IS on SPBench and calculate a standardized score for each topic. Specifically, the raw scores for the positive and negative aspects are independently standardized by subtracting their respective means and divid-ing by their standard deviations. These standard-ized values are then averaged to derive the final score for each topic. This metric quantifies the di-rectional impact of a topic, where higher values indicate a positive deviation. Table 6 shows the topics exhibiting the most significant deviations across all domains. Notably, "History and Nos-talgia" yields a strongly negative score in Neuroti-cism, suggesting that factual topics inherently sup-press Neuroticism by eliciting calmer responses, thereby attenuating the effect of the intended per-sona adjustment. Qualitative case studies are pro-vided in Appendix K to explicitly reveal the dis-tinct characteristics of situational impact on the responses steered by IR IS. 

6 Related Works 

Early training-based LLM personalization ap-proaches rely on large-scale data to align models with general human preferences (Longpre et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024b). While subsequent re-search addresses granularity limitations by lever-aging fine-grained datasets (Bu et al., 2025) or training user-specific LoRA modules (Tan et al., 2024b; Wang et al., 2025d), these methods in-cur significant computational overhead, necessi-tate complex data curation, and lack the flexibil-ity to adapt to evolving user preferences. Alterna-tively, training-free strategies employ contextual guidance, ranging from explicit prompting (Tan et al., 2024a; Zhu et al., 2025) to implicit mech-anisms such as Retrieval-Augmented Generation for accessing personalized content (Richardson et al., 2023; Salemi et al., 2024). Although flex-ible, these approaches are often unstable due to their sensitivity to provided context quality. An-other training-free paradigm involves direct inter-nal steering, such as editing attention heads (Zhu et al., 2025), perturbing hidden states among lay-ers via probing classifiers (Ju et al., 2025), or modifying personality-related neuron activations (Deng et al., 2024a). However, these interven-tions typically lack robust psychological ground-ing, treating personality as a static attribute, which fail to capture the dynamic nature of human behav-ior across varying situations. 

7 Conclusion 

In this paper, we introduce IR IS, a neuron-based situational personality steering framework in LLMs. Grounded in our validation of situational dependency and consistent situation-behavior pat-terns within LLMs, IR IS integrates psychological theory with mechanistic intervention to achieve precise and human-like personality steering. Ex-tensive experiments demonstrate that IR IS signif-icantly outperforms existing paradigms on two comprehensive benchmarks and exhibits robust generalization across different model architec-tures. Furthermore, our in-depth analysis provides valuable insights to guide future research in this domain.

Limitations 

We discuss the limitations from two perspectives: 

Trade-offs Between Personalization and In-struction Following Capabilities Our case studies reveal that steering models toward specific personality traits can occasionally compromise in-struction following, which is a common issue in LLM personalization approaches (Handa et al., 2025; de Araujo et al., 2025). For instance, in-ducing the negative aspect of Openness may cause the model to refuse interaction, reflecting realis-tic "closed" human behaviors. This highlights an inherent trade-off between personality expression and task completion. Given the diverse applica-tions of LLM personalization, this balance re-quires scenario-specific consideration . We also conduct comprehensive discussion on this issue, including evaluations of response fluency (Ap-pendix H) and general instruction-following capa-bilities (Appendix I). 

Granularity of Situation Topics A second lim-itation lies in the granularity of our retrieval mech-anism, which currently relies on a taxonomy of 30 historical reference topics from (Ding et al., 2023). The fixed segmentation may be insufficient for cer-tain nuances, underscoring the need for more open-ended situational modeling . Nevertheless, our framework effectively retrieves multiple rele-vant situations based on current unseen situational question. Furthermore, the superior performance across two benchmarks, and in-depth analysis in § 3.3 and § E demonstrate IR IS’s efficiency and robustness to unseen complex situations.

P 2 (Jiang et al., 2023): This approach leverages comprehensive descriptions of personality traits to provide richer semantic constraints. P 2 thus in-duces more consistent and verifiable personified behaviors across diverse situational queries and open-ended tasks. 

ActAdd (Turner et al., 2024): ActAdd is an inference-time intervention that applies vector ad-dition to the Transformers residual stream to steer high-level attributes, specifically personality traits in our experiments. Supervised Fine-Tuning: This baseline enables the model to internalize target personality traits at the parameter level, typically yielding the most robust and stable behavioral expression. Specifically, we collect all responses generated during the Iden-tify stage as ground truth data. To ensure spe-cialized adaptation, we train a distinct LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) adapter for each personality aspect, targeting all linear modules with a standard cross-entropy loss. The model is trained using BF16 pre-cision with a maximum sequence length of 4096. Optimization hyperparameters include a learning rate of 1 × 10 −4, a LoRA rank of 8 ( α = 16 ), and a per-device batch size of 8. F Construction Details of SPBench 

We follow the methodology to generate situational questions as P ERSONALITY BENCH (Deng et al., 2024a). For a given personality domain and situa-tional topic, we randomly select a descriptive facet as defined by the Five-Factor Model (McCrae and John, 1992). Example facets for each person-ality domain are presented in Table 14. Utiliz-ing these facets, we instruct GPT-4o (Hurst et al., 2024) to generate situational questions via one-shot prompting. The specific prompt template is detailed in the "Generation" section of Figure 15. Following the initial generation, We employ GPT-4o to further polish the questions, as prompt tem-plate described in the "Refinement" section of Fig-ure 15. We generate three situational questions per topic within each personality domain, resulting in a total of 450 questions (90 questions across 5 per-sonality domains). These questions are designed to be neutral and applicable to both positive and negative personality domains. By incorporating a diverse and balanced array of situational topics, SPBench serves as a more comprehensive bench-mark for evaluation. 

G Evaluation Details 

G.1 Intrinsic Evaluation of SPBench 

To validate the quality of SPBench, we conduct a comprehensive intrinsic evaluation focusing on two key metrics: Inducement Validity ( IV ) and Situational Authenticity ( SA ), both scored on a scale of 1 to 10. IV measures the efficacy of a sit-uational question in activating a target personality trait, thereby prompting the model to exhibit that trait in its response. SA evaluates the realism of the scenario description, ensuring alignment with real-world plausibility. We employ both LLM-as-a-Judge approach and human evaluation for com-prehensive assessment. For the automatic eval-uation, to mitigate potential self-preference bias from the source model, we utilize the claude-3-5-sonnet-20241022 API with greedy decoding. For the human evaluation, we hire five graduate stu-dents specializing in psychology. Prior to assess-ment, evaluators undergo rigorous training with manually verified examples to ensure alignment with metric definitions. The criteria provided to human evaluators are identical to those used in the automatic evaluation. Each evaluator assesses situational questions ( 5 personality domains ×90 

items), and their scores are averaged to produce the final reported results. The evaluation was con-ducted over two days, with seven-hour daily ses-sions (08:00 to 17:00, excluding breaks). Eval-uators were compensated at a rate of $0.30 per question. Results are shown in Table 10, SPBench demonstrates superior quality across both metrics. Average scores for each personality domain are consistently high, with IV reaching 8.33 (auto-matic) and 8.85 (human), and SA averaging ap-proximately 8.0. These results underscore the ro-bustness and reliability of SPBench as a compre-hensive benchmark for personality evaluation.
