Abstract. Open-Vocabulary Object Detection (OVOD) has achieved remarkable success in generalizing to novel categories. However, this success often rests on the implicit assumption of domain stationarity. In this work, we provide a principled revisit of the OVOD paradigm, uncovering a fundamental vulnerability: the fragile coupling between vi-sual manifolds and textual embeddings when distribution shifts occur. We first systematically formalize Domain-Generalized Open-Vocabulary Object Detection (DG-OVOD). Through empirical analysis, we demon-strate that visual shifts do not merely add noise; they cause a collapse of the latent cross-modal space where novel category visual signals detach from their semantic anchors. Motivated by these insights, we propose Progressive Domain-invariant Cross-modal Alignment (PICA). PICA de-parts from uniform training by introducing a multi-level ambiguity and signal strength curriculum. It builds adaptive pseudo-word prototypes, refined via sample confidence and visual consistency, to enforce invari-ant cross-domain modality alignment. Our findings suggest that OVOD’s robustness to domain shifts is intrinsically linked to the stability of the la-tent cross-modal alignment space. Our work provides both a challenging benchmark and a new perspective on building truly generalizable open-vocabulary systems that extend beyond static laboratory conditions. 

Keywords: Domain generalization · Open vocabulary object detection

leaving it unable to maintain stable cross-modal alignment under distribution shifts. Taking Baron [52] as a representative example, as shown in Figure 1a, domain shifts degrade spatial grounding for both base and novel categories. For the base class (Person), attention under frost becomes more dispersed but still retains coarse object-centric localization. In contrast, the novel class (Ele-phant) exhibits substantially stronger distortion: activations detach from the object boundary and shift toward background textures and frost artifacts. This disparity suggests that although spatial grounding is generally perturbed, the degradation is disproportionately amplified for novel categories, whose predic-tions depend entirely on stable cross-modal alignment rather than learned visual priors [13,32,34,52,55,59]. This reveals a fundamental paradox in current OVOD: the very mechanism that enables open-vocabulary expansion, cross-modal align-ment, becomes the critical point of failure under distribution shifts, as it lacks the domain-invariant correspondence to withstand visual perturbations. Moreover, the impact of distribution shifts is highly sample-specific: as shown in Figure 1, some regions suffer from signal collapse, while others face boundary confusion, where the margin to incorrect categories shrinks. This heterogeneity means that uniform training treats reliable and unreliable regions indiscrimi-nately, destabilizing cross-modal alignment precisely where novel categories are most vulnerable. Collectively, these issues demonstrate that without alignment invariance, the cross-modal projection cannot provide reliable implicit supervi-sion for novel categories, severely limiting generalization under domain shift. To address the alignment degradation, we propose Progressive Domain-invariant Cross-modal Alignment (PICA). PICA evaluates each region along two comple-mentary dimensions that directly characterize these failure modes: (1) ambiguity proxy hri , which measures boundary confusion by capturing how easily the region can be confused with hard negatives, and (2) signal strength proxy qri , which measures signal collapse by quantifying the absolute confidence of its positive cross-modal alignment. These two proxies jointly determine each region’s train-ing priority: during training, we follow a curriculum-guided adaptation strategy that prioritizes samples with low ambiguity and high alignment confidence in the early stages, and progressively incorporates harder or less reliable instances as the projection layer becomes better calibrated. This progressive stratification mitigates misleading image-text associations and the resulting gradient conflicts caused by uncertain cross-modal signals in early training, while preserving in-formative yet challenging samples for later refinement. In summary, our work makes the following contributions: 

– We formalize Domain-Generalized Open-Vocabulary Object Detection (DG-OVOD), a systematic unified evaluation protocol designed to quantify the impact of distribution shifts on open-vocabulary generalization. 

– Motivated by our findings, we propose PICA, which enforces domain-invariant cross-modal alignment through a progressive curriculum, effectively rectify-ing the observed semantic-visual detachment. 4 X. Xu et al. 

– We establish the first systematic benchmark for DG-OVOD and reveal that existing OVOD methods universally fail to maintain cross-modal alignment stability under distribution shifts, exposing a paradigm-level vulnerability. 

## 2 Related Work 

2.1 Open-Vocabulary Object Detection 

Open-vocabulary object detection (OVOD) enables detectors trained on a closed set of base categories to recognize novel classes at inference. Since annotations for novel categories are unavailable during training, OVOD is typically formulated as a vision-language task, allowing detectors to leverage pretrained vision-language models (VLMs) for knowledge transfer from seen to unseen categories. Existing methods mainly fall into two categories. The first aligns detection heads with pretrained VLMs, most notably CLIP [36], to exploit its strong zero-shot transfer ability by integrating CLIP features or aligning region features with text em-beddings [1, 22, 48, 51–53, 55, 57]. While effective, these methods rely on CLIP’s image-level pretraining, limiting robustness under distribution shifts. The second line of work constructs object-aware vision-language spaces through large-scale heterogeneous supervision across classification [10], detection [15,26,41], ground-ing [35,54], and image-text datasets [42], enabling finer-grained cross-modal rep-resentations [25, 58, 59]. In this work, we focus on the first paradigm, which has been widely adopted in recent OVOD approaches, and systematically investigate its robustness under distribution shifts and corrupted inputs. 

2.2 Domain Generalization 

Domain generalization (DG) aims to enable models to perform reliably across unseen or distribution-shifted domains without access to target-domain data during training [14, 47]. In computer vision, models often face diverse domain shifts caused by adverse weather, motion blur, sensor noise [16, 28, 31], or style variations such as artistic renditions and synthetic-to-real transitions [18, 30]. Efforts to improve domain generalization can be broadly categorized into three directions. Data-centric approaches enhance model robustness by synthesizing diverse domains or applying corruption- and style-based augmentations [9,17,37]. Model-centric approaches focus on architectural or adaptation strategies, such as domain adversarial training, normalization modulation, and meta-learning [4, 11,39]. Representation-centric approaches leverage self-supervised or contrastive pretraining to learn domain-invariant features, often preserving semantic consis-tency through vision-language alignment [19, 36]. Most studies focus on closed-vocabulary detection. In contrast, open-vocabulary detection relies on fine-grained image-text alignment to generalize beyond seen categories. Existing domain generalization and robustness approaches mainly en-hance visual representations, yet they often overlook the cross-modal semantic consistency crucial for OVOD under domain shifts. 2.3 Curriculum Learning 

Curriculum Learning (CL) [2] is a training paradigm that mimics the human learning process by organizing samples from easy to hard, thereby improving convergence and generalization. Extensive research has explored diverse for-mulations and scheduling strategies, including manually defined curricula and automatic mechanisms such as self-paced learning [24], teacher-student frame-works [12], and reinforcement-based sample selection [33]. These methods gen-erally consist of a difficulty estimator and a scheduler that jointly determine the learning order of training instances. In computer vision, CL has been applied to domain adaptation, semi-supervised learning, and robustness enhancement un-der distribution shifts. Recent extensions target multi-modal and cross-domain settings, including vision-language pretraining [44, 59] and robustness [3]. Most existing approaches overlook the progressive alignment between modal-ities and domains, focusing only on single-modality or data-level difficulty. We in-troduce a curriculum-guided modality learning framework for domain-generalized open-vocabulary object detection, achieving a gradual refinement from coarse to fine-grained cross-modal alignment. 

## 3 Methodology 

3.1 Problem Statement 

Open-Vocabulary Object Detection (OVOD) aims to detect objects from both base categories Cbase and novel categories Cnovel , where Cbase ∩ C novel = ∅. The model leverages a pretrained vision-language model (VLM) [36], with text and visual encoders denoted as Ftext and Fvision , respectively. For a class c, its se-mantic embedding is tc = Ftext (prompt (c)) ; for an image region r, its visual em-bedding is fr = Fvision (r). Detection scores are computed as sr,c = sim (fr , t c),with higher values indicating stronger cross-modal alignment. For regions without associated class labels (i.e., from Cnovel ), we employ a learnable pseudo-word prototype wr ∈ Rd as a textual proxy that approximates the semantic content of region r:

wr = Pimg →text (fr ) = Wfr + b, (1) where Pimg →text is a trainable projection head, and W, b are learnable param-eters, following common practice in vision-language alignment [20, 32, 36, 52]. Crucially, Pimg →text is jointly optimized by Lovod , which grounds wr toward text embeddings tc, ensuring that wr approximates a semantic position in the language embedding space rather than a self-referential projection artifact. The standard OVOD assumes that Dsrc and Dtgt share the same distribution. Under the DG-OVOD setting, the core challenge lies in the domain-induced variance of visual embeddings fr , which triggers an alignment-drift, causing a huge performance drop on novel categories, which lack explicit supervision, ne-cessitating a mechanism to enforce domain-invariant cross-modal alignment. To empirically validate this alignment-drift hypothesis, we evaluate a broad set of representative OVOD methods on our DG-OVOD benchmark, detailed in Table 1 and Table 2. Since novel-category generalization in these methods is entirely contingent on cross-modal alignment [1,22,48,51–53,57,58], their catas-trophic performance drop, averaging over 50%, is a direct proxy for cross-modal alignment collapse. Critically, this failure is not merely a loss of precision but a structural breakdown of the semantic manifold: rankings under clean conditions fail to predict robustness under shift. This systematic evidence confirms that current alignment mechanisms, while effective in stationary environments, lack the domain-invariant correspondence to anchor novel-class signals against visual perturbations. This fundamental blind spot in the existing paradigm directly motivates the invariant alignment approach developed in the following section.

## 4 Experiment

We compare PICA with state-of-the-art Open-Vocabulary Object Detection (OVOD) methods and con-duct detailed ablation studies to analyze the effect of each component.

Effect of Sample-Adaptive Stratification. This confirms that the curriculum selectively consolidates alignment robustness for informative regions, a property that uniform training fails to achieve.

## 5 Conclusion 

We present PICA for Domain-Generalized Open-Vocabulary Object Detection, establishing the first systematic evidence that cross-modal alignment instabil-ity is the binding constraint on open-vocabulary generalization under distribu-tion shifts. Our benchmark and findings reveal a blind spot shared across exist-ing OVOD methods, and demonstrate that progressive domain-invariant cross-modal alignment provides a principled path toward truly domain-generalized open-vocabulary systems beyond static laboratory conditions.
