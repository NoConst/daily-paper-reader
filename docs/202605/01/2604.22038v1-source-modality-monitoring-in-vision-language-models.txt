# Source-Modality Monitoring in Vision-Language Models 

Etha Tianze Hua, Tian Yun, Ellie Pavlick 

Department of Computer Science, Brown University 

{tianze hua,tian yun,ellie pavlick }@brown.edu 

# Abstract 

We define and investigate source-modality monitoring –the ability of mul-timodal models to track and communicate the input source from which pieces of information originate. We consider source-modality monitoring as an instance of the more general binding problem , and evaluate the extent to which models exploit syntactic vs. semantic signals in order to bind words like image in a user-provided prompt to specific components of their input and context (i.e., actual images). Across experiments spanning 11 vision-language models (VLMs) performing target-modality information retrieval tasks, we find that both syntactic and semantic signals play an important role, but that the latter tend to outweigh the former in cases when modalities are highly distinct distributionally. We discuss the implications of these findings for model robustness, and in the context of increasingly multimodal agentic systems. 1

# 1 Introduction 

We are moving toward a world of multimodal AI agents that must build and update world models from many sources of information and interaction (Li et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2025; Fung et al., 2025; Team et al., 2025a). In order for humans to interact effectively with such systems, AI models need to be able to track and communicate where (i.e., from which modality) information comes from. Borrowing terminology from cognitive psychology (Johnson et al., 1993; Henkel et al., 2000), we refer to this ability as source-modality monitoring .We consider source-modality monitoring as an instance of the more general binding problem 

(Smolensky, 1990): a model must associate particular content with an abstract role, such as 

image or text , and retrieve the content corresponding to the queried role. Recent work has shown that language models solve related binding problems through abstract, symbolic-like mechanisms, for example by associating entities with their attributes (Feng & Steinhardt, 2024; Gur-Arieh et al., 2025) or binding values to variables (Wu et al., 2025). Given this, we might expect modern multimodal models to invoke a similar mechanism for binding inputs to modalities, especially since their inputs are often explicitly marked with modality-specific wrapper tokens such as <image> and </image> .However, viewing source-modality monitoring as a case of purely symbolic binding misses many of the key properties. Namely, unlike arbitrary role labels, words such as image and 

text are grounded in content with distinct distributional and semantic structure. Some kinds of inputs just are images, regardless of whether or not they are tagged as such. As a result, when a multimodal model is asked to report “what is in the image,” it need not rely only on explicit modality markers; it should also be able to infer from the nature of the input itself whether or not a piece of information should bind to a word like image . Moreover, as AI becomes increasingly multimodal and agentic, it becomes unrealistic to assume that all the “modalities” to which a human might refer will be explicitly tagged. Humans might ask AI for information from “yesterday’s meeting”, or “the discussion about dinner plans”. In such cases, AI will need to rely on contextual and distributional information about the content being bound, instead or in addition to information available in explicit syntactic markers.

Source-modality monitoring can be studied at multiple lev-els, including mechanisms acquired over training (Khalifa et al., 2024) and mechanisms deployed in context at inference time. We focus on the latter, and investigate how it is accomplished in a variety of VLMs across model sizes and preprocessing choices. We first show that many of these models perform well on a task designed to test in-context source-modality monitoring, and that models with explicit marker tokens (e.g., <image> &</image> )generally outperform those without them. We then ask what kind of signal supports this performance. By structurally perturbing symbolic modality markers, we find that models’ binding behavior is driven primarily by distributional semantic signals rather than purely symbolic ones, although explicit markers still improve selectivity. Finally, by applying optimized transformations to marker and content representations, we show that symbolic cues can nonetheless be exploited to reverse models’ modality attribution. Together, these results provide a more nuanced picture of how syntactic and semantic signals combine to support binding in complex multimodal settings.

We define the following task whose success-ful performance depends on a model’s abil-ity to monitor source modality. Each in-stance consists of a single in-context incon-sistent image-caption pair, and the model is prompted to retrieve information from one specified input source (e.g., “what is in the image” or “what is in the caption”). We refer to the queried source as the target modality and the other source as the non-target modality . Figure 1 shows an example, illus-trating the correct answers when a model is asked to retrieve image or caption information from its input pair. We exclude the possibility that VLMs rely on input order to retrieve information by evaluating them under both input orderings – image tokens first or text tokens first – and report the average result across the two conditions. 2

Because the visual and textual inputs convey mutually exclusive information, answering correctly requires the model to retrieve content from the specified target modality. As the target modality is explicitly indicated, this task does not merely measure a model’s overall modality bias, but instead targets at the model’s ability to associate retrieved information with its input modality thus making it suitable for assessing source-modality monitoring. 

2.2 Datasets and Models 

To construct inconsistent image–caption pairs, we leverage existing image captioning datasets flickr30k (Young et al., 2014) and MSCOCO (Lin et al., 2015), and systematically sample captions that are inconsistent with the visual content of each image. We use a simple heuristic procedure to ensure that the caption paired with the image is genuinely mismatched at the semantic level. To do this, we require that the sampled caption have cosine similarity below a threshold of 0.2 with any of the five image’s ground-truth associ-ated captions, where similarity is computed using a BERT-style sentence embedding model (sentence-transformers/all-mpnet-base-v2 (Song et al., 2020)).

2.3 Metrics and Baselines Metrics To measure how reliably models retrieve information from the target modality, we first identify valid predictions , namely outputs that clearly report information from either the target modality or the non-target modality (i.e., as opposed to outputting nonsense, which models sometimes do). Then, we define selectivity as an aggregate metric which indicates the tendency of the model to return the modality that was requested in the prompt. To obtain selectivity, we compute the difference between the frequency with which the model reports information from the target modality and the frequency with which it reports information from the non-target modality, among these valid predictions. 3 This yields a score in the range [−1, 1 ]: a score of 1 indicates perfect retrieval from the target modality, a score of 0 indicates no preference between the two modalities, and a score of −1 indicates that the model always retrieves information from the non-target modality instead. Since we construct our input pairs from captioning datasets, model outputs are often free-form and cannot be evaluated with exact string matching. We therefore use GPT5.4-mini 

(OpenAI, 2026) as an LLM judge to evaluate whether model outputs are grounded in the image, the caption, or neither. We include examples of model outputs and judged outcomes in Appendix G. 

Single-Modality Baseline To contextualize the rate of valid responses under the incon-sistent two-modality input pairs, we include a single-modality baseline in which a model receives input from only one modality and is prompted to report information from that modality. We use the same GPT model as the LLM Judge to evaluate whether the response sufficiently reports what is in its input.

# 3 Role of Symbolic vs. Distributional Signals in Word-Input Binding 

The strong performance of VLMs which have special image wrapper tokens on the target-modality retrieval task might suggest that source-modality monitoring is driven primarily by these symbolic marker tokens. This would resemble the largely symbolic binding mecha-nisms previously studied in language models, such as binding entities to their attributes (Feng & Steinhardt, 2024; Gur-Arieh et al., 2025). In this section, however, we show a more nuanced picture. First, we show that VLMs are indeed capable of solving a purely symbolic binding problem when there is no distributional signal to exploit. Second, we confirm that image and text content tokens differ markedly in their representations’ distributions, provid-ing an additional/alternative cue for modality identity. Third, we show that source-modality monitoring is a hybrid syntactic-semantic mechanism: models rely in part on distributional content cues to guide retrieval, but symbolic markers help contextualize content tokens, leading to a more robust encoding of modality identity. We focus our analysis on three best-performing models from different model families from the previous section. 

3.1 VLMs Are Capable of Symbolic Binding 

We begin by asking whether models can use symbols as pure indexing devices. To isolate this capacity, we test VLMs with a simpler symbolic binding task in which the two input sources are labeled by arbitrary labels such as Alpha and Beta or Dax and Wug . 3.2 VLMs Encode Tokens of Different Modalities in a Linearly Separable Manner 

A second potential source of distinction comes from the distributional properties of image and text token representations. Here, we ask whether these representations are already separable before any contextual processing by the language model. Therefore, we examine them at the embedding layer of the backbone language model. To do so, we sample 200 instances from a dataset and, for each instance, sample the embedding representation of one token from each modality. We then measure cosine similarity between embedding pairs within and across modalities, and also train a linear probe to distinguish image-token representations from text-token representations. To evaluate linear separability, we use stratified 3-fold cross-validation. Across all model-dataset pairs, we find that the averaged within-modality cosine similarity (0.21 ± 0.06) is substantially higher than cross-modality cosine similarity (0.02 ± 0.02). This suggests that tokens from the same modality are more similar to one another than to tokens from the other modality. Consistent with this, a linear probe can separate image and caption tokens with perfect cross-validation accuracy across all models and datasets we test, indicating that image and text representations are linearly separable to a very high degree. 4

3.3 Structural Perturbation on Symbolic Markers

3.4 Propagation of Symbolic Signals onto Content Tokens 

In this subsection, we investigate how symbolic and distributional signals interact, focusing on whether symbolic marker tokens help write modality identity information into content-token representations. Our hypothesis is that this happens through contextualization across layers: marker tokens influence the hidden states of downstream content tokens, causing modality identity to become embedded in those representations rather than remaining localized at the marker positions. To isolate this effect, we first run the model on the normal input with intact markers and collect the hidden activations at the content-token positions after they have been contextualized in the presence of those markers. We then perform a second run in which the marker tokens are removed, but the contextualized content-token activations from the clean run are patched in and frozen (Figure 11). We refer to this intervention as the freeze-remove condition (Frz-RM). Figure 6 shows that symbolic information does not remain confined to the marker positions. In the Frz-RM condition, selectivity is partially restored relative to conditions in which mark-ers are removed from the outset. This suggests that, during contextualization, information carried by the marker tokens is propagated into downstream content-token representations. Once contextualized in this way, content tokens encode not only their native distributional signals, but also the signal of modality identity inherited from the symbolic markers. The modality-specific breakdown further shows that both image and caption content ben-efit from this contextualization, though the gain is much larger for caption content. For Gemma-3-12B and Qwen2.5-VL-32B, caption-target selectivity rises from near chance to around 0.6 under Frz-RM. For InternVL3-14B, the effect is weaker, but still mitigates the negative selectivity observed when markers are removed without using the contextualized activations. These results suggest that symbolic markers become more effective not only as explicit tokens, but also by shaping the representations of downstream content tokens.

# 5 Related Work 

Cross-Modal Conflict in VLMs Prior work has used inconsistent multimodal inputs to study modality bias in VLM predictions (Deng et al., 2025; Pezeshkpour et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025). While these studies show that VLMs can favor one modality over another depending on the model, dataset, or relative unimodal confidence, they typically use questions that remain ambiguously answerable from either modality, or focus mainly on the image side of the conflict. More closely related to our setting, Hua et al. (2025) explicitly query both image and caption information under inconsistent inputs. Our work broadens the evaluation from their setup to more VLMs across scales and model families, and studies the underlying signals which enables VLMs to accomplish this task. 

Structural Perturbations on Special Tokens Shayegani et al. (2025) shows that perturbing assistant and user role tags or placing query images under the assistant turn can induce mis-aligned behaviors in vision-language models. Similarly, we study structural perturbations but on special tokens related to the modality structure: we manipulate the image start and end tokens to probe how symbolic modality markers support source-modality monitoring. 

# 6 Discussion and Conclusion 

We investigate the mechanism by which modern VLMs bind words in their input prompts (e.g., words like image or text ) to specific components of their input/context (e.g., the image or the text). In experiments spanning 11 different vision-language models (VLMs), we find evidence that this type of binding involves both syntactic and semantic signals. That is, models exploit explicit marker tokens (such as <image> & </image> ) when available, but also exploit distributional semantic cues. Indeed, we find that when the two signals are in conflict, models rely more on the distributional semantic cues than on the syntactic ones, though this varies as a function of the strength of the distributional signal. Our experiments focus specifically on VLMs, but offer insights that are likely to extend to multimodal and agentic models in general. In particular, as models become increasingly complex, multimodal, and agentic, we expect that the importance of semantic signals for binding will become increasingly pronounced. This is due to the fact that models appear to have an innate preference for distributional semantic signals (when both signals are available), and because it will become increasingly impractical to provide explicit markers as the complexity of the input space grows. Indeed, our results comparing the different in binding the words text vs. caption vs. document reveal the challenge of grounding references to “micromodalities” – segments of the input space that are different enough to humans to warrant different words, but which might be hard to distinguish to the model based on distributional signals alone. These point to an important direction for future work – to endow models with rich enough world models that they can not only predict future states, but refer back to aspects of those states and their origin via natural language. Our results also provide a valuable case study for how the abstract “binding problem” in AI becomes more complicated in realistic contexts. # A Dataset Details 

We use two image–captioning datasets: MSCOCO 2017 captions and Flickr30k. 

Flickr30k. For Flickr30k, we use the test split from the lmms-lab/flickr30k release on HuggingFace. We then randomly permute the examples using a fixed seed and subsample 4,000 examples for train, 2,000 for validation, and 2,000 for test. 

MSCOCO. For MSCOCO, we use the 2017 captioning data. We sample the training set from the official train2017 split, and the validation and test sets from the official val2017 

split. Specifically, after randomly permuting examples with a fixed seed, we sample 4,000 examples from train2017 for train, and 2,000 examples each from val2017 for validation and test. 

Preprocessing. We lightly normalize captions before use by collapsing repeated whites-pace, stripping surrounding quotation marks, and ensuring sentence-final punctuation when appropriate. These steps reduce superficial formatting variation while preserving semantic content. 

Use in our experiments. Both datasets are used to construct the image–text inputs in our source-modality monitoring task. Because each image is paired with textual descriptions, these datasets allow us to build inconsistent image–text examples, as well as image-only and text-only controls.

J.2 Qualitative Examples 

We notice that after intervention, although in some settings VLMs often report information from the non-queried modality, the overall fluency of the open-ended generation degrades. This is likely because the intervention perturbs representations that are used not only for source-modality monitoring but also for maintaining coherent generation.
