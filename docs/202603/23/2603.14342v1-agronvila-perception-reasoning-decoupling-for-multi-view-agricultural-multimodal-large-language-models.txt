Abstract. Agricultural multimodal reasoning requires robust spatial understanding across varying scales, from Ground close-ups to top-down UAV and Satellite imagery. Existing Multi-modal Large Language Mod-els (MLLMs) suffer from a significant “terrestrial-centric” bias, causing scale confusion and logic drift during complex agricultural planning. To address this, we introduce AgroOmni (288K), the first large-scale multi-view training corpus designed to capture diverse spatial topologies and scales in modern precision agriculture. Built on this dataset, we propose 

AgroNVILA , an MLLM that utilizes a novel Perception-Reasoning Decoupling (PRD) architecture. On the perception side, we incorporate a View-Conditioned Meta-Net (VCMN), which injects macroscopic spa-tial context into visual tokens, resolving scale ambiguities with minimal computational overhead. On the reasoning side, Agriculture-aware Rel-ative Policy Optimization (ARPO) leverages reinforcement learning to align the model’s decision-making with expert agricultural logic, pre-venting statistical shortcuts. Extensive experiments demonstrate that AgroNVILA outperforms state-of-the-art MLLMs, achieving significant improvements( +15.18% ) in multi-altitude agricultural reasoning, reflect-ing its robust capability for holistic agricultural spatial planning.

2 Related Work 

2.1 Multi-modal Large Language Models in Agriculture. 

Multi-modal Large Language Models (MLLMs) signify a paradigm shift in agri-cultural AI, transitioning from isolated visual recognition to complex agronomic reasoning [4, 36, 41, 50]. Early foundational datasets primarily targeted narrow perception tasks, such as pest and disease identification [15,38]. Recently, domain-specific training corpora (e.g., AgroInstruct [4], AGBASE [11], Agri-3M-VL [41]) and rigorous benchmarks (e.g., AgMMU [11], AgroBench [30], AgroMind [17]) have emerged to cultivate broader vision-language capabilities. Consequently, generic architectures (e.g., LLaVA-OneVision [16], Qwen-VL [5], NVILA [23]) have been adapted into specialized MLLMs, such as AgriGPT-VL [41] and Agri-LLaVA [36], excelling in fine-grained diagnosis and visual question answering. However, despite this expansion, existing resources remain predominantly re-stricted to ground-level perception. They lack the large-scale multi-view coverage (e.g., UAV and Satellite view) required for holistic top-down planning, resulting in fragmented, terrestrial-centric efforts rather than a cohesive, cross-scale spatial foundation. 

2.2 Reinforcement Learning for Alignment 

Model alignment has transitioned from PPO-based RLHF toward more efficient, critic-free preference optimization paradigms [2, 14, 19, 24, 26]. Early approaches like InstructGPT [25] employ PPO [28] to optimize policies against reward models; however, these incur substantial costs due to explicit value networks and complex KL tuning. DPO [26] reformulates alignment as supervised preference learning, whereas GRPO [29] further eliminates critics via group-relative advantages—a strategy adopted by recent systems such as DeepSeek-R1 [13] and Qwen3 [40]. To address domain imbalance, DRPO [9] extends GRPO with hierarchical ad-vantage scaling. However, these methods primarily target general object-level hallucinations. In agricultural Direct QA, errors often stem from dataset short-cuts (e.g., majority-class priors) rather than visual evidence, rendering standard hallucination-oriented objectives insufficient.

4 AgroNVILA 

4.1 Overall Architecture 

Existing Multi-modal Large Language Models (MLLMs) inherently suffer from a severe Ground-view bias Fig. 5(Stage 1) when transposed to agricultural domains. This bias heavily distorts spatial understanding, manifesting as Scale and Perspective Confusion when processing Unmanned Aerial Vehicle (UAV) and Satellite imagery. Conventional paradigms (e.g., AnyRes) attempt to mitigate this by aggressively cropping high-resolution images, inevitably leading to a computationally prohibitive token explosion. To circumvent this, we approach the architecture from first principles and propose an elegant, lightweight Perception-Reasoning Decoupling (PRD) framework. As illustrated in Fig. 4, the input image first traverses a frozen visual encoder (SigLIP) and a primary projector, compressing the raw pixels into a discrete visual token sequence X ∈ RN ×D . The PRD framework inherently bifurcates the subse-quent processing into two cohesive phases. The first phase, the View-Conditioned Meta-Net, is strictly localized at the perception front to collapse scale ambiguity before the tokens enter the LLM. The second phase, RL-based Alignment, oper-ates purely on the reasoning front, leveraging the purified perceptual features to rigorously align with expert agricultural logic.

5 Experiment 

5.1 Experimental Setup Model, dataset and training. We build our model upon NVILA-Lite-8B with a frozen SigLIP vision encoder and adopt the same backbone for all experiments to ensure fair comparison. We introduce the View-Conditioned Meta-Net (VCMN) AgroNVILA: Perception-Reasoning Decoupling 11 

between the projector and LLM and perform supervised fine-tuning (SFT) on AgroOmni (288K QA pairs) before reinforcement learning. The SFT stage is optimized using a cosine decay learning rate peaking at 2 × 10 −5 and a global batch size of 32 with one epoch. During the reinforcement learning stage, we apply Agricultural-aware Relative Policy Optimization (ARPO) with one epoch, a learning rate of 1 × 10 −6 for the LLM and 1 × 10 −7 for the projector (scale 0.1), gradient accumulation of 4 steps, and group size 8 for advantage computation, on 4 ×A100 GPUs. Further details are deferred to Suppl. 3. 

Evaluation. We evaluate our model on the AgroMind benchmark [17], a multi-view agricultural VQA benchmark covering Ground, UAV, and Satellite imagery with 13 tasks across four dimensions: Spatial Perception (SL, SR, BD), Object Understanding (OC, PDD, GSR), Scene Understanding (SC, CO, AS), and Scene Reasoning (VPR, AR, CTR, PL). The MLLMs evaluated on AgroMind are grouped into three categories: closed-source models, open-source MLLMs, and our variants. Following the official protocol, we report per-task accuracy (%) and take the overall score as the mean accuracy across all 13 tasks. The results on other agricultural benchmarks will be presented in Suppl. 2. 

5.2 Main Results 

Table 3 presents a comprehensive performance comparison of AgroNVILA against 16 leading open-source and proprietary MLLMs. AgroNVILA achieves a new state-of-the-art average accuracy of 62.47% on the AgroMind benchmark, establishing a definitive superiority in cross-scale agricultural reasoning. Despite their immense parameter scale, leading proprietary models such as GPT-5.2 (47.29%) and Gemini-1.5-Pro (41.06%) exhibit severe performance degra-dation on AgroMind due to inherent terrestrial bias . Pre-trained predominantly on human-centric imagery, they suffer critical scale confusion when presented with macroscopic agricultural scenes, failing to map visual tokens to correct physical dimensions. Consequently, AgroNVILA surpasses GPT-5.2 by absolute margins of +17.64% and +16.85% in rigorous geometric tasks like Boundary Detection (BD) and Area Statistics (AS) estimation, as our VCMN explicitly recalibrates the spatial metric. Furthermore, within the open-source and specialized ecosystems, standard foundational models (e.g., LLaVA-NeXT-8B at 31.53%) struggle with agronomic logic, while remote sensing experts like GeoChat (25.93%) catastroph-ically fail due to over-optimization for strictly top-down orthophotos. Especially in complex Anomaly Reasoning (AR), AgroNVILA secures a dominant 78.11% accuracy with the former(17.22%) and latter(29.80%), underscoring the PRD framework’s efficacy in bridging cross-scale spatial perception.

Building on the AgroOmni dataset, we pro-posed AgroNVILA , a novel MLLM framework based on a Perception-Reasoning Decoupling (PRD) architecture. This architecture separates the perception and reasoning processes, introducing a View-Conditioned Meta-Net (VCMN) to inject altitude-specific spatial priors, resolving scale ambiguity and improving cross-scale spatial perception with minimal computational overhead. On the reasoning side, we leveraged Agricultural-aware Relative Policy Optimization (ARPO), a reinforcement learning strategy that aligns the model’s decision-making with expert agricultural logic, preventing statistical shortcuts. Our experiments on the AgroMind benchmark demonstrate that AgroNVILA achieves a new state-of-the-art performance (62.47%), significantly outperforming the second-best model GPT-5.2 (47.29%). These results underline the effectiveness of the AgroOmni dataset and the AgroNVILA model in advancing multi-view agricultural rea-soning. 3 Reproducibility and Implementation Details 

To facilitate full reproducibility of our Perception-Reasoning Decoupling (PRD) framework, we detail the hardware environment and the stage-wise training hyperparameters. 

3.1 Hardware and Environment 

All experiments, including training and evaluation, are conducted on a computing cluster equipped with 8 × NVIDIA A100 (80GB) GPUs. Our implementation is built upon the official NVILA codebase. We extend its core architecture to incorporate the View-Conditioned Meta-Net (VCMN) and the Agronomic Reasoning Policy Optimization (ARPO) modules. 

3.2 Stage-wise Training Pipeline 

The training pipeline of our proposed AgroNVILA is executed in a rigorous two-stage progression. The comprehensive hyperparameters for both stages are summarized in Tab. 10. 

Stage 1: Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT). In this initial perception-alignment phase, we fine-tune the multimodal framework using the AgroOmni dataset. To ensure parameter efficiency while acquiring domain-specific knowl-edge, we freeze the language backbone and inject Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) modules ( r = 128 , α = 256 ) into the base LLM. The vision encoder remains strictly frozen, while the vision-language projector and the newly initialized VCMN module are fully updated. The model is trained for a single epoch with a maximum sequence length of 5120. 

Stage 2: Agronomic Reasoning Policy Optimization (ARPO). In the final reinforcement learning stage, we unfreeze the LLM backbone for full-parameter training to align its decision-making trajectory with expert logic. We follow a Domain-aware Relative Policy Optimization (DRPO) style objective: for each prompt we sample a group of G = 8 rollouts, compute group-relative AgroNVILA: Perception-Reasoning Decoupling 23 

normalized advantages with hierarchical domain and in-domain (K-means) tem-perature scaling, and optimize the clipped surrogate objective plus a KL penalty to the frozen reference policy. Our ARPO experiments use 4 GPUs, with each device generating 2 rollout per prompt ( Glocal = 2 ). The vision tower remains frozen and gradient checkpointing is enabled to fit the computation on 4 ×A100 (80GB). To prevent catastrophic forgetting of the spatial and semantic priors acquired during SFT, we use a small learning rate ( 2 × 10 −7), a KL coefficient 

β = 0 .05 , and PPO-style clipping ( ε = 0 .2). The combined reward includes a format term (weight 0.1); samples with near-uniform group rewards are skipped (threshold 0.05 ) to avoid low-signal updates.

In this subsection, we detail our data collection paradigm. Multi-scale Viewpoint Integration. Unlike previous agricultural datasets confined to a single perspective, AgroOmni spatially spans three distinct observa-tional scales (shown in Fig. 3(b)) by integrating 12 diverse open-source datasets with a proprietary parcel collection (detailed in Tab. 11), comprehensively cover-ing real-world scenarios in modern precision agriculture. This cross-scale visual instruction tuning fully activates and endows the model with superior practical capabilities in the agricultural domain: 

– Ground-view: Focuses on micro-scale crop phenotypes and pathological features. – UAV-view: Fills the meso-scale gap between the micro and macro levels. This perspective trains the model to develop strong dense object counting capabilities and local spatial topology awareness. 

– Satellite-view: Endows the model with macro-scale global planning capa-bilities. By incorporating OAM-TCD, CropHarvest, and internally collected farmland vector data, the trained model handles large-scale land coverage analysis, Climate Type Reasoning (CTR), and Boundary Detection (BD), directly serving regional-level agricultural resource scheduling. 

Scenario Diversity. Beyond multi-scale perspective coverage, AgroOmni exhibits exceptional scene generalization. Our data sources transcend isolated greenhouses or controlled experimental fields, spanning diverse global climate zones—such as tropical rainforests, temperate plains, and arid regions—and complex agricultural topographies. The dataset covers dozens of staple and cash crops (e.g., wheat, maize, oil palm, tomato) while faithfully preserving challenging environmental interferences, including variable lighting, cloud-induced occlusions, dense weed cohabitation, and irregular field boundaries. This cross-regional, cross-species, and cross-environmental richness significantly raises the complexity upper bound of multimodal reasoning and cultivates generalization in realistic agricultural settings. Furthermore, to transcend the limitations of static single-frame recognition, we introduce the Temporal Variation (TV) dimension. By integrating GWHD [10] for crop growth stage evolution and CLCD [22] for bi-temporal farmland change detection, we equip the model with dynamic monitoring and longitudinal comparative reasoning capabilities. This temporal dimension aligns with the long-cycle nature of real-world agricultural production, addressing a critical deficiency in existing training corpora. 

Analysis of Real-world Domain Imbalance. It is crucial to note that AgroOmni exhibits a pronounced native imbalance, mirroring the realistic ac-quisition costs and long-tail characteristics of agricultural data: Ground and AgroNVILA: Perception-Reasoning Decoupling 25 

Satellite modalities dominate the perspective distribution ( Fig. 3(b)), while foundational perception tasks (e.g., disease classification) significantly outweigh complex planning and temporal reasoning tasks ( Fig. 3(a)). We intentionally pre-serve this heterogeneous, "extremes-dominant" distribution rather than resorting to aggressive undersampling, as it constitutes the most authentic mapping of real-world agricultural intelligence. However, performing conventional SFT on such a skewed corpus inevitably drives models toward "perspective bias" and "statistical shortcuts"—overfitting to data-dense single perspectives while failing to internalize reasoning logic for complex long-tail scenarios. This distribution challenge serves as the core physical motivation for our AgroNVILA framework: we introduce the Perception-Reasoning Decoupling (PRD) architecture, utilizing VCMN to inject view-specific priors to resolve cross-view feature confusion, and the ARPO strategy, which employs Hierarchical Advantage Scaling to dynamically re-weight gradient signals across heterogeneous tasks. This synergy enables the model to maximize the utility of the full-scale corpus while genuinely internalizing cross-scale agronomic expert logic (see Sec. 4 and Suppl. 1). 

4.2 Data Pre-processing 

We adopt and extend the rigorous data processing pipeline established in Agro-Mind [17], implementing customized protocols to handle the heterogeneity of our data sources: 

– Manual Screening and Deduplication: A manual screening phase is conducted on augmented images to eliminate defective samples exhibiting artifacts, such as excessive content overlap or extreme flipping, which can otherwise distort semantic information and introduce noise. 

– Format Conversion: TIFF-format remote sensing imagery undergoes content-aware processing: colorful visual-spectrum images are converted to PNG/JPG formats to ensure model compatibility and prevent parsing failures. Conversely, images dominated by non-visible spectral bands are transformed into concatenated grayscale blocks, thereby preserving original spectral information within a grayscale representation. 

– Multi-Scale Spatial Cropping: For high-resolution geospatial imagery, we apply randomized cropping based on parcel boundaries. This strategy generates multi-scale samples that simulate diverse spatial query resolutions, enhancing the model’s robustness to scale variations. 

– Hierarchical Annotation Extraction: We leverage original labels and masks to derive instance-level statistical metrics and bounding boxes, creating a standardized dataset featuring hierarchical annotations across three levels: pixel-level (segmentation masks), instance-level (object detection boxes), and parcel-level (agricultural boundaries). To maintain data fidelity, original image resolutions are preserved ranging from 300 ×300 to 4,500 ×4,500 pixels. 

– Strict Benchmark Decontamination: To ensure rigorous and unbiased evaluation, we enforce a strict physical separation between our instruction-tuning corpus (AgroOmni) and the evaluation benchmark (AgroMind [17]). 26 J. Zhang et al.

4.3 Question Generation 

We propose a Dual-Track QA Generation method to transform multi-level an-notations into high-fidelity instruction-tuning data. Leveraging the expert-level information embedded in the raw data, this method employs two complementary strategies—“Rule-based Generation” and “Human-based Logic Synthesis”—to automatically generate QA pairs covering the full cognitive spectrum from basic perception to complex reasoning. 

Rule-based Generation. For tasks demanding rigorous numerical and po-sitional accuracy, such as Spatial Perception and Object Understanding tasks, we employ a deterministic template filling strategy. Utilizing JSON metadata or segmentation masks extracted during pre-processing (e.g., precise polygon coordinates, CRS projections, and geolocation), we construct a library of param-eterized templates. For instance, boundary detection questions are generated by directly formatting normalized coordinates, while climate zone reasoning is derived by mapping geographical coordinates to climate labels. This paradigm relies entirely on ground truth, fundamentally eliminating the numerical and spatial hallucinations often exhibited by LLMs. 

Human-based Logic Synthesis. For tasks involving complex agronomic decision-making, such as Scene Understanding and Scene Reasoning tasks, we translate raw annotations into dynamic reasoning processes that simulate hu-man experts, guiding the model through pre-defined logical chains. We adopt a “reverse-engineering” approach from evidence to conclusion: starting with ground-truth annotations (e.g., “sparse and small weed clusters”), we apply agronomic principles to deduce valid conclusions (e.g., “early-stage infestation requiring preventive weeding”) and formulate corresponding decision-making questions. We require the model to replicate this observational path by first identifying specific visual evidence, then deriving the answer through logical deduction. This strategy balances semantic richness with factual accuracy while mitigating the hallucination risks associated with open-ended generation.

2018 AI Challenge Dataset. At first we refine the raw leaf imagery by discarding augmented artifacts, such as rotated, flipped, or redundant files flagged by filename metadata. We then map the original categorical labels—encompassing species, disease types, and severity levels—onto spatial disease distributions to instantiate diverse QA templates. IP102 Dataset. We restructure the VOC-formatted annotations of this large-scale pest collection to develop a fine-grained recognition and reasoning corpus. This comparative learning strategy encourages the model to focus on subtle morphological distinctions rather than surface-level appearance. Beyond classification, we integrate pest count labels into the instruction-tuning process to train the model’s quantitative reasoning abilities. By linking specific pest identities with their associated host crops, we synthesize conversational pairs that not only evaluate semantic recognition but also enhance the model’s foundational knowledge regarding pest-crop ecological relationships. 

Agriculture Vision Challenge. OilPalmUAV Dataset. Initially, we process the original bounding-box annotations by randomly scaling them by a factor of 1.1–1.5 using the PIL ImageDraw module. CropHarvest Dataset. To process the multi-spectral spatiotemporal se-quences, we convert the raw GeoTIFF data into normalized band mosaics. This AgroNVILA: Perception-Reasoning Decoupling 29

GWHD Dataset. We unify the dataset formats and map each image to its corresponding metadata, including geographical information and wheat growth stages. CLCD Dataset. To address the necessity of dynamic agricultural monitoring, we utilize the provided bi-temporal semantic masks to construct high-order temporal reasoning benchmarks. PhenoBench Dataset. In this dataset, we process the semantic segmen-tation masks to explicitly distinguish between intact and partially visible crop or weed instances. By aggregating instance-level annotations, we derive precise counts for individual plants and leaves. This structured con-version enables the model to learn complex spatial and occlusion-aware concepts essential for precision agricultural monitoring.

EarthVQA Dataset. To ground the model’s spatial cognition in complex agricultural environments, we leverage the eight provided semantic category labels. OAM-TCD Dataset. In this dataset, we curate a training subset converting raw GeoTIFF tree-cover maps into RGB-compressed JPEGs to optimize visual fidelity for multimodal encoders. These spatial statistics enable advanced queries concerning global tree-cover patterns, while the random sampling of multi-grid cell pairs allows for the construction of multi-image comparison tasks that train the model to reason across heterogeneous landscape layouts.
